# STORY 1: UK BANS SOCIAL MEDIA FOR UNDER-16s — LANDMARK GLOBAL SHIFT
**Domains:** Social Media Platforms | Media Laws & Policy | Education & Higher Education | Global Communication Trends

---

## PART A — NEWS SUMMARY

On 15 June 2026, the UK Government announced a landmark ban on social media platforms offering services to children under 16. Using the same model as Australia's December 2025 ban, the UK will prohibit platforms including Snapchat, TikTok, YouTube, Instagram, Facebook and X from serving under-16s. Messaging services like WhatsApp and Signal are exempted. The government will also implement world-leading blocks on harmful functions such as livestreaming and stranger communication with minors, extending these to gaming platforms. Overnight curfews and breaks in infinite scrolling for under-18s are under consideration. The move follows a national consultation with 116,000 responses — 90% of parents and two-thirds of young people supported tougher action. Canada simultaneously introduced a similar bill (Bill C-??) targeting under-16s, with penalties of 3% of global revenue or CAD 10 million, and a new Digital Safety Commission of Canada. Australia's ban, passed in December 2025, was the world's first.

## PART B — COMMUNICATION THEORY MAPPING

| Theory | Application |
|--------|-------------|
| **Uses & Gratifications** | Children use social media for entertainment, identity formation, peer connection. Ban disrupts these gratifications, forcing alternative channels. |
| **Cultivation Theory** (Gerbner) | Heavy social media use cultivates distorted perceptions of reality, body image, danger. Ban aims to reduce cultivation effects on developing minds. |
| **Agenda-Setting Theory** (McCombs & Shaw) | UK government's announcement sets the agenda globally — other nations now forced to respond. Media coverage amplifies the policy priority. |
| **Third-Person Effect** (Davison) | Parents and policymakers believe *other people's children* are more vulnerable to social media harm, justifying regulation. |
| **Spiral of Silence** (Noelle-Neumann) | Children who abstain from social media may fear isolation from peers. Ban creates a structural solution to this pressure. |
| **Media Literacy / Media Education** (Masterman, Buckingham) | Ban complements media literacy education; both needed for sustainable digital citizenship. |
| **Protectionist vs Empowerment Paradigm** | This ban represents pure protectionism. Critics argue it disempowers youth from learning to navigate digital spaces. |
| **Technological Determinism** (McLuhan) | Policy assumes technology *determines* outcomes (harm); regulation can shape technological effects. |
| **Social Construction of Technology** (Pinch & Bijker) | Counter-view: how technology is used depends on social context, not just the platform itself. |
| **Public Sphere Theory** (Habermas) | Social media as a digital public sphere; excluding youth raises questions about their democratic participation rights. |

## PART E — JRF ANALYTICAL QUESTIONS

### 10-Mark Question
**Q. Discuss the role of the "Third-Person Effect" in shaping public support for social media regulation for minors.**

*Model Answer Points:*
- Define Third-Person Effect (Davison, 1983): individuals believe media affects *others* more than themselves
- Parents perceive their own children as vulnerable → support regulation
- Policymakers use perceived vulnerability to justify restrictions
- Perceptual component: "others at risk" → behavioral component: support for censorship
- Criticisms: overestimates media effects, underestimates youth agency
- Connection to protectionist vs empowerment paradigms in media policy

### 15-Mark Question
**Q. Analyze the UK's under-16 social media ban using Habermas's theory of the Public Sphere. Does the ban strengthen or weaken democratic participation?**

*Model Answer Points:*
- Habermas's Public Sphere: a space for rational-critical debate, accessible to all citizens
- Social media as a digital public sphere — youth participate in civic discourse
- **Ban weakens Public Sphere**: excludes 16M+ young voices from digital discourse; youth lose platform for political expression, activism, identity formation
- **Counter-argument**: the current commercialized social media is a *refeudalized* public sphere (Habermas) — dominated by algorithmic manipulation, surveillance capitalism, not rational debate
- Ban may *strengthen* alternative public spheres (offline, school, community)
- Net result: policy trades democratic participation for developmental protection
- Need for "youth digital public sphere" — safe, regulated, participatory spaces

### 20-Mark Question
**Q. Critically evaluate the global trend toward banning social media for minors. Compare the approaches of Australia, the UK, and Canada, and discuss the implications for media policy harmonization in an era of platform capitalism.**

*Model Answer Points:*
- **Comparative framework**:
  - Australia: first mover, blanket ban (Dec 2025), served as model
  - UK: ban + functionality restrictions (livestreaming, stranger comms) + curfew consideration; exemption for messaging apps
  - Canada: ban + Digital Safety Commission (new regulator) + 7 categories of harmful content + AI chatbot safety
- **Common features**: age verification, platform responsibility, enforcement mechanisms
- **Differences**: regulatory body design, scope of exemptions, penalty structures
- **Platform Capitalism** (Srnicek): platforms extract data, maximize engagement; youth are vulnerable commodity
- **Policy harmonization challenges**:
  - Different legal traditions (common law vs civil law)
  - Free speech protections (First Amendment in US blocks similar ban)
  - Technical feasibility of age verification (privacy concerns)
  - Enforcement across borders (extra-territorial reach)
- **Theoretical framework**: Protectionist paradigm vs Children's Rights (UNCRC Article 12 — right to be heard; Article 13 — freedom of expression)
- **Critical perspectives**:
  - Ban may drive youth to unregulated, darker corners of internet
  - Ignores digital divide — regulated access better than no access
  - Overstates media effects, understates resilience
- **Way forward**: co-regulatory models, media literacy integration, age-appropriate design codes (UK Age-Appropriate Design Code / "Children's Code" as precursor)

### 10-Mark Question
**Q. Explain Johan Galtung's concept of Peace Journalism. How does it apply to media coverage of the US-Iran peace deal?**

*Model Answer Points:*
- Galtung introduced Peace Journalism vs War Journalism framework
- Peace Journalism: focuses on conflict resolution, humanizes all sides, highlights peace initiatives, explores root causes
- War Journalism: focuses on violence, "us vs them", elite sources, zero-sum outcomes
- Application: media covering the deal could focus on the *negotiation process* (peace journalism) or on *who won/lost* (war journalism)
- Pakistani media: peace journalism emphasis (mediation, diplomacy, hope)
- US/Israeli media: mixed — some framed as Trump victory (war journalism)
- Iranian media: framed as resistance validated (also war journalism framing)

### 15-Mark Question
**Q. Analyze the role of social media platforms in modern diplomatic communication, using the US-Iran peace deal announcement as a case study.**

*Model Answer Points:*
- **Digital diplomacy** (Bjola & Holmes): diplomats use social media for real-time communication
- PM Shehbaz announced on X → immediate global dissemination
- Trump confirmed via media → multi-platform amplification
- **Advantages**: speed, direct-to-public, bypasses traditional gatekeepers
- **Disadvantages**: misinformation risk, lack of nuance, 280-character diplomacy
- **Crisis communication**: social media enables rapid disinformation during conflict

### 20-Mark Question
**Q. Critically analyze the "Propaganda Model" (Herman & Chomsky) in the context of media coverage of the US-Iran conflict and peace deal across different national media systems.**

*Model Answer Points:*
- **Five filters** of the propaganda model:
  1. Ownership — US media owned by conglomerates (Murdoch, Bezos) vs Iranian state-controlled media
  2. Advertising — commercial pressures shape US coverage
  3. Sourcing — US media relies on government/military sources
  4. Flak — criticism of US policy is punished
  5. Anti-communism / "anti-terror" — Iran framed as enemy
- **US coverage**: filtered through national security lens; Iranian aggression highlighted; US strikes de-emphasized
- **Iranian coverage**: state-controlled; anti-US/Israel narrative; resistance as heroic
- **Pakistani media**: less filtered by filters 1-4; more independent; focused on mediation success
- **Global South perspective**: Herman & Chomsky's model is US-centric; doesn't fully explain non-Western media
- **Critique**: model is too deterministic; ignores agency of journalists; social media provides alternative narratives
- **Digital age update**: algorithmic filtering functions as a new filter (6th filter?)

### 10-Mark
**Q. Explain the concept of "emergent risk" in the context of multi-agent AI systems. Why does DeepMind believe simulation is the only way to study these risks?**

*Model Answer:*
- Emergent risk = risks that arise from interaction of multiple agents, not from any single agent
- Examples: coordinated cyberattacks, market manipulation, information cascades
- DeepMind argues that emergent behavior cannot be predicted analytically — must be observed
- Simulation (sandbox environments) allows controlled observation of agent interactions
- Ethical advantage: no real-world harm during study
