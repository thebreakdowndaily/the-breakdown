## Cold Open

On June 24, 2026, US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer departed New Delhi after two days of intensive talks with Commerce Minister Piyush Goyal and Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman. The joint statement described "substantial progress" toward an interim bilateral trade pact. With the July 24 deadline for Section 122 tariff expiry just 29 days away, India and the US are racing to seal an agreement that has survived a landmark Supreme Court ruling, two distinct tariff regimes, and 18 months of negotiations.

Both sides say they are "very, very close," but the remaining one per cent of unresolved issues contains the hardest political and economic trade-offs. The deal would grant India a competitive tariff advantage over Vietnam, ASEAN, and China — a critical objective for Indian exporters whose trade surplus with the US has shrunk from roughly $40.89 billion to $34.4 billion over the past year.

## The Deal Structure

Negotiations for the Bilateral Trade Agreement were formally launched on February 13, 2025, when Modi and Trump committed to a comprehensive trade framework. The process has been anything but straightforward. A joint statement on February 7, 2026, established the framework for an interim agreement, with the US agreeing to reduce tariffs on Indian goods to 18 per cent.

On February 20, however, the US Supreme Court struck down Trump's use of the International Emergency Economic Powers Act to impose reciprocal tariffs, creating legal chaos. On February 24, the US imposed a temporary 10 per cent tariff under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, applicable to all trading partners for 150 days. The current negotiations aim to replace this temporary arrangement before the Section 122 authority expires on July 24, after which the USTR is expected to release Section 301 investigation findings into India's digital trade practices.

## Tariff Reductions

The proposed interim agreement would give India a competitive edge over rival exporting nations. India's exports to the US currently stand at $87.3 billion, up marginally by 0.92 per cent, while imports from the US have grown by nearly 16 per cent to $52.9 billion, driven largely by energy purchases. Bilateral trade overall reached $149 billion in 2025.

India has proposed approximately $500 billion in purchases of US energy resources, aircraft, and technology over five years — designed to address the Trump administration's focus on trade imbalances while advancing India's own infrastructure and defence modernisation goals. However, India has maintained dairy products as a non-negotiable exclusion from tariff reduction commitments, citing the livelihood concerns of millions of small dairy farmers.

## Defence and Tech Cooperation

Beyond tariff schedules, the interim deal includes significant defence and technology components. The agreement builds on the February 2026 joint statement committing both nations to enhanced collaboration in semiconductors, quantum computing, and artificial intelligence. Defence procurement and co-production arrangements are likely to be formalised alongside the trade pact.

India has sought assurances that Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974 cannot be used to re-impose tariffs after the interim agreement is signed — a demand addressing India's longstanding concern about the unpredictability of unilateral US trade actions.

## Strategic Implications

A successful interim trade deal would provide India a competitive edge in the US market that could help propel bilateral trade toward the Mission 500 target of $500 billion by 2030. For the US, the deal would demonstrate that the Trump administration can achieve bilateral trade outcomes through negotiation rather than unilateral tariff action, and would strengthen the strategic partnership with India as a counterweight to Chinese influence in the Indo-Pacific.

If no deal is reached by July 24, India would face the re-imposition of 12.5 per cent Section 301 tariffs without any of the competitive advantages the interim agreement would provide. Experts currently estimate only a ten per cent probability of missing the deadline, but the hardest issues — agriculture market access, medical devices, ICT goods, and dairy — remain unresolved. The next 29 days will determine whether the world's largest and fifth-largest economies can turn proximity into a deal.
