India-US Trade Deal Nears Finish Line — July 24 Tariff Deadline Puts Mission 500 at Stake

The Breakdown · June 25, 2026 · 10 min read

On June 24, US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer left New Delhi after two days of meetings with Commerce Minister Piyush Goyal and Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman. The joint statement that followed used a carefully chosen phrase: "substantial progress." Not a deal — but the closest the two sides have come in 18 months of on-and-off negotiations. With a July 24 deadline now 29 days away, the question is no longer whether India and the US want a trade agreement. It is whether they can get one done in time.

The State of Play

India and the United States have been negotiating a Bilateral Trade Agreement since February 2025, when President Donald Trump and Prime Minister Narendra Modi launched the process. A framework for an interim deal was signed on February 7, 2026, under which the US agreed to reduce tariffs on Indian goods from 50% to 18%. But thirteen days later, the US Supreme Court struck down Trump's reciprocal tariffs under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act, sending the negotiations back to zero.

The Trump administration pivoted to Section 122 of the Trade Act, imposing a temporary 10% tariff on all trading partners for 150 days from February 24. That window expires on July 24 — and the administration is already preparing a new tariff architecture under Section 301. India is among 16 countries facing a second Section 301 investigation, with findings expected before the July 24 deadline.

The consequence is stark: if no deal is reached by July 24, India faces a proposed 12.5% tariff under Section 301 with no competitive advantage over rivals like Vietnam, Bangladesh, and ASEAN countries.

What India Wants

India's objective is straightforward: a tariff advantage over competing exporting nations. Under the February framework, India was to face an 18% tariff while competitors like Vietnam would face 19-20%. That differential is the entire point of the deal for New Delhi.

"We cannot implement the FTA until we secure a competitive advantage," Goyal told reporters on June 20. "The issue currently pending is that our duties need to be lower compared to those of competing nations; once this is settled, the trade agreement will be implemented."

India has drawn red lines. Dairy is non-negotiable — "a very sensitive sector," Goyal said. The country has signed multiple trade agreements but has never opened dairy. On Section 301, India wants assurances that the deal would prevent the US from opening new Section 301 investigations or re-imposing tariffs after signing.

What the US Wants

Washington's goal is "Mission 500" — $500 billion in bilateral trade by 2030, up from $149 billion in 2025. The US wants deeper market access for American goods, particularly:

  • Agricultural products: dried distillers' grains, red sorghum, tree nuts, fresh and processed fruit, soybean oil, wine and spirits
  • Medical devices: India to eliminate restrictive import licensing
  • ICT goods: India to eliminate licensing that delays market access
  • Strategic sectors: critical minerals, defense, technology, energy

India has proposed purchasing $500 billion in US energy products, aircraft and parts, precious metals, technology equipment, and coking coal over five years — a massive procurement that would dramatically rebalance bilateral trade.

The July 24 Cliff

India's tariff rate has been through four regimes in 18 months. The July 24 decision point will determine whether India secures a competitive advantage or faces a new tariff architecture.

India's export growth to the US has stagnated — just 0.92% — while imports surged 16%, narrowing the trade surplus by nearly $6.5 billion. Gross FDI hit a record $94.53 billion, but net FDI was a mere $7.65 billion as foreign investors repatriated profits, invested in debt, or routed funds through shell entities. The economic relationship is growing, but it is also tilting.

The Section 301 Shadow

The Indian side is concerned that the US could use Section 301 to re-impose tariffs even after a deal is signed. Trade expert Abhijit Mukhopadhyay of CRF told ET Now: "No country, including India, will want a deal that has the provision to revert back to the status quo. Since the July 24 deadline is hanging over our heads, there will be more concern on the Indian side rather than the American side."

He puts the odds of missing the deadline at about 10%, warning that failure "would revert talks to MFN tariffs and potentially stall market access discussions."

Three Section 301 investigations have been opened against Vietnam. China remains a key target. India wants written assurances that it will not face the same treatment if the deal is signed — a demand that has become a sticking point in the final stretch.

The Strategic Context

The trade negotiations are happening against a wider strategic realignment. Modi and Trump met at the G7 in France on June 17 — two weeks before Greer's visit — and the political signal was clear: the US-India relationship is too important to let tariff disagreements define it. Foreign Secretary Vikram Misri confirmed the interim pact had seen "significant progress," and US Ambassador Sergio Gor said only "1%" of the deal remained unresolved.

But that 1% contains the hardest issues: the exact tariff differential India will enjoy over competitors; the scope of Section 301 protections; market access for American agriculture; and the treatment of digital trade and data flows.

The US has already sealed trade deals with nearly every other major trading partner. India is among the last holdouts — and both sides know that the terms of this agreement will set the template for the broader BTA that is meant to follow.

The Bottom Line

THE VERDICT

India and the United States are in the final stretch of a trade negotiation that has already survived a Supreme Court ruling, two tariff regimes, and 18 months of geopolitical turbulence. The July 24 deadline is real: if the interim deal is not signed by then, India faces a 12.5% tariff under Section 301 with no advantage over competitors. If it is signed, India secures a tariff differential that makes its exporters more competitive in the $149 billion bilateral trade relationship. Both sides say they are "very, very close." The remaining 1% contains the hardest issues — tariff arithmetic, Section 301 protections, agricultural access. The next 29 days will determine whether the world's largest and fifth-largest economies can turn proximity into a deal.
