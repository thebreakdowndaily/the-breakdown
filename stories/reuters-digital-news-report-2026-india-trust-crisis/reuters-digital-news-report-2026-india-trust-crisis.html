Trust in Indian News Collapses to 39%

Audiences are not simply losing faith in journalism—they are abandoning traditional news sources entirely, migrating to platforms where trust is structurally lower, and in many cases, switching off from the news altogether.

For a decade, the Reuters Institute's Digital News Report has tracked a slow but steady erosion of trust in journalism across the world. In 2026, that erosion became something far more alarming: a wholesale collapse. Trust in news globally has fallen to 37% — the lowest level since the survey began in 2015, down 3 percentage points in a single year. In India, the situation is even more stark. Trust in news now stands at just 39%, a precipitous 4-point drop from 43% in 2025, placing the country 18th among the 48 markets surveyed.

Those headline numbers alone would be cause for concern. But the 2026 report, released this month by the Reuters Institute for the Study of Journalism at the University of Oxford, reveals that the trust crisis is merely a symptom of deeper structural shifts. Audiences are not simply losing faith in journalism — they are abandoning traditional news sources entirely, migrating to platforms where trust is structurally lower, and in many cases, switching off from the news altogether. The India chapter, authored by Anjana Krishnan of the Asian College of Journalism, draws on a survey of 2,000 Indian respondents to paint a detailed picture of a media ecosystem at an inflection point.

Bottom Line

Trust in Indian news fell to 39% in 2026, down 4 points year-on-year and in line with a global decline to a decade-low 37%. A staggering 58% of Indians now rely on YouTube for news, 56% use WhatsApp, 52% actively avoid news, and 22% use AI chatbots for news — double the global average. The Reuters Institute Digital News Report 2026 reveals a media ecosystem where audiences have migrated to platforms, trust has collapsed, and traditional newsrooms are struggling to keep pace with a transformation that shows no signs of slowing.

These are not isolated data points. They are interconnected indicators of a system-wide transformation. The platform migration drives the trust decline. The trust decline fuels news avoidance. Each trend amplifies the next, creating a cycle that traditional newsroom strategies — more reporting, better journalism, paywalls — have so far been unable to break.

02. The Platform Migration: YouTube First, Everything Else Second

If there is a single finding in the 2026 report that explains most of the others, it is this: social media and video networks have overtaken news organisations' own websites and apps as the most widely used means of accessing news — and this has happened for the first time since the Reuters Institute began tracking the metric. The shift is not incremental. It is structural. And in India, it has happened faster and more completely than almost anywhere else.

58% of Indian respondents now rely on YouTube for news. That makes the Google-owned video platform the single most important news source in the country — more than television, more than print, more than any individual news website. WhatsApp follows at 56%, a figure that has climbed 10 percentage points since 2021, driven by the proliferation of family and community groups that serve as de facto news distribution networks. Together, YouTube and WhatsApp have created a parallel news ecosystem that operates largely outside the control of traditional newsrooms.

The report notes that this is not a phenomenon confined to younger demographics. Older audiences, who once formed the loyal base of print and broadcast news, are migrating toward the same consumption habits. The gap between age groups is narrowing, not because younger audiences are coming to traditional media, but because older audiences are leaving it. Legacy news organisations are losing their most reliable readership, and they are not gaining a new one.

"Social media and video networks have overtaken news organisations' own websites and apps as the most widely used means of accessing news for the first time since we began tracking this metric. This structural shift has profound implications for trust, revenue, and the relationship between journalists and audiences."

— Jim Egan, Senior Research Associate, Reuters Institute and lead author of the report

For Indian newsrooms, the platform migration presents a brutal arithmetic problem. Advertising and subscription revenue follow audiences. As audiences move to platforms, revenue moves with them — but it does not flow back to the news organisations that created the content. YouTube, Facebook, and WhatsApp capture the monetisation value of news distribution while bearing none of the costs of news production. The result is a market where the economics of journalism become increasingly unsustainable even as the volume of news consumption rises.

03. Trust: The Collapse

India's trust decline from 43% in 2025 to 39% in 2026 mirrors a global drop of 3 percentage points to 37% — the lowest point since 2015 and a figure that the report's authors describe as "concerning across the board." Twenty-nine of the 48 markets surveyed recorded statistically significant trust declines. The United States, wrestling with its own polarised media environment, sits at just 25%. At the other end of the spectrum, Finland remains the highest-trust market surveyed, with 69%, demonstrating that high trust in news is achievable — but requires sustained investment in media literacy and independent journalism.

The report identifies what it calls a "trust gap" — a structural explanation for why trust is declining faster than any single measure of journalistic quality might suggest. Trust in news consumed via social media stands at just 22%. Trust in news received through AI chatbots is even lower at 20%. Compare that to trust in news overall at 37%, and the picture becomes clear: as audiences consume more news through platforms and less through owned media properties, the composition effect drives the headline trust number downward. This is not simply a crisis of journalism — it is a crisis of distribution. Audiences do not inherently distrust journalism. They distrust the channels through which they increasingly encounter it.

Sannuta Raghu, deputy editor of Scroll.in, told the Reuters Institute that the trust problem is compounded by the sheer volume of unverified information circulating on platforms. "The challenge is not just that people are getting news from WhatsApp and YouTube — it is that they are getting news from WhatsApp and YouTube instead of from news organisations, and the two sources are presented without any distinction in quality or reliability," she said.

In India, trust in legacy print publishers and public broadcasters remains relatively high at the brand level. The country's most trusted news brands — The Hindu, The Indian Express, and the public broadcaster Doordarshan — continue to score well on trust metrics when measured in isolation. The problem is that fewer people are encountering these brands directly. They are encountering news through YouTube creators, WhatsApp forwards, and AI-generated summaries — channels where the originating brand is invisible and trust is structurally lower.

Pradeep Gairola, digital editor of The Hindu, acknowledged the challenge to the Reuters Institute: "We know our journalism is trusted. The question is how to make that trusted journalism visible in an ecosystem where the audience does not come to us anymore — we have to go to them, and going to them means operating on platforms where our brand signal is weaker."

04. News Avoidance: Half the Country Tuning Out

Perhaps the most worrying statistic in the report for the commercial prospects of the news industry is this: 52% of Indian respondents say they sometimes or often avoid the news. This places India among the highest rates of news avoidance in the entire 48-market survey, alongside countries like Brazil and the United Kingdom that have seen sharp rises in avoidance since 2020.

The reasons cited by Indian respondents follow a pattern familiar from other markets. The most common explanations include the negative effect of news on mood and mental well-being, a sense of political fatigue driven by the relentlessness of the election cycle, and a growing perception that news coverage is repetitive, conflict-driven, and unhelpful in addressing the problems it describes. In a country that has experienced a series of high-stakes state and national elections, the non-stop news cycle appears to have exhausted a significant portion of the audience.

News avoidance is not a new phenomenon, and the Reuters Institute has tracked it since 2017. But its scale in 2026 suggests a structural shift that traditional remedies — better headlines, positive news sections, digital subscriptions — have not addressed. Audiences are not merely choosing different sources. They are choosing to disengage entirely. For a news organisation whose business model depends on attention, an audience that has opted out of news consumption altogether is far harder to reach than one that simply switched from print to digital.

The report also finds that news avoidance is higher among women than men in India, and higher among younger respondents than older ones — suggesting that the problem will intensify as the current cohort of light news consumers ages into the demographic that has traditionally been the most loyal news audience.

Dhanya Rajendran, co-founder of The News Minute, offered a blunt assessment to the Reuters Institute: "We are in a situation where we are producing more news than ever before, and a significant percentage of the population is telling us they do not want it. The industry's response has largely been to produce more of the same and hope the audience comes back. The data suggests that is not working."

05. The AI Chatbot Factor

22% of Indian respondents now use AI chatbots for news — more than double the global average of 10%, and among the highest rates across all 48 markets surveyed. Usage is particularly concentrated among audiences under 35, with nearly a third of respondents in the 18–24 age group reporting that they use tools like ChatGPT, Gemini, or Perplexity to access news content. This figure has more than doubled since the 2025 report, making AI chatbots the fastest-growing news source in the survey.

The implications are profound. AI chatbots do not produce original journalism. They aggregate, summarise, and repackage existing news content — often without clear attribution to the original sources. When a user asks a chatbot for the latest news on a political development, the response is a synthesis drawn from multiple sources, stripped of branding and presented as a single authoritative answer. For news organisations, this creates a visibility problem that is even more acute than the platform migration: at least on YouTube and WhatsApp, the originating news brand can be identified. In an AI-generated summary, it often cannot.

The report documents how Indian newsrooms are responding to the AI challenge. The adoption of AI tools is, in the report's words, "uneven." Some outlets like Odisha TV and Aaj Tak have experimented with AI-generated news anchors and AI clones of popular on-air personalities — a move that has drawn both interest and criticism. Scroll.in developed an internal tool that extracts visual media from text articles and automatically generates short-form videos optimised for social platforms. The Hindu experimented with an AI character during the recent Assembly elections to deliver real-time election results in a conversational format.

But the report cautions that broader adoption is held back by "a lack of resources, knowledge, or genuine concerns around the dilution of editorial safeguards and trust in news." The trust gap is particularly relevant here: if audiences already distrust AI chatbots (only 20% trust them for news), newsrooms that rush into AI deployment risk further eroding the credibility they are trying to preserve. The report suggests that the most successful AI strategies will be those that maintain editorial oversight and transparency about how AI is used — a standard that few Indian newsrooms have fully implemented.

06. The Rise of Content Creators

About 27% of respondents globally get news through individual content creators — personalities on YouTube, Instagram, and TikTok who offer commentary, analysis, and sometimes original reporting on current events. In India, the figure is higher than the global average, reflecting the country's deep YouTube penetration and the rise of a thriving ecosystem of independent news commentators.

The report finds an important nuance in how audiences perceive creators versus traditional media. Creators are rated lower than established news organisations on trust and impartiality — consistent with the overall trust findings. But they are rated significantly higher on entertainment value, accessibility, and the sense that they represent the audience's perspective rather than an institutional one. Audiences do not see creators as replacements for traditional journalism. They see them as complements — sources that explain, contextualise, and humanise the news in ways that institutional reporting often does not.

This finding challenges the narrative that traditional newsrooms are failing because audiences no longer care about quality journalism. The report suggests the opposite: audiences still value quality, but they also value accessibility, authenticity, and format innovation. Traditional newsrooms have lost market share not because they produce journalism that is too good, but because they have been slow to adapt to how audiences want to consume journalism. The most successful news organisations of the next decade, the report implies, will be those that learn to operate in both worlds — producing rigorous, trusted journalism while distributing it through the formats and channels that audiences actually use.

"Creators are not the enemy of journalism," Jim Egan told The Breakdown. "The data shows that people who follow creators for news are often the same people who follow traditional news brands. The audience is not choosing one over the other — they are layering them. The question for news organisations is whether they are willing to adapt their formats to meet audiences where they already are."

07. The Regulatory Response

In parallel with these market-driven shifts, the Indian government has moved to assert greater control over the digital news ecosystem. The Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology's proposed IT Rules Second Amendment 2026, released for public consultation in March, represents the most significant regulatory intervention in India's online news space since the original IT Rules 2021.

The amendment proposes three key changes that directly affect the news ecosystem. First, it brings social media accounts that regularly share news and current affairs content under the same Digital Media Ethics Code that applies to mainstream news publishers — a move that would subject individual creators and unofficial news aggregators to the same three-tier grievance redressal mechanism that governs traditional media. Second, it makes government "advisories" to social media platforms legally binding, giving the executive branch greater authority to demand content removal without judicial oversight. Third, it introduces mandatory labelling requirements for synthetically generated or AI-produced information — a provision that responds directly to the rise of AI chatbots and deepfake content documented in the Reuters report.

The government's stated rationale is that these measures are necessary to curb the spread of misinformation and to ensure accountability among the growing number of "shadow news" accounts that operate outside the regulatory framework that governs traditional journalism. In a media environment where 58% of the population gets news from YouTube and 56% from WhatsApp — platforms where the line between news and opinion, fact and rumour, is often invisible — the argument for some form of regulatory oversight is not without merit.

Critics, however, argue that the amendments expand executive power over digital speech without adequate procedural safeguards. The provision making government advisories legally binding has drawn particular concern from free-expression advocates, who note that it allows content to be removed without a judicial order or a hearing. The inclusion of individual social media accounts under the Ethics Code has also been questioned, with critics arguing that it could be used to target independent journalists and political commentators who lack the institutional backing of established news organisations.

The tension between these two positions — the need to combat misinformation versus the risk of government overreach — is not unique to India. But it is especially acute in a country where trust in news is already at 39%, press freedom ranks 157th out of 180 countries in the RSF World Press Freedom Index (down six places from 2025), and the government is simultaneously a regulator of the news industry and a major subject of its coverage. The IT Rules Second Amendment arrives at a moment when the news ecosystem is already under immense structural pressure. Whether regulation can improve trust — or whether it will further undermine confidence in the independence of journalism — is an open question.

08. What This Means for Journalism

The 2026 Digital News Report is not a diagnosis of a problem that can be fixed with better headlines or a social media strategy. It is a description of a systemic transformation that has fundamentally altered the relationship between journalism and the public. The implications for Indian newsrooms — and for the journalists who work in them — are far-reaching.

Key Implications

Platform dependency is a revenue crisis: As audiences shift from owned properties to platforms, advertising and subscription revenue follow. Indian newsrooms are competing with YouTube creators and AI chatbots for the same audience, often on terms that favour the platforms. The business model of journalism — built on the assumption that quality content will attract paying audiences — needs fundamental rethinking.

Trust is a distribution problem as much as a journalism problem: Audiences trust individual news brands reasonably well. But they encounter news through channels — social media, messaging apps, AI summaries — where the originating brand is invisible and trust is inherently lower. Fixing trust requires not just better journalism but better distribution strategies that bring trusted brands into platform environments.

News avoidance is existential: If 52% of potential readers are actively avoiding news, the traditional industry response — produce more and better journalism and audiences will return — is insufficient. The industry must understand why audiences are tuning out and address the format, tone, and relevance of its coverage.

AI is both an existential threat and a genuine opportunity: India's high AI chatbot usage demonstrates audience openness to AI-assisted news consumption. But the trust gap (20% trust in AI-sourced news versus 37% overall) means that careless AI deployment risks further eroding credibility. The winning newsrooms will be those that find ways to use AI transparently and editorially.

Content creators are not the enemy: The report's data suggests that creators and traditional media serve different needs and can coexist. Audiences want both rigorous reporting and accessible, entertaining formats. The most successful news organisations will be those that find ways to adapt to platform-native formats without sacrificing editorial standards.

Regulation is a double-edged sword: The IT Rules Second Amendment may address legitimate concerns about misinformation on platforms. But it also risks adding compliance burdens to an already struggling industry and potentially reducing the space for independent journalism. The relationship between regulation and trust is complex, and there is no evidence that government oversight of content increases public confidence in the news.

09. Final Analysis

The Reuters Institute's 2026 Digital News Report is the most comprehensive picture yet of a media ecosystem in transition. In India, the triple shock of platform migration, trust erosion, and news avoidance has created an environment where the foundations of the industry — audience attention, advertising revenue, public trust — are all under simultaneous structural pressure. The numbers are sobering: 39% trust, 58% on YouTube, 52% avoiding news, 22% on AI chatbots. But they are not a death sentence for journalism.

The report also contains reasons for cautious optimism. Trust in individual news brands remains relatively strong. Audiences continue to value journalism — they just want it in different formats and through different channels. The markets with the highest trust (Finland at 69%) demonstrate that trust in news is not a relic of a pre-digital age; it is a product of sustained investment in media literacy, editorial independence, and transparent journalism. The countries that have maintained trust have done so by treating it as a strategic priority, not an afterthought.

What the 2026 report ultimately reveals is that the crisis facing Indian journalism is not a crisis of quality — it is a crisis of connection. Indian newsrooms produce journalism that is, by and large, as good as it has ever been. But they have lost the connection to the audiences they serve. Rebuilding that connection will require changes that go beyond social media distribution strategies. It will require a fundamental reassessment of how journalism presents itself, how it formats its output, how it engages with platforms, and how it earns — and keeps — the trust of a public that has more reasons than ever to tune out.

The warning in the 2026 report is real. But so is the opportunity. The news industry cannot rebuild trust without first understanding why it lost it. This year's Digital News Report provides that understanding. The question now is whether the industry is ready to act on it.
