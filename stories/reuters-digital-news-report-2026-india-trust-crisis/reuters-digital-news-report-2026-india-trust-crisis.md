## Cold Open

Trust in news has hit its lowest level globally since the Reuters Institute began its survey in 2015 — 37 per cent across 47 countries. India recorded one of the steepest year-on-year declines, dropping 4 percentage points to 39 per cent. The 15th Digital News Report, released on June 16, documents a structural shift: for the first time, social media and video networks have overtaken owned websites and apps as the primary news source, fundamentally altering the relationship between publishers and their audiences. In India, 22 per cent of respondents now use AI chatbots for news — double the global average and the highest among all surveyed markets.

## Key Findings

YouTube has emerged as India's dominant news platform, used by 58 per cent of respondents, followed by WhatsApp at 56 per cent — a 10-point increase since 2021. News avoidance has reached alarming levels: 52 per cent of Indian respondents said they sometimes or often actively avoid news, citing negative effects on mood, political fatigue, and misinformation concerns. The shift to platform-dominated consumption undermines publishers' direct relationships with audiences and their ability to monetise content. As audiences migrate toward lower-trust sources — trust in social media news stands at just 22 per cent, AI chatbot news at 20 per cent — the overall trust figure is pulled downward by the changing composition of consumption itself.

## India's Trust Decline

India's 39 per cent trust figure places it well below the global average and represents the steepest year-on-year decline among all 47 surveyed countries. The report identifies a feedback loop: as trust erodes, audiences shift to platforms and chatbots; those channels score lower on trust, which further erodes aggregate trust. The "shadow news" ecosystem — unverified content shared through WhatsApp forwards, YouTube channels, and individual social media accounts — compounds the problem by blurring the line between journalism and information arbitrage. India's proposed IT Rules Second Amendment 2026, which brings news-sharing social media accounts under the Digital Media Ethics Code, is a direct policy response to this shadow ecosystem.

## Comparison with Other Nations

Finland retains the highest trust at over 60 per cent, driven by strong public service media and high media literacy. The United States recorded 25 per cent — among the lowest in the developed world. India's 4-point decline is the steepest single-year drop in the survey. Northern European markets consistently score above 50 per cent, while Southern Europe, Latin America, and parts of Asia — markets with high political polarisation and weak public service media — have seen the most significant erosion. India's position at 39 per cent in this global context suggests structural, not cyclical, trust deterioration.

## Implications

The shift to platform-dominated consumption weakens publishers' direct audience relationships and monetisation capability. India's 22 per cent AI chatbot usage for news — double the global average — signals that Indian audiences are early adopters of algorithmically curated news, raising concerns about filter bubbles and misinformation amplification. The report notes that 27 per cent of global respondents get news through individual creators, who score lower on trust but higher on entertainment. The overall trajectory points toward a more fragmented, platform-dependent, and trust-challenged news environment — and India, with its steep trust decline and high chatbot adoption, is at the leading edge of that trend.
