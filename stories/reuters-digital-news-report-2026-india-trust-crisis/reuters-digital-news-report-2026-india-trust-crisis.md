## Cold Open

The Reuters Institute's 15th Digital News Report has documented a historic collapse in trust in news, with the global average falling to 37 per cent â€” the lowest level since the survey began in 2015. India recorded one of the steepest declines among the 47 countries surveyed, with trust dropping 4 percentage points from 2025 to 39 per cent. The 2026 report, released on June 16, paints a sobering picture of a news industry grappling with platform displacement, audience fragmentation, and a deepening trust deficit that threatens the commercial viability of professional journalism.

## Key Findings

The report reveals transformative shifts in how Indians consume news. For the first time, social media and video networks have overtaken owned websites and apps as the primary source of news, marking a fundamental change in the relationship between publishers and audiences. YouTube has emerged as India's dominant news platform, used by 58 per cent of respondents, followed by WhatsApp at 56 per cent â€” a 10 percentage point increase since 2021. News avoidance has reached alarming levels: 52 per cent of Indian respondents said they sometimes or often actively avoid news, citing negative effects on mood, political fatigue, and concerns about misinformation. In a striking indicator of the changing landscape, 22 per cent of Indian respondents reported using AI chatbots for news â€” double the global average of 10 per cent and the highest among all markets surveyed.

## India's Trust Decline

India's 39 per cent trust figure places it well below the top-performing countries and significantly lower than the global average. The decline is particularly noteworthy given that trust in Indian news media had remained relatively stable in previous years. The report identifies a pronounced trust gap: trust in news on social media stands at just 22 per cent, while trust in news accessed through AI chatbots is even lower at 20 per cent. As audiences shift towards platform-based consumption, lower-trust sources account for a growing share of news engagement, pulling the overall trust figure downward. The "shadow news" ecosystem of unverified content shared through WhatsApp forwards, YouTube channels, and social media posts has further eroded confidence in journalistic institutions.

## Comparison with Other Nations

Finland retains the highest trust levels at over 60 per cent, attributed to strong public service media and high media literacy. The United States recorded 25 per cent â€” one of the lowest among developed nations. India's 39 per cent represents the steepest year-on-year decline among all 47 surveyed countries. The report notes that markets with high political polarisation and weak public service media tend to record lower trust levels. Countries in Northern Europe consistently score above 50 per cent, while Southern Europe, Latin America, and parts of Asia have seen the most significant erosion.

## Implications

The shift to platform-dominated consumption undermines publishers' direct relationships with audiences and their ability to monetise content. India's 22 per cent AI chatbot usage for news â€” double the global average â€” suggests audiences are early adopters of algorithmically curated news, raising concerns about filter bubbles. The overall trajectory points towards a more fragmented, platform-dependent, and trust-challenged news environment.
