The Long Thaw: India and China Inch Toward Normalisation

After six years of frozen ties, NSA Ajit Doval and Chinese FM Wang Yi met in New Delhi and noted "progress towards gradual normalisation." The trajectory has shifted — but the gap between diplomatic language and ground reality remains wide.

For four years — from the Galwan Valley bloodshed in June 2020 through a diplomatic deep freeze that stretched into late 2024 — the India-China relationship was defined not by what was said but by what was not. No high-level political contact. No new dialogue mechanisms. Over 50,000 troops stationed within striking distance along the Line of Actual Control in eastern Ladakh. Then, over two days on the margins of the BRICS National Security Advisers meeting in New Delhi, NSA Ajit Doval and Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi did what neither side had managed with conviction since the winter of 2020: they acknowledged that the relationship was, slowly, healing.

The Indian readout was measured but significant: "The two sides reviewed recent developments in bilateral relations and noted progress towards gradual normalisation in ties." Doval underlined that stable, predictable and constructive relations contribute to building trust, and stressed the importance of sensitivity to each other's "issues of core concern." The Chinese account, released by state-run Xinhua, was notably warmer. Wang Yi called India "an important neighbour of China," hailed the Kazan consensus that "China and India are partners rather than rivals," and urged both sides to accelerate dialogue across trade, finance, law enforcement, and media.

Bottom Line

The Doval-Wang meeting on June 22–23, 2026, marks the highest-level bilateral contact since the Ladakh standoff began. Both sides confirmed a trajectory toward normalisation. But no new disengagement agreements were announced, direct flights remain suspended, and India's trade deficit with China exceeds $60 billion. The diplomatic language has shifted from confrontation to cautious engagement; the hard work of rebuilding trust lies ahead.

Historical Background: From Galwan to Kazan

The current thaw did not begin in June 2026. It is the product of a slow, often interrupted process that started with the bloodshed of June 15, 2020, when soldiers on both sides clashed in the Galwan Valley with rocks, clubs, and improvised weapons — the first fatal confrontation between Indian and Chinese troops along the LAC since 1975. Twenty Indian soldiers and at least four Chinese soldiers were killed. The shock fundamentally altered India's approach to China: New Delhi imposed a sweeping ban on Chinese apps, tightened investment rules, and froze virtually all dialogue mechanisms.

The first breakthrough came in February 2021, when after nine rounds of military talks, both sides completed disengagement at Pangong Tso. But the breakthrough was partial. Troops remained in place at other friction points, and the diplomatic chill persisted through 2022 and 2023. No high-level political contact occurred between the two governments.

The logjam broke in October 2024. India and China announced a disengagement pact covering the remaining friction points at Depsang and Demchok. Days later, Prime Minister Narendra Modi and President Xi Jinping held their first bilateral meeting in five years on the margins of the BRICS summit in Kazan, Russia. The meeting produced what both sides now call the Kazan consensus: "China and India are partners rather than rivals."

The next milestone came in August 2025, when Doval and Wang held the Special Representatives dialogue in New Delhi — the first SR meeting since 2019, producing multiple outcomes to maintain border peace. The June 2026 BRICS meeting was the natural next step in that sequence.

Current Situation: The New Delhi Meeting

The Doval-Wang meeting took place on Monday, June 22, on the sidelines of the two-day BRICS NSAs meeting — the first such meeting hosted under India's 2025–26 BRICS chairmanship, themed "Building for Resilience, Innovation, Cooperation and Sustainability." Foreign Secretary Vikram Misri and Chinese Ambassador to India Xu Feihong were also present.

The Indian position, as articulated by Doval, was conditional but not hostile. He stressed that stable, predictable and constructive bilateral relations contribute to building trust and better understanding, and emphasised the importance of sensitivity to each other's "issues of core concern" — diplomatic shorthand for border sovereignty, Chinese infrastructure construction along the LAC, and the status of India's northeastern states. The sequence in Doval's framing was deliberate: stability first, trust second.

The Chinese readout struck a notably different tone. Wang Yi described bilateral relations as having "returned to the path of recovery and improvement." He called on both sides to implement the Kazan consensus faithfully and urged accelerating the resumption of dialogue mechanisms in trade, finance, law enforcement, and — notably — media and societal exchanges. This was the first time China has explicitly pushed for people-to-people and media engagement as part of the normalisation track.

"China and India are partners rather than rivals. This is the most important strategic consensus between the two sides."

Wang Yi, Xinhua readout, June 22, 2026

Wang also argued that the border issue should be placed "in an appropriate position so that it does not affect the overall trajectory of bilateral relations" — signalling Beijing's desire to compartmentalise the border dispute rather than let it define the entire relationship. He framed India-China cooperation as a broader geopolitical good, urging both sides to "promote their respective development and revitalisation through cooperation, and accelerate the modernisation process of the Global South."

Winners & Losers

The gradual normalisation creates distinct winners and losers across the strategic landscape. Not everyone gains from a reset in India-China ties.

Winners

Indian border security: Reduced troop deployment frees resources for other theatres

Indian exporters: Potential market access improvements could narrow the $60B+ trade deficit

Chinese diplomacy: Beijing prevents India from drifting fully into the US-led Indo-Pacific architecture

BRICS multilateralism: China-India convergence strengthens the bloc's Global South messaging

Direct flight operators: Resumption of air links would unlock pent-up demand

Losers

Taiwan lobby: Improved India-China ties reduce space for parallel diplomacy with Taipei

Indian defence hawks: Normalisation undermines the case for a permanent cold war posture

US strategic planners: A stable India-China relationship weakens the Quad's original rationale

Chinese border hawks: Those who view concessions at Depsang/Demchok as a retreat

Chinese app ban beneficiaries: Indian alternatives that filled the TikTok/WeChat void face renewed competition

Expert Opinions

The Doval-Wang meeting has drawn a spectrum of analysis from strategic commentators. The consensus: the direction is positive, but the pace is uncertain.

"The Kazan consensus gave both sides political cover to resume engagement. The SR dialogue in August 2025 provided the operational framework. The June 2026 meeting is the first test of whether that framework can sustain momentum. The next six months — particularly Doval's expected visit to Beijing — will tell us if this is a real thaw or a tactical pause."

Strategic affairs analyst, specialising in India-China border issues

"Wang Yi's call for accelerating media and societal exchanges is significant. For the first time, Beijing is explicitly targeting Indian public opinion as a channel of influence. This is a calculated bet that normalising the information environment — direct flights, journalist visas, think-tank exchanges — will create a constituency for stable ties that constrains India's ability to adopt hardline positions."

Former diplomat, on condition of anonymity

"India's $60 billion trade deficit with China is not a natural market outcome — it reflects structural asymmetries in market access, tariffs, and non-tariff barriers. If normalisation is to be meaningful, it must address this imbalance. India cannot indefinitely sustain a relationship where the primary economic metric is a deficit that exceeds the GDP of half the countries in the world."

Trade economist, New Delhi-based research institution

What's Changed — And What Hasn't

Progress Achieved

  • High-level political contact resumed at NSA and foreign minister level
  • Disengagement at Depsang and Demchok removed key military friction points
  • Kazan consensus provides a shared political framework: "partners, not rivals"
  • China explicitly pushing for broader exchanges beyond security — trade, finance, media
  • India's BRICS chairmanship creates natural institutional opportunities for dialogue
  • Beijing has publicly supported India's BRICS leadership role

Remaining Friction Points

  • No agreement on patrolling rights or buffer zones along the LAC
  • Direct flights remain suspended despite months of negotiations
  • Most dialogue mechanisms — joint working groups, boundary talks — not yet resumed
  • Chinese road-building and military infrastructure in border areas continues
  • India's trade deficit with China exceeded $60 billion in 2025–26
  • Chinese apps and investment restrictions remain in place
  • Trust between security establishments has not been rebuilt

Future Outlook

Doval's Beijing visit: The next Special Representatives dialogue is expected to take place in Beijing, though no date has been confirmed. The visit will be the critical test of whether "gradual normalisation" translates into concrete outcomes — particularly on patrol protocols and dialogue resumption timelines.

Xi's India visit: President Xi Jinping is widely expected to attend the BRICS Leaders Summit in September 2026 in India. If confirmed, it would be Xi's first visit to India since the Wuhan and Mamallapuram summits of 2018–2019, and would provide a second Modi-Xi meeting in two years — a pace of top-level contact unseen since the pre-Galwan era.

Dialogue resumption: Wang's call for accelerating trade, finance, law enforcement, and media exchanges suggests Beijing is pushing for a broader reset that extends well beyond border management. India's response will depend on progress on core concerns — New Delhi has consistently held that normalisation cannot be piecemeal and that progress on the border is a prerequisite for wider engagement.

Structural constraints: Both sides face domestic constituencies deeply sceptical of the other. India's political establishment and strategic community remain wary of Chinese intentions after 2020. China's leadership, navigating a period of economic slowdown and geopolitical isolation, finds India engagement valuable but conditional. The structural competition between Asia's two giants — over influence in the Indo-Pacific, the Global South, and the evolving world order — is not resolved by joint communiqués.

Final Analysis

Final Analysis

The Doval-Wang meeting represents a genuine but fragile step toward normalisation. Both sides have an interest in stabilising the relationship — India to reduce military pressure on its northern border and free resources for other strategic priorities; China to prevent India from drifting further into the US-led Indo-Pacific architecture and to strengthen the BRICS mechanism at a time of Western pressure on Beijing. But the gap between diplomatic language and ground reality remains wide. The LAC has not been fully restored to its pre-2020 status. Trust has not been rebuilt. The structural competition between Asia's two giants — demographic trajectories, technological rivalry, contested influence in South Asia and Southeast Asia — is not resolved by joint readouts.

The next six months will determine whether "gradual normalisation" becomes a durable trend or just another chapter in the cycle of confrontation and thaw that has defined India-China relations for decades. Doval's expected visit to Beijing and Xi Jinping's anticipated attendance at the BRICS Leaders Summit in September 2026 will be the definitive tests. Until then, the trajectory has shifted — but the destination remains uncertain.
