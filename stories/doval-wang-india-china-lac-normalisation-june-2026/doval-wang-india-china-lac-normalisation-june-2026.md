# India-China LAC Normalisation: After Four Years, a 'Gradual Normalisation'

## Cold Open

On June 22, 2026, India's National Security Adviser Ajit Doval and Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi met on the margins of the BRICS National Security Advisers meeting in New Delhi. The meeting produced what both sides described as "progress towards gradual normalisation" of bilateral ties â€” the most significant high-level contact between the two nations since the Galwan Valley clash of June 2020 that left twenty Indian soldiers dead and an unspecified number of Chinese casualties. The June 22-23 meeting in New Delhi was not merely a diplomatic formality; it represented the culmination of a painstaking process that began with tactical disengagement agreements in late 2024 and has slowly built momentum towards political dialogue.

## The Meeting

Doval and Wang met against the backdrop of India's chairpersonship of BRICS in 2026, a role that has given New Delhi additional diplomatic leverage. During the discussions, Doval stressed the need for "stable, predictable and constructive bilateral relations" and emphasised the importance of sensitivity to each other's "core concerns" â€” a phrase that Indian officials have consistently used to reference border sovereignty, cross-border terrorism, and the strategic balance in the Indian Ocean Region. Wang, for his part, described India as "an important neighbour of China" and urged both sides to accelerate the resumption of stalled dialogue mechanisms across multiple domains including trade, finance, law enforcement, and media exchanges. Both sides also acknowledged the role of the October 2024 Depsang and Demchok disengagement pact, as well as the subsequent meeting between Prime Minister Narendra Modi and President Xi Jinping in Kazan in October 2024, which provided the political framework for the current normalisation process.

## Key Outcomes

The meeting yielded no formal agreements or signed documents, but the joint readout was significant for its carefully calibrated language. Both sides reviewed the current situation along the Line of Actual Control in eastern Ladakh and noted the enhanced trust and deeper understanding that has emerged from sustained diplomatic engagement at multiple levels. The trajectory has perceptibly shifted from the confrontation that defined bilateral relations between 2020 and 2024 to a phase of cautious engagement. However, several key irritants remain unresolved. Direct flights between India and China remain suspended, most of the bilateral dialogue mechanisms that were frozen after Galwan have not yet been formally revived, and India's trade deficit with China continues to exceed $60 billion annually â€” a figure that rankles Indian policymakers.

## LAC Status

The situation on the LAC has improved significantly since the peak of the military standoff in 2020-2021, when an estimated 50,000 to 60,000 troops from each side were deployed in close proximity along the disputed boundary. The disengagement at Pangong Tso in February 2021 was followed by the more consequential Depsang and Demchok pact in October 2024, which addressed the last remaining friction points in eastern Ladakh. Military deployment levels have been reduced from their 2020 peak, although both sides continue to maintain a substantial presence in the region. No new disengagement agreements were announced during the Doval-Wang meeting, suggesting that the focus has now shifted from tactical military arrangements to the broader political framework for stable bilateral relations.

## Next Steps

The normalisation process is expected to continue through a series of high-level engagements in the coming months. Doval is expected to travel to Beijing for the next round of the Special Representatives dialogue on the boundary question, a mechanism that has historically been the primary channel for discussing the broader border dispute. The most significant milestone on the horizon is President Xi Jinping's anticipated visit to India for the BRICS Leaders Summit scheduled for September 2026, which would mark the first Chinese presidential visit to India in several years and could provide the political impetus needed to move beyond cautious engagement towards a more stable and predictable bilateral relationship.
