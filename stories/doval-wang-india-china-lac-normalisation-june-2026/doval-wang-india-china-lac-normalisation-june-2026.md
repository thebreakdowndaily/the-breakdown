# India-China LAC Normalisation: Four Years After Galwan, the Ice Is Finally Thawing — Barely

## Cold Open

On June 22, 2026, India's National Security Adviser Ajit Doval and Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi met in New Delhi — the highest-level bilateral contact since the Galwan Valley clash of June 2020 that left 20 Indian soldiers dead. Both sides described the discussion as registering "progress towards gradual normalisation" of ties, but the joint readout contained no new agreements, no signed documents, and no troop disengagement announcements. Six years after the deadliest India-China border conflict in decades, the relationship remains frozen in a state of cautious management rather than genuine rapprochement.

## The Meeting

Doval-Wang talks were held on the margins of the BRICS NSAs meeting, with India's 2026 BRICS chairpersonship providing additional diplomatic leverage. Doval stressed the need for "stable, predictable and constructive bilateral relations" with sensitivity to each other's "core concerns" — Indian code for border sovereignty, cross-border terrorism, and Indian Ocean Region strategic balance. Wang described India as "an important neighbour" and urged resumption of stalled dialogue mechanisms across trade, finance, law enforcement, and media. Both sides credited the October 2024 Depsang and Demchok disengagement pact and the subsequent Modi-Xi meeting in Kazan as the political foundation for the current thaw.

## Key Outcomes

The absence of formal outcomes was itself meaningful. The trajectory has shifted from the 2020-2024 confrontation phase to cautious engagement, but four critical irritants remain unresolved. Direct flights between India and China are still suspended. Most bilateral dialogue mechanisms frozen after Galwan have not been revived. India's trade deficit with China continues to exceed $60 billion annually. And China has yet to address India's concerns over cross-border terrorism and the Belt and Road projects in Pakistan-occupied Kashmir. The Doval-Wang meeting tested the waters; the real decisions await higher-level engagement.

## LAC Status

Military deployment levels along the Line of Actual Control have been reduced from the 2020-2021 peak — when an estimated 50,000-60,000 troops from each side were deployed in close proximity — following the Depsang and Demchok pact. No further disengagement was announced, indicating that the focus has shifted from tactical military arrangements to the broader political framework for stable bilateral relations. Both sides continue to maintain a substantial presence in eastern Ladakh, and the underlying territorial dispute remains as intractable as ever.

## Next Steps

Doval is expected to travel to Beijing for the next round of the Special Representatives dialogue on the boundary question. The most significant milestone on the horizon is President Xi Jinping's anticipated visit to India for the BRICS Leaders Summit in September 2026 — which would be the first Chinese presidential visit in several years. A Xi visit would provide the political impetus to move beyond cautious engagement toward a more stable relationship, but it also raises the stakes: if the visit produces no concrete outcomes, the normalisation narrative itself could stall.
