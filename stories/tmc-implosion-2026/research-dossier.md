## PHASE 1 — TOPIC DEFINITION & RESEARCH QUESTIONS

### Primary Topic
The collapse of the All India Trinamool Congress (AITC/TMC) after losing the May 2026 West Bengal Assembly election, culminating in 20 of its 28 Lok Sabha MPs defecting to the NDA by merging with the obscure Nationalist Citizens Party of India (NCPI).

### Related Topics
- Anti-defection law (10th Schedule) and the "merger" clause
- BJP's "Operation Tiger" / Operation Kamal strategy to split opposition parties
- The NCPI — a registered-unrecognised party used as a vehicle
- Speaker Om Birla's powers and pending decision
- NDA parliamentary arithmetic and hunt for 2/3 majority
- INDIA bloc disintegration (DMK exit, AAP split, TMC collapse)
- West Bengal post-election governance under CM Suvendu Adhikari
- Delimitation Bill 2026, One Nation One Election, Uniform Civil Code

## APPENDIX: LEGAL FRAMEWORK REFERENCE

### The 10th Schedule (Anti-Defection Law) — Key Provisions

- **Paragraph 2:** An MP/MLA is disqualified if they voluntarily give up party membership or vote against party whip.
- **Paragraph 3 (deleted by 91st Amendment, 2003):** Previously allowed "split" if 1/3 broke away. Now deleted.
- **Paragraph 4 (Merger Clause):** Disqualification does not apply if the original political party merges with another party, provided at least 2/3 of the legislature party members agree to the merger.
- **Paragraph 6:** Speaker/Chairman decides; decision subject to judicial review (Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu, 1992).
- **Key SC rulings:** Subhash Desai v. Principal Secretary (2023) — political party and legislature party are distinct; Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker (2016) — anti-defection purpose; Mayawati v. Markandeya Chand (1998) — "political party" cannot be read as "legislature party."

### The Core Legal Question

Can 20 MPs (individually, without the TMC party's consent) merge into the NCPI and claim protection under Paragraph 4?

**Rebel position:** Yes — 2/3 of legislature party is sufficient; NCPI is a registered party.

**TMC position:** No — Subhash Desai requires the *original political party* (TMC) itself to merge; only the legislature party acting cannot effect a merger.

**Current status:** Before Speaker Om Birla. He is seeking Law Ministry opinion and will hear both sides before ruling. Decision expected before Monsoon Session (July 2026).
