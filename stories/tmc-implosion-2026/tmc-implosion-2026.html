TMC Implosion — 20 MPs Defect to NDA via Obscure Party, Rewriting Parliament's Arithmetic

India's strongest regional party has effectively disintegrated. Twenty of 28 TMC Lok Sabha MPs merged into the Nationalist Citizens Party of India — a shell entity with 822 career votes and 90 Facebook followers — and pledged support to the NDA. Here is how it happened, what the law says, and what comes next.

The NDA now commands approximately 313 votes in the 543-member Lok Sabha — up from 293 after the 2024 election. It remains 49 short of the 362 needed for a two-thirds majority under Article 368, but the gap is closing faster than at any point in Modi's tenure.

How It Happened

The collapse of India's strongest regional party unfolded in three phases over 42 days — from a lost state election to a parliamentary defection that reshaped the Lok Sabha's balance of power.

Phase 1: The Bengal Election — May 2026

The West Bengal Assembly election held April 23–29, 2026, produced a political earthquake. The BJP won 206–207 seats to the TMC's 80–81 — a swing of more than 130 seats from 2021. Mamata Banerjee lost her own Bhabanipur seat to Suvendu Adhikari by approximately 15,000 votes. On May 9, Adhikari was sworn in as the first BJP chief minister of West Bengal at Brigade Parade Ground, with Amit Shah as central observer.

Phase 2: The Assembly Rebellion — Late May to Early June

Within weeks of the election, internal dissent within the TMC's legislative party boiled over. On June 1, the party expelled MLAs Ritabrata Banerjee and Sandipan Saha for "anti-party activities." Two days later, 58 of the 80 TMC MLAs backed Banerjee as Leader of the Opposition. Assembly Speaker Rathindra Bose accepted the claim. Banerjee's support base has since escalated to a claimed 65 MLAs, though the Calcutta High Court refused to stay his LoP recognition on June 18.

Phase 3: The Lok Sabha Defection — June 8–14

On June 8, 20 TMC Lok Sabha MPs — amounting to 71% of the party's parliamentary strength — wrote to Speaker Om Birla expressing their desire to ally with the NDA. The same day, senior Rajya Sabha MP Sukhendu Sekhar Ray resigned from the party and his seat, citing corruption within the TMC. Three more Rajya Sabha MPs followed: Sushmita Dev (June 10), Prakash Chik Baraik, and actress Koel Mallick.

On June 14, the rebel MPs met Speaker Om Birla and formally announced their merger with the Nationalist Citizens Party of India (NCPI). Kakoli Ghosh Dastidar, the former TMC chief whip who had resigned her party posts on May 27, was elected president of the NCPI — having taken over the party's leadership days before the merger was publicly announced. Veteran six-time MP Sudip Bandyopadhyay, initially absent from the rebel list, joined the group and was photographed with the Speaker, bringing the defector count to 20.

"We, the twenty MPs elected from the AITC, met the Speaker and submitted a letter requesting to sit separately," Kakoli told reporters. "We are merging with the Nationalist Citizens Party. Moving forward, we will work for the nation and collaborate with the NDA under the leadership of the Prime Minister."

The NCPI Mystery — From 822 Votes to 20 MPs

The Nationalist Citizens Party of India was founded on October 14, 2022, by Shewly Kundu in Howrah, West Bengal. It registered with the Election Commission on January 20, 2023, as a Registered-Unrecognised Political Party (RUPP) — the lowest tier of official recognition.

The NCPI's political philosophy, per its ECI registration, is "Liberalism (Indian), Bengali nationalism, Regionalism." It contested four assembly seats in the 2023 Tripura elections and received between 822 and 1,198 total votes across all constituencies. It has received ₹1.13 lakh in total donations. Before June 2026, it had approximately 90 Facebook followers.

Its registered headquarters is a residential property in Hatgacha village, Howrah district — the home of the Kundu family. The original party leadership consisted of Shewly Kundu (president), Uttiya Kundu (vice president and treasurer), and Shantanu Dey (organising secretary). Its election symbol is a pen nib with seven rays.

The central irony: A party that received 822 career votes across four assembly seats in Tripura now controls 20 MPs in the Lok Sabha — making it the second-largest NDA constituent by parliamentary strength, behind only the BJP itself.

The takeover appears to have been planned well in advance. Shewly Kundu resigned as NCPI president on May 28 — 11 days before the merger was announced and just 24 days after the Bengal election results. Kakoli Ghosh Dastidar was elected president on May 31. Shantanu Dey, the NCPI's organising secretary, told reporters on June 15 that he "learned about this development through social media and news reports" — though he later performed a U-turn and welcomed the merger. Shewly Kundu told the Indian Express she "does not know who the new president is."

The Defectors

The 20 defecting MPs represent all major regions of West Bengal — from the northern tea belt of Cooch Behar to the industrial Howrah and Hooghly districts, the cultural hubs of Kolkata and Jadavpur, and the rural SC/ST constituencies of Jhargram and Arambagh. Five are former actors or actresses; one is a former cricketer (Yusuf Pathan).

The Legal Battle — Can 20 MPs Merge Without Their Party?

The legality of the NCPI merger hinges on a single question: does Paragraph 4 of the 10th Schedule (the anti-defection law) allow two-thirds of a legislature party to merge into another party without the consent of the original political party?

The rebel MPs argue yes. Paragraph 4 states that disqualification does not apply if the original political party merges with another party, provided at least two-thirds of the legislature party members agree to the merger. With 20 of 28 MPs (71%) supporting the move, they comfortably meet the numerical threshold.

The TMC argues no. The Supreme Court's 2023 judgment in Subhash Desai v. Principal Secretary established that the political party (the AITC as an organisation) and the legislature party (its MPs/MLAs) are legally distinct entities. Two-thirds of the legislature party cannot effect a merger that the political party itself has not consented to — and Mamata Banerjee remains the TMC chairperson.

"Paragraph 4 of the 10th Schedule — only a political party can merge with another political party, not MPs or MLAs. If the leadership of a political party decides to merge with another political party, its MLAs and MPs have to agree on the merger. But MPs or MLAs alone cannot merge with another political party."

— PDT Achary, former Lok Sabha Secretary General

Kapil Sibal, the constitutional lawyer and Rajya Sabha MP, has described the rebels' move as legally unsustainable: the party must merge first; only then does the question of the fate of MPs arise. Former Election Commission officials have called the strategy an "innovation" with no basis in either the anti-defection law or the Representation of the People Act.

On June 19, Abhishek Banerjee met Speaker Om Birla and filed 20 disqualification petitions against the rebel MPs. The Speaker's office has sought the Law Ministry's opinion and must hear both factions before ruling. As of June 26, the decision remains pending — and it is the single most consequential parliamentary ruling since the recognition of the Shinde faction in 2022.

The Speaker faces a delicate political calculation. If he rules against the merger, 20 MPs face disqualification, the NDA loses its newly gained numbers, and the government's legislative agenda is hobbled. If he rules in favour, it sets a precedent that any two-thirds of a legislature party can defect en masse using a shell party — potentially triggering further opposition collapses ahead of the 2029 election.

What It Means for Parliament

Lok Sabha: Closer, but Still Short of 2/3

The NDA's effective Lok Sabha strength has risen from 293 to approximately 313 — an increase of 20 seats. But the 362 needed for a two-thirds majority under Article 368 remains out of reach. The shortfall of 49 seats could narrow further if six Shiv Sena (UBT) MPs complete their anticipated switch to the Shinde camp (+6 → 319) and if the DMK's 22 MPs extend issue-based support on constitutional amendments (+22 → 341). Even in the most optimistic scenario — adding the four YSRCP MPs and winning three pending bypolls — the NDA would reach approximately 345 seats, still 17 short of 362.

What 2/3 would unlock: Constitutional amendments require a two-thirds majority of members present and voting, plus a majority of state legislatures for certain bills. The key prizes are the Delimitation Bill (defeated in April 2026 with 298 votes for, 230 against), the "One Nation One Election" framework, and potentially the Uniform Civil Code. None of these are achievable at 313.

Rajya Sabha: The Real Story

The NDA's Rajya Sabha position has strengthened more decisively. The resignation of four TMC Rajya Sabha MPs — Sukhendu Sekhar Ray, Sushmita Dev, Prakash Chik Baraik, and Koel Mallick — has created vacancies that the NDA is likely to fill. Current estimates put the NDA at approximately 151 seats, with the potential to reach 154–155 after the TMC vacancies are filled. The target for a Rajya Sabha two-thirds majority is 163 out of 245 — a shortfall of approximately 8–9. The opposition's effective strength has collapsed to approximately 63 MPs.

The INDIA Bloc: Effectively Dead

The 17-party opposition alliance that contested the 2024 election no longer exists in any meaningful form. The DMK exited in early June after the Congress allied with the TVK in Tamil Nadu. The AAP left after seven of its ten Rajya Sabha MPs joined the BJP in April. The TMC — the alliance's second-largest member — has imploded. The Shiv Sena (UBT) is on the verge of losing six of its nine Lok Sabha MPs. The BJP has declared the bloc "dead and buried." Only the Congress, RJD, SP, and NCP (SP) remain — a fraction of the coalition that once held the government accountable.

Mamata Banerjee, once India's most formidable regional leader, now presides over a party reduced from 29 Lok Sabha MPs in June 2024 to approximately eight. She lost the Bengal Assembly, lost her own seat, and has now lost control of her party's parliamentary wing. Abhishek Banerjee, who inherited the organisational leadership, faces both the political crisis and an expanding ED/CBI investigation. The Calcutta High Court on June 24 rejected his plea to travel abroad for medical treatment.

The next 60 days will determine whether the TMC's collapse is a one-party tragedy or the opening act of a broader opposition realignment. If the Speaker rules in favour of the merger, the template is established — any two-thirds of a legislature party can defect via a shell party without triggering disqualification. The immediate targets are the remaining opposition parties with vulnerable legislators.

If the Speaker rules against the merger, 20 MPs face by-elections, the NDA loses its gains, and the government's constitutional agenda stalls. The Supreme Court will likely have the final word regardless — but that could take months or years.

The Monsoon Session, confirmed for July 21 to August 21, will be the first real test. How the House treats 20 NCPI MPs — what seating they get, whether they are recognised as a bloc, how whips are enforced — will signal the government's confidence in its new numbers.

West Bengal, meanwhile, is adjusting to a new political reality. The BJP's Suvendu Adhikari governs with a comfortable Assembly majority. Ritabrata Banerjee leads an opposition whose numbers keep shifting. And the party that dominated Bengal politics for over a decade has been reduced to a rump — fighting for relevance in the state it once ruled uncontested.
