## Cold Open

On 14 June 2026, a group of 20 parliamentarians walked into Lok Sabha Speaker Om Birla's official residence in New Delhi and calmly announced they were no longer members of the Trinamool Congress. In a single afternoon, the party that had dominated West Bengal politics for fifteen years and once commanded 29 Lok Sabha seats saw its parliamentary strength collapse to just eight MPs. The defectors — led by four-term MP Kakoli Ghosh Dastidar — declared a merger with the Nationalist Citizens Party of India (NCPI), an entity so obscure it had received 822 votes in its last election and counted barely 90 followers on Facebook.

## The Backstory

The seeds of the TMC's implosion were sown in the West Bengal Assembly election of May 2026. For fifteen years, Mamata Banerjee had dominated Bengal politics, winning three consecutive terms. The 2026 election shattered that narrative: the BJP swept to power with 206 seats while the TMC was reduced to 80. Mamata herself lost her Bhabanipur constituency to Suvendu Adhikari, who was sworn in as Bengal's first BJP chief minister on 9 May 2026.

The scale of the defeat triggered an immediate leadership crisis. With Mamata no longer an MLA and her nephew Abhishek Banerjee increasingly seen as the de facto party chief, factional grievances erupted. "Was it Didi standing for election, or was it us?" rebel MP Arup Chakraborty later asked. "She handed responsibility to Abhishek Banerjee — that's why the party was ruined."

## The Assembly Rebellion

The first wave of the rebellion hit the West Bengal Assembly. Within weeks of the election result, discontent among TMC legislators escalated into open revolt. On 1 June, the party expelled MLAs Ritabrata Banerjee and Sandipan Saha for "anti-party activities." The expulsion backfired spectacularly. Two days later, 58 of the TMC's 80 MLAs — 72 per cent of the legislature party — backed Ritabrata Banerjee as the Leader of the Opposition. Assembly Speaker Rathindra Bose accepted the claim, effectively recognising the rebel faction as the official opposition in the House.

The TMC leadership responded by dissolving all organisational committees and frontal organisations across West Bengal, a move that only deepened the chaos. By mid-June, Ritabrata claimed his support had swelled to 65 MLAs. The Calcutta High Court, which the TMC approached to challenge his LoP recognition, refused to stay the appointment on 18 June, finding "no prima facie case." The matter is listed for further hearing on 28 July.

## The Parliament Defection

If the Assembly rebellion was damaging, the Lok Sabha defection was catastrophic. On 8 June — the same day Mamata and Abhishek Banerjee attended an INDIA bloc meeting in Delhi — 20 TMC Lok Sabha MPs wrote to Speaker Om Birla expressing their desire to align with the NDA. The group included some of the party's most recognisable faces: actors-turned-politicians Satabdi Roy, Saayoni Ghosh, and Dev (Deepak Adhikari); cricketer Yusuf Pathan; and six-time MP Sudip Bandyopadhyay, who had served as the TMC's Lok Sabha leader from 2011 to 2025 and joined the rebels on 14 June after initially staying out of the first letter.

On 14 June, the rebel MPs met the Speaker, submitted their merger papers, and formally announced their allegiance to the NDA. "We, the twenty MPs elected from the AITC, met the Speaker and submitted a letter requesting to sit separately," Kakoli Ghosh Dastidar told reporters. "We are merging with the Nationalist Citizens Party. Moving forward, we will work for the nation and collaborate with the NDA under the leadership of the Prime Minister."

Of the TMC's original 28 Lok Sabha MPs (one seat, Basirhat, had been vacant since the death of MP Haji Nurul Islam), only eight remained loyal: Abhishek Banerjee, Mahua Moitra, Kalyan Banerjee, Saugata Roy, Kirti Azad, Shatrughan Sinha, Sajda Ahmed, and Pratima Mondal. The Rajya Sabha contingent also took a hit — four MPs including senior leader Sukhendu Sekhar Ray and former Union minister Sushmita Dev resigned from the party and the Upper House within the span of a week.

## The NCPI Vehicle

The vehicle for this defection was the Nationalist Citizens Party of India, a registered-unrecognised political entity founded on 14 October 2022 by Shewly Kundu in Howrah, West Bengal. Before June 2026, the NCPI had contested exactly one election — the 2023 Tripura Assembly polls — fielding candidates in four seats and collecting a combined 822 votes. Its total declared donations since inception amounted to Rs 1.13 lakh. Its official headquarters in Howrah's Sankrail area doubled as the residence of the Kundu family. Its Facebook page had roughly 90 followers.

What happened next was a carefully choreographed takeover. On 28 May 2026 — as the TMC rebellion was brewing — Shewly Kundu resigned as NCPI president. Three days later, on 31 May, Kakoli Ghosh Dastidar was elected president. The merger with the TMC rebel faction was announced on 14 June. The original NCPI organising secretary, Shantanu Dey, told reporters he had learned of the merger "through social media and news reports" — though he later did a U-turn and welcomed the development. Shewly Kundu told the Indian Express she did not know "who the new president is." By the end of that week, a political party with no elected representation in any legislature in the country had become the fifth-largest bloc in the Lok Sabha.

## The Legal Battle

The legality of the merger now rests with Speaker Om Birla, whose decision is expected before the Monsoon Session of Parliament, scheduled from 21 July to 21 August. The case hinges on Paragraph 4 of the Tenth Schedule of the Constitution — the "merger clause" of the anti-defection law — which exempts MPs from disqualification if two-thirds of a legislature party agrees to merge into another party.

The rebel MPs argue that 20 of 28 TMC Lok Sabha MPs — 71.4 per cent — easily clears the two-thirds threshold. "Om Birla verified all the signatures. There were 20 signatures. It is two-thirds now," Sudip Bandyopadhyay said. "In July, we will make a demand to give us Trinamool since we have two-thirds majority from Trinamool. Then the court will decide."

Abhishek Banerjee, who met the Speaker on 19 June and filed 20 disqualification petitions, argues otherwise. "We have left it to the Speaker's wisdom," he said. "If the Speaker functions as per the Constitution and Supreme Court orders, then these MPs should be disqualified."

The TMC's position rests on a key distinction drawn by the Supreme Court in the 2023 *Subhash Desai* judgment: the political party and its legislature party are distinct entities. Paragraph 4, experts argue, requires the *original political party* — the TMC at the organisational level — to merge with another party. Individual MPs cannot effect a merger on their own. "The party must merge first," Kapil Sibal has argued. "Two-thirds of the legislature party cannot merge with another party without the original party merging at the organisational level."

PDT Achary, former Lok Sabha Secretary General, concurs: "Only a political party can merge with another political party — not MPs or MLAs." Speaker Birla is understood to have sought the Law Ministry's opinion and will hear both factions before ruling. One former Election Commission officer described the rebels' plan as an "innovation" with no mention in either the anti-defection law or the Representation of the People Act.

## The Bigger Picture

The TMC's disintegration is not an isolated event. It is part of a broader realignment that has left the INDIA bloc in tatters. The DMK exited in early June after the Congress allied with the TVK in Tamil Nadu. The AAP had already left after seven of its ten Rajya Sabha MPs joined the BJP in April. The Shiv Sena (UBT) is on the verge of its own split, with six of its nine Lok Sabha MPs signalling a switch to the Shinde camp. The BJP has declared the INDIA bloc "dead and buried."

For the NDA, the arithmetic is improving but remains short of a two-thirds majority. The ruling coalition now commands roughly 313 seats in the 543-member Lok Sabha, up from 293 after absorbing the TMC rebels. The target for two-thirds — needed to pass bills such as the Delimitation Bill (the Constitution 131st Amendment), which was defeated in April 2026, or the "One Nation One Election" legislation — is 362. Even with potential gains from by-elections in three vacant seats and possible DMK support on specific bills, the NDA is still 17 to 49 seats short.

In the Rajya Sabha, the picture is more favourable: the NDA is approaching 154-155 seats against a two-thirds target of 163, with the opposition reduced to roughly 63 members. Congress leader Jairam Ramesh has accused Amit Shah of masterminding an "illegal breakaway" to engineer a two-thirds majority, pointing to a pattern of opposition splits — AAP in April, TMC in June, Shiv Sena (UBT) threatening to follow — as evidence of a coordinated strategy.

The story is still unfolding. The Speaker's ruling, the inevitable court challenge, and the looming Monsoon Session will determine whether the NCPI merger is remembered as a masterstroke of political engineering or a constitutional overreach. What is already clear is that on 14 June 2026, the landscape of Indian parliamentary politics shifted — and it may be a very long time before it settles.
