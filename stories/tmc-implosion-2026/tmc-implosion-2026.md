## Cold Open

On June 14, 2026, 20 parliamentarians walked into Lok Sabha Speaker Om Birla's official residence in New Delhi and calmly announced they were no longer members of the Trinamool Congress. In a single afternoon, the party that had dominated West Bengal politics for fifteen years and once commanded 29 Lok Sabha seats saw its parliamentary strength collapse to just eight MPs.

The defectors — led by four-term MP Kakoli Ghosh Dastidar — declared a merger with the Nationalist Citizens Party of India (NCPI), a registered-unrecognised political entity that had contested exactly one election, fielded candidates in four seats, and collected a combined 822 votes. Its total declared donations since inception amounted to ₹1.13 lakh. Its Facebook page had roughly 90 followers.

## The Backstory

The seeds of the TMC's implosion were sown in the West Bengal Assembly election of May 2026. For fifteen years, Mamata Banerjee had dominated Bengal politics, winning three consecutive terms. The 2026 election shattered that narrative: the BJP swept to power with 206 seats while the TMC was reduced to 80. Mamata herself lost her Bhabanipur constituency to Suvendu Adhikari, who was sworn in as Bengal's first BJP chief minister on May 9, 2026.

The scale of the defeat triggered an immediate leadership crisis. With Mamata no longer an MLA and her nephew Abhishek Banerjee increasingly seen as the de facto party chief, factional grievances erupted. "Was it Didi standing for election, or was it us?" rebel MP Arup Chakraborty later asked.

## The Assembly Rebellion

The first wave of rebellion hit the West Bengal Assembly. Within weeks of the election result, discontent among TMC legislators escalated into open revolt. On June 1, the party expelled MLAs Ritabrata Banerjee and Sandipan Saha. The expulsion backfired spectacularly.

Two days later, 58 of the TMC's 80 MLAs — 72 per cent of the legislature party — backed Ritabrata Banerjee as the Leader of the Opposition. Assembly Speaker Rathindra Bose accepted the claim, effectively recognising the rebel faction as the official opposition. By mid-June, Ritabrata claimed his support had swelled to 65 MLAs. The Calcutta High Court, which the TMC approached to challenge his LoP recognition, refused to stay the appointment on June 18, finding "no prima facie case."

## The Parliament Defection

If the Assembly rebellion was damaging, the Lok Sabha defection was catastrophic. On June 8 — the same day Mamata and Abhishek attended an INDIA bloc meeting in Delhi — 20 TMC Lok Sabha MPs wrote to Speaker Om Birla expressing their desire to align with the NDA. The group included actors-turned-politicians Satabdi Roy, Saayoni Ghosh, and Dev (Deepak Adhikari); cricketer Yusuf Pathan; and six-time MP Sudip Bandyopadhyay.

Of the TMC's original 28 Lok Sabha MPs, only eight remained loyal: Abhishek Banerjee, Mahua Moitra, Kalyan Banerjee, Saugata Roy, Kirti Azad, Shatrughan Sinha, Sajda Ahmed, and Pratima Mondal. The Rajya Sabha contingent also took a hit — four MPs including senior leader Sukhendu Sekhar Ray and former Union minister Sushmita Dev resigned from the party and the Upper House.

## The NCPI Vehicle

The vehicle for this defection was the Nationalist Citizens Party of India, founded on October 14, 2022, by Shewly Kundu in Howrah, West Bengal. Before June 2026, the NCPI had contested exactly one election — the 2023 Tripura Assembly polls — collecting 822 votes. Its headquarters doubled as the Kundu family residence.

What happened next was a carefully choreographed takeover. On May 28, 2026 — as the TMC rebellion was brewing — Shewly Kundu resigned as NCPI president. Three days later, Kakoli Ghosh Dastidar was elected president. The merger with the TMC rebel faction was announced on June 14. By the end of that week, a political party with no elected representation in any legislature in India had become the fifth-largest bloc in the Lok Sabha.

## The Legal Battle

The legality of the merger now rests with Speaker Om Birla, whose decision is expected before the Monsoon Session of Parliament (July 21 to August 21). The case hinges on Paragraph 4 of the Tenth Schedule of the Constitution — the "merger clause" of the anti-defection law — which exempts MPs from disqualification if two-thirds of a legislature party agrees to merge into another party.

The rebel MPs argue that 20 of 28 TMC Lok Sabha MPs — 71.4 per cent — easily clears the two-thirds threshold. Abhishek Banerjee, who met the Speaker on June 19 and filed 20 disqualification petitions, argues otherwise. The TMC's position rests on a distinction drawn by the Supreme Court in the 2023 *Subhash Desai* judgment: the political party and its legislature party are distinct entities. Paragraph 4, experts argue, requires the *original political party* — the TMC at the organisational level — to merge with another party.

"The party must merge first," Kapil Sibal has argued. "Two-thirds of the legislature party cannot merge with another party without the original party merging at the organisational level." PDT Achary, former Lok Sabha Secretary General, concurs: "Only a political party can merge with another political party — not MPs or MLAs."

## The Bigger Picture

The TMC's disintegration is part of a broader realignment that has left the INDIA bloc in tatters. The DMK exited in early June after the Congress allied with the TVK in Tamil Nadu. The AAP had already left after seven of its ten Rajya Sabha MPs joined the BJP in April. The Shiv Sena (UBT) is on the verge of its own split, with six of its nine Lok Sabha MPs signalling a switch.

For the NDA, the arithmetic is improving but remains short of a two-thirds majority. The ruling coalition now commands roughly 313 seats in the 543-member Lok Sabha, up from 293 after absorbing the TMC rebels. The target for two-thirds — needed for constitutional amendments such as the Delimitation Bill — is 362. In the Rajya Sabha, the NDA is approaching 154-155 seats against a two-thirds target of 163.

The Speaker's ruling, the inevitable court challenge, and the Monsoon Session will determine whether the NCPI merger is remembered as a masterstroke of political engineering or a constitutional overreach. What is already clear is that on June 14, 2026, the landscape of Indian parliamentary politics shifted — and it may be a very long time before it settles.
