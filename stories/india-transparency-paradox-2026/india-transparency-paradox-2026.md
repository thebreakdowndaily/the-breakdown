## What Happened

India's flagship transparency law, the Right to Information Act, completed 22 years on June 12, 2026. The milestone passed without parliamentary notice. Weeks earlier, the Corruption Perceptions Index 2025 released by Transparency International in February 2026 ranked India 91st out of 182 countries with a score of 39 out of 100 — statistically unchanged from the 38-41 range the country has occupied for over a decade.

**Verified Fact.** Source: Transparency International, Corruption Perceptions Index 2025, February 10, 2026; CIC Annual Report 2024-25. Confidence: 100.

The stagnation is not because nothing has changed. India has:
- Expanded its digital public infrastructure to cover over a billion people
- Seen the Supreme Court strike down the Electoral Bonds scheme as unconstitutional in February 2024
- Implemented faceless tax assessment and direct benefit transfers that reduce human discretion
- Passed the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023, which simultaneously strengthened privacy and weakened RTI's personal information exemption

Yet the perception of public sector corruption has not shifted. The reason, according to every major civil society assessment, is that the institutional architecture meant to enforce transparency — information commissions, the Lokpal, whistleblower protections — is in a state of systemic collapse.

**Independent Verification.** Source: Satark Nagrik Sangathan, Report Card on Information Commissions 2024-25, October 2025; BTI 2026 India Country Report; The Hindu, March 24, 2026. Confidence: 95.

## Why This Matters

Corruption perceptions are not an abstract ranking. They correlate with:
- **Foreign direct investment**: Countries with CPI scores below 50 receive disproportionately less FDI relative to GDP
- **Cost of capital**: Sovereign bond yields incorporate a corruption risk premium
- **Public service delivery**: Corruption diverts an estimated 1-3% of GDP annually from intended beneficiaries in India, according to various studies
- **Trust in democracy**: The 2026 Freedom House report rated India "Partly Free" — a score of 62/100 — noting that the legal framework for transparency "has been eroded in recent years"

**Verified Fact.** Source: Freedom House, Freedom in the World 2026; Transparency International CPI 2025 report; World Bank governance indicators. Confidence: 90.

At stake is not just India's international reputation but the quality of its democracy. The RTI Act was passed unanimously by Parliament in 2005 and hailed globally as a model for transparency legislation. Two decades later, the system it created is struggling to function.

### 4. Penalties: ₹453 Collected in a Year

The RTI Act empowers information commissions to impose penalties of up to ₹25,000 per case against public information officers who deny information without reasonable cause. In practice, this deterrent is barely used.

The CIC Annual Report for 2024-25 shows that penalties were imposed in just **1.2%** of all cases disposed of — a mere 1,252 instances. The total penalty amount collected, as recorded in the consolidated data table, was **₹453** — less than the cost of filing 45 RTI applications.

**Verified Fact.** Source: CIC Annual Report 2024-25, page 184; Counterview analysis, June 2026. Confidence: 100.

To put this in perspective:
- Ministry of External Affairs: ₹2 collected (₹1 each from two public authorities)
- Department of Personnel and Training: ₹79
- Coal Ministry: ₹17
- UT Delhi: ₹4
- MSME: ₹1

**Verified Fact.** Source: Counterview.net, June 1, 2026, analysing CIC annual report data tables. Confidence: 100.

### 5. Lokpal: 12 Years, No Inquiry Wing

The Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act was passed in December 2013 and came into force on January 16, 2014, following a decade-long anti-corruption movement. Twelve years later:

- **Inquiry Wing**: Director of Inquiry not yet appointed. The post exists on paper but remains unfilled despite 70 regular posts having been created and encadred.
- **Prosecution Wing**: Formally constituted on June 6, 2025 — 11.5 years after the Act. Still not fully operational; prosecutions handled through CBI.
- **Special Courts**: Under Section 35 of the Act, none have been notified. Cases are tried under the Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988 by special judges.
- **Staffing**: 24 of 65 sanctioned posts vacant (37% vacancy rate).
- **Complaints**: 389 registered in 2025-26 (up from 166 in 2023-24), with 336 disposed — but with no inquiry wing to investigate them substantively.

**Verified Fact.** Source: Parliamentary Standing Committee on Personnel, Public Grievances, Law and Justice, 160th Report, March 2026; Devdiscourse, March 25, 2026; The Hindu, March 22, 2026; The Wire, March 23, 2026. Confidence: 100.

The Lokpal chairperson position remained vacant from May 2022 before a new appointment was made. The BTI 2026 report described the anti-corruption body as having "proved to be a toothless organization," noting that no one had been prosecuted by the Lokpal as of March 2023.

**Independent Verification.** Source: BTI 2026 India Country Report, Bertelsmann Stiftung. Confidence: 90.

### 8. Journalist Safety: India Among Most Dangerous Countries for Corruption Reporting

The CPI 2025 report specifically listed India among countries "dangerous for journalists reporting on corruption." The report noted:

> "When journalists are attacked or killed for investigating corruption, power cannot be held to account effectively and corruption tends to worsen. Since 2012, in non-conflict zones worldwide, 829 journalists have been murdered. Over 90% of these killings happened in countries with a CPI score lower than 50, including in Brazil (35), India (39), Mexico (27), Pakistan (28) and Iraq (28)."

**Official Statement.** Source: Transparency International, CPI 2025 Report, February 2026. Confidence: 100.

In 2025, four journalists were killed in India, according to the International Federation of Journalists. The Free Speech Collective put the figure at eight for the calendar year. The most prominent was Mukesh Chandrakar, a freelance journalist in Chhattisgarh whose body was found in a septic tank on January 3, 2025 — allegedly murdered for reporting on irregularities in a road construction project.

**Verified Fact.** Source: Scroll.in, January 1, 2026; IFJ; Indian Express, January 4, 2025; CPJ, February 25, 2026. Confidence: 100.

Raghvendra Bajpai, a reporter and RTI activist in Uttar Pradesh, was shot dead in March 2025, with police confirming his recent reports on irregularities in paddy procurement and stamp duties were being investigated as a motive.

**Verified Fact.** Source: Times of India, March 9-10, 2025. Confidence: 100.

## The Debate

### The Government's Record

The government and its supporters point to several transparency achievements:

- **Electoral Bonds struck down**: The Supreme Court's February 2024 judgment striking down the anonymous political funding scheme was welcomed by transparency advocates. The Court held the scheme "violative of Article 19(1)(a)" — the right to information.
- **Digital governance**: Aadhaar, DBT, faceless income tax assessment, and the RBI Digital Payments Index (which rose to 516.76 in September 2025) have reduced human discretion and leakages.
- **Lokpal complaints rising**: 389 complaints registered in 2025-26, up from 166 in 2023-24, suggesting growing public engagement with the anti-corruption body.
- **RTI application volume**: Over 50 lakh applications in 2024-25 indicates the law remains widely used.
- **Whistleblowers Protection Act, 2014**: Provides a legal framework for whistleblower complaints, though critics argue it is limited in scope.
- **Prosecution wing constituted**: The Lokpal's prosecution wing was formally established in June 2025, after 11 years.

**Official Statement / Verified Fact.** Source: Lok Sabha statements by Union Minister Jitendra Singh, March 2026; Supreme Court judgment in ADR v. Union of India, February 15, 2024; PIB releases. Confidence: 100.

### The Critics' Case

Civil society organisations, opposition parties, and independent analysts raise systemic concerns:

**1. RTI has been systematically weakened**

- The 2019 amendments to the RTI Act ended the fixed five-year terms of information commissioners and removed their parity with Election Commissioners — reducing the independence of transparency bodies.
- The DPDP Act, 2023, through Section 44(3), amended RTI Section 8(1)(j) — removing the "unwarranted invasion of privacy" qualifier. The public interest carve-out remains but at a higher threshold, making it harder to access information.
- Fee revision (proposed ₹50 in 2024) was deferred after backlash but could be revived.
- The CIC has been headless 7 times in 11 years — always due to the government failing to appoint before a known retirement date.

**Expert Opinion.** Source: Frontline, October 14, 2025 — "RTI at 20: India's Transparency Law on Life Support"; BTI 2026 country report; Counterview, June 2026. Confidence: 95.

**2. Information Commissions are deliberately understaffed**

- Despite Supreme Court directions in Anjali Bhardwaj v. UoI (2019) that vacancies must be filled 1-2 months before they arise, not a single state complied fully.
- As of January 2026, the SC was still hearing a contempt-like petition on CIC vacancies, with the government having issued an advertisement but made no appointments.
- Uttar Pradesh — India's most populous state — had 7 of 11 commissioner posts vacant.

**Verified Fact.** Source: Supreme Court hearing records, February 2026; SNS Report Card; LawChakra, February 10, 2026. Confidence: 100.

**3. The Lokpal is structurally non-functional**

- "After some years of steady improvement on this issue, India's score on Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index declined again after 2019, falling from 41 to 39 in 2023. The Lokpal anti-corruption authority was formed in 2019 after a six-year delay. Critics say it has proved to be a toothless organization." — BTI 2026 India Country Report.
- A parliamentary committee in March 2026 asked the Lokpal to clarify its roadmap for making the inquiry and prosecution wings functional — 12 years after the Act was passed.

**Independent Verification.** Source: BTI 2026; Parliamentary Standing Committee 160th Report, March 2026. Confidence: 95.

**4. Journalist and activist killings with impunity**

- The Committee to Protect Journalists recorded India among countries that "persistently failed to secure justice for journalists' murders."
- RTI activists have been murdered with impunity. Dozens of RTI users and activists have been killed since the Act's introduction, and hundreds assaulted or harassed, according to Freedom House.

**Independent Verification.** Source: CPJ, February 25, 2026; Freedom House, Freedom in the World 2026. Confidence: 100.

Both the government and transparency advocates agree on certain points:

- Digital governance reduces corruption opportunities in service delivery
- The Electoral Bonds scheme was a setback for transparency
- India has the legal framework for strong anti-corruption institutions
- Implementation — not legislation — is the binding constraint

**Consensus Assessment.** Confidence: 90.

## The Bigger Picture

### The RTI Act: A Short History of Hope and Decline

The Right to Information Act was passed unanimously by both Houses of Parliament on May 11, 2005, and received presidential assent on June 21, 2005. It was the culmination of a grassroots movement led by the National Campaign for People's Right to Information (NCPRI), the Mazdoor Kisan Shakti Sangathan (MKSS), and activists like Aruna Roy and Anna Hazare.

**Historical Record.** Source: RTI Act, 2005; PRS India; historical accounts of the RTI movement. Confidence: 100.

The Act's architecture was radical for India:
- Any citizen could demand information from any public authority
- Time-bound response within 30 days (48 hours for life and liberty matters)
- Independent Information Commissions at Centre and state levels
- Penalties for non-compliance

For the first decade, the system worked reasonably well. Information Commissions were functional, penalties were occasionally imposed, and a culture of transparency began to take root. The Comptroller and Auditor General (CAG) used RTI-sourced information in its reports. Investigative journalists built stories on RTI-obtained documents.

The decline began around 2014-15, as administrative spending on MGNREGA was cut, the RTI Act was amended to reduce commissioner independence, and vacancies in Information Commissions went unfilled for longer periods.

**Expert Analysis.** Source: Drèze & Rahul R, "A Short History of MGNREGA" (administrative cost patterns); Frontline, October 2025; BTI 2026. Confidence: 90.

### The Economic Cost of Perceived Corruption

How much does a CPI score of 39 cost India? While precise quantification is difficult, multiple studies indicate:

- **Foreign direct investment**: Countries with CPI scores below 50 attract disproportionately less FDI. A 2018 IMF working paper found that a 1-point improvement in CPI correlates with a 0.5% increase in FDI inflows relative to GDP.
- **Sovereign borrowing**: The corruption premium in sovereign bond yields has been estimated at 20-50 basis points for countries in India's CPI range.
- **Domestic investment**: The World Bank's Enterprise Surveys consistently rank corruption among the top three constraints for Indian businesses.
- **Tax compliance**: Perceived corruption reduces voluntary tax compliance, creating a self-reinforcing cycle.

**Independent Verification.** Source: IMF Working Paper WP/18/227; World Bank Enterprise Surveys; Transparency International cost-of-corruption analysis. Confidence: 80.

### What Other Countries Did Right

Several countries have made measurable progress on the CPI since 2012:

- **Estonia**: Rose from 64 (2012) to 76 (2025) — digital governance + strong independent anti-corruption agency
- **South Korea**: Rose from 56 to 64 — sustained institutional reform after the 2016 presidential impeachment
- **Seychelles**: Rose from 52 to 71 — comprehensive anti-corruption strategy with international monitoring
- **Maldives**: Rose from 31 to 39 — institutional reforms after democratic transition

The common thread: sustained institutional investment, not episodic crackdowns.

**Independent Verification.** Source: Transparency International, CPI 2025 long-term trends analysis. Confidence: 85.

### The Political Economy of Transparency

India's transparency crisis is not a technical problem. It is a political one. Information Commissions are not underfunded by accident. The Lokpal is not dysfunctional through neglect. The RTI Act was not weakened by oversight. Each reflects a deliberate choice by the executive branch to reduce accountability mechanisms that could be used against it.

As the BTI 2026 report notes: "The fight against corruption and bureaucratic inefficiency has long been prominent on Modi's agenda. However, after some years of steady improvement on this issue, India's score on Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index declined again after 2019."

**Independent Verification.** Source: BTI 2026 India Country Report. Confidence: 90.

## What's Next

### Immediate (July-September 2026)

- **CIC appointments**: The Supreme Court continues to oversee the case on CIC vacancies. The next hearing could result in timelines for filling all 11 commissioners' posts.
- **RTI fee proposal**: The government's ₹50 application fee proposal could resurface. If implemented, it would represent a 500% increase in the cost of filing an RTI.
- **DPDP Rules implementation**: The full impact of the DPDP Act's amendment to RTI §8(1)(j) will become clearer as Information Commissions begin adjudicating cases under the new framework.

### Short Term (FY 2026-27)

- **Lokpal inquiry wing**: The parliamentary committee has demanded a roadmap within six months for operationalising the inquiry and prosecution wings. The government has not yet committed to a timeline.
- **State portal upgrades**: Pressure from the CIC and civil society may push more states toward functional RTI portals. The next SNS report card will measure progress.
- **Electoral Bonds aftermath**: The State Bank of India's disclosure of Electoral Bond data has provided the most detailed picture of political funding in Indian history. Analysis of this data will continue to shape the transparency debate.

### Medium Term (2027-2029)

- **CPI trajectory**: India's score is unlikely to move significantly unless institutional reforms are implemented. Historical data suggests even sustained reform takes 5-8 years to register in perception indices.
- **RTI Act reform**: Proposals for strengthening the RTI Act — including protecting commissioner independence, mandating time-bound appointments, and increasing penalty provisions — may gain legislative traction if civil society mobilisation continues.
- **Whistleblower protection**: The limited scope of the 2014 Act may be revisited, particularly given international pressure (the CPI report noted India as dangerous for journalists, a category that directly affects TI's scoring methodology).

### Long Term (2030+)

- **Generational change**: Younger Indians, who have grown up with RTI and digital governance, may demand higher standards of transparency. The CPI 2025 noted that "widespread frustration over weak governance and limited accountability triggered protests across several countries in 2025, particularly among younger populations."
- **Convergence with DPDP**: The tension between the Right to Information and the Right to Privacy (now codified in the DPDP Act) will need legislative resolution. The courts may play a defining role in balancing the two rights.
- **Viksit Bharat @2047**: The government's long-term vision document emphasises governance reform, but whether this translates into institutional strengthening of transparency bodies remains to be seen.
