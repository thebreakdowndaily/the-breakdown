India's Transparency Paradox: CPI Score Stagnant at 39 for a Decade While 4 Lakh RTI Appeals Pile Up

The Breakdown · July 1, 2026 · 18 min read · Editorial Intelligence Unit
A citizen files an RTI application at a public information office. The 2005 transparency law faces its deepest crisis in two decades. | Representational image
✓ FACT-CHECKED Every claim labeled with evidence type, source, date, and confidence score (0-100). Primary sources include Transparency International, CIC Annual Reports, Supreme Court records, parliamentary committee reports, and civil society audits.

India's anti-corruption watchdog collected Rs 453 in penalties last year. The law allows up to Rs 25,000 per case. There were 58,501 cases where information was denied. The gap between what the law permits and what the system delivers is the story of India's transparency paradox.

The Rs 453 is not an isolated data point. It is the predictable result of a transparency architecture that has been allowed to decay — 4.13 lakh RTI appeals pending, six state information commissions rendered defunct, a Lokpal without an inquiry wing 12 years after its Act was passed, and India classified among countries most dangerous for journalists reporting on corruption.

Yet digital governance expanded to cover a billion people. The Supreme Court struck down the Electoral Bonds scheme as unconstitutional. Faceless tax assessment and direct benefit transfers reduced human discretion in public programmes.

How can so much transparency progress coexist with so little measurable improvement in how corruption is perceived?

1. The Evidence

1.1 The CPI Score — 14 Years of Stagnation

Verified Fact Source: Transparency International CPI historical data 2012-2025. Confidence: 100

INDIA'S CPI SCORE: 14 YEARS OF STAGNATION
Corruption Perceptions Index (0-100) — 2012 to 2025

India's CPI score has fluctuated within a narrow 36-41 band for 14 years. Peak was 41 in 2018-19. Score in 2025: 39/100.

Over the same period, India's nominal GDP more than doubled from $1.83 trillion (2012) to approximately $4.15 trillion (2025). The disconnect between economic expansion and governance perception is the central paradox.

Verified Fact Source: IMF World Economic Outlook database; Government of India Economic Survey 2025-26. Confidence: 100

1.2 The RTI Backlog — 4.13 Lakh Appeals Pending

Verified Fact Source: SNS Report Card on Performance of Information Commissions 2024-25, October 2025. Confidence: 100

The Satark Nagrik Sangathan (SNS) Report Card on Information Commissions 2024-25 found total appeals/complaints pending across 29 Commissions at 4,13,972 as of June 30, 2025 — a 26% year-on-year increase from 3.21 lakh in 2023. The backlog in 2019 was 2.18 lakh. That is an 89% increase in 6 years.

1.3 Penalties — Rs 453 Collected in a Year

Verified Fact Source: CIC Annual Report 2024-25, page 184; Counterview analysis, June 2026. Confidence: 100

WHAT THE LAW ALLOWS

Rs 1.5 Cr

Maximum potential penalties at Rs 25,000 per case, applied to 58,501 denial cases

WHAT WAS COLLECTED

Rs 453

Actual penalty amount collected. That is a 0.003% enforcement rate.

Penalties were imposed in just 1.2% of all cases disposed of — a mere 1,252 instances. The implied deterrent value of the penalty provision — up to Rs 25,000 per denial, multiplied by 58,501 denial cases — would be nearly Rs 1.5 crore. The amount actually collected was Rs 453.

1.4 Lokpal — 12 Years, No Inquiry Wing

Verified Fact Source: Parliamentary Standing Committee 160th Report, March 2026. Confidence: 100

The Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act was passed in December 2013. Twelve years later: Inquiry Wing director not yet appointed; Prosecution Wing constituted June 2025 (11.5 years late); Special Courts under Section 35 of the Act — none notified; 24 of 65 sanctioned posts vacant (37% vacancy rate).

Independent Verification Source: BTI 2026 India Country Report, Bertelsmann Stiftung. Confidence: 90

The BTI 2026 report described the anti-corruption body as having "proved to be a toothless organization," noting that no one had been prosecuted by the Lokpal as of March 2023.

1.6 Journalist Safety — India Among Most Dangerous for Corruption Reporting

Official Statement Source: Transparency International, CPI 2025 Report, February 2026. Confidence: 100

The CPI 2025 report listed India among countries "dangerous for journalists reporting on corruption." In 2025, four journalists were killed in India (IFJ). The most prominent: Mukesh Chandrakar, a freelance journalist in Chhattisgarh whose body was found in a septic tank — allegedly murdered for reporting on irregularities in a road construction project.

3. The Debate

The Government's Record

The government and its supporters point to several transparency achievements:

  • Electoral Bonds struck down — The Supreme Court's February 2024 judgment striking down the anonymous political funding scheme was a transparency victory.
  • Digital governance — Aadhaar, DBT, faceless income tax assessment have reduced human discretion and leakages.
  • Lokpal complaints rising — 389 complaints registered in 2025-26, up from 166 in 2023-24.
  • RTI application volume — Over 50 lakh applications in 2024-25 indicates the law remains widely used.

The Critics' Case

  • RTI systematically weakened — 2019 amendments ended fixed five-year terms of commissioners. DPDP Act, 2023 amended RTI Section 8(1)(j), making it harder to access information. CIC headless 7 times in 11 years.
  • Information Commissions deliberately understaffed — Despite Supreme Court directions, not a single state complied fully. UP had 7 of 11 commissioner posts vacant.
  • Lokpal structurally non-functional — "A toothless organization" (BTI 2026). Parliamentary committee demanded roadmap for inquiry/prosecution wings — 12 years after the Act was passed.
  • Journalist killings with impunity — India among countries that "persistently failed to secure justice for journalists' murders" (CPJ).

Reform does not require new laws. It requires filling existing vacancies, funding existing institutions, and enforcing existing penalties. The political will to do so — or the lack of it — is what separates the government's record from its stated objectives.

4. The Bigger Picture

The RTI Act: A Short History of Hope and Decline

Historical Record Source: RTI Act, 2005; PRS India. Confidence: 100

The RTI Act was passed unanimously by both Houses of Parliament on May 11, 2005 — the culmination of a grassroots movement led by the National Campaign for People's Right to Information (NCPRI) and activists like Aruna Roy. For the first decade, the system worked reasonably well. The decline began around 2014-15, as administrative spending was cut, the RTI Act was amended to reduce commissioner independence, and vacancies went unfilled for longer periods.

The Economic Cost of Perceived Corruption

  • Foreign direct investment — Countries with CPI scores below 50 attract disproportionately less FDI. A 1-point CPI improvement correlates with a 0.5% increase in FDI inflows relative to GDP.
  • Sovereign borrowing — The corruption premium in sovereign bond yields estimated at 20-50 basis points for India's CPI range.
  • Tax compliance — Perceived corruption reduces voluntary tax compliance, creating a self-reinforcing cycle.

What Other Countries Did Right

  • Estonia — Rose from 64 (2012) to 76 (2025): digital governance + strong independent anti-corruption agency.
  • South Korea — Rose from 56 to 64: sustained institutional reform after the 2016 presidential impeachment.
  • Seychelles — Rose from 52 to 71: comprehensive anti-corruption strategy with international monitoring.

The common thread: sustained institutional investment, not episodic crackdowns.

THE BOTTOM LINE

India's transparency architecture is not broken by design — it is broken by neglect. The RTI Act, Lokpal Act, and Whistleblowers Protection Act together create one of the world's most comprehensive legal frameworks for accountability. But a framework without enforcement is a fiction. The 4.13 lakh pending appeals, the Rs 453 in penalties, the 12-year-old Lokpal without an inquiry wing — these are not failures of law. They are failures of political will. The Supreme Court's Electoral Bonds verdict showed that transparency is possible when institutions act. The question is whether the rest of the system will follow.
