India's Monsoon Crisis 2026

Historic June drought threatens Asia's third-largest economy

June 2026 closed as the third-driest June in 126 years — a 42% rainfall deficit that has crippled kharif sowing, drained reservoirs, and placed the Indian economy on inflation alert. With El Niño strengthening (Nino 3.4 at +1.7°C) and the India Meteorological Department forecasting below-average July rainfall, India faces a multi-dimensional crisis affecting 42% of its workforce, food prices, rural incomes, power generation, and macroeconomic stability.

Section 1

The Evidence

Every important statement in this report is labeled by type and assigned a confidence score. We separate verified facts from official statements, expert analysis, and claims.

Rainfall Deficit

Verified Fact

India recorded 92.1 mm of rainfall in June 2026 against the Long Period Average (LPA) of ~165 mm, a deficit of 42%. This makes June 2026 the third-driest June since records began in 1901. Only 2009 (87.5 mm) and 1905 (92.3 mm) were drier.

Source: IMD, Reuters — June 30, 2026
Confidence: 98%
Official Statement

IMD Director-General Mrutyunjay Mohapatra announced that July monsoon rainfall is forecast to be "below 94% of the LPA." The IMD had previously warned of a 60% probability of deficient seasonal rainfall.

Source: India Meteorological Department — June 30, 2026
Confidence: 100%
Verified Fact

State-wise rainfall deficits during the critical June 4–22 period: Maharashtra (-85%), Gujarat (-84%), Madhya Pradesh (-58%), Chhattisgarh (-71%), Jharkhand (-71%).

Source: IMD station data (36 meteorological subdivisions) — via India Today DIU
Confidence: 95%

Kharif Sowing

Verified Fact

Total kharif sowing area: 182.72 lakh hectares vs 236.46 lakh hectares YoY, a decline of 53.74 lakh hectares (-22.7%). Oilseeds saw the steepest decline at -53%, followed by cotton (-35%), rice (-25%), and pulses.

Source: Ministry of Agriculture via Moneycontrol — June 25, 2026
Confidence: 97%
Expert Analysis

"If rainfall picks up over the next few days, as forecast by the weather department, there will be enough soil moisture for farmers to begin sowing summer crops."

Source: Mumbai-based dealer at a global commodities trading house — via Reuters, June 30, 2026
Confidence: 80%

Reservoir Levels

Verified Fact

Live storage across 166 major reservoirs stood at 26.4% of capacity vs 36% last year. The southern region's reservoirs are at 20.8% vs 44.7% last year. Karnataka's reservoirs stand at just 14.7% vs 48.6% a year ago.

Source: Central Water Commission via Moneycontrol — June 30, 2026
Confidence: 95%

Food Prices

Verified Fact

Average retail tomato prices jumped 24% year-on-year to ₹43.7/kg. In Delhi's Azadpur market, wholesale prices soared 66% YoY to ₹2,700/quintal. Tomato inflation reached 48.43% in May 2026, contributing to a food inflation rate of 4.78%.

Source: Department of Consumer Affairs, Ministry of Statistics — via ET Agriculture, June 25, 2026
Confidence: 95%

Macroeconomic Impact

Official Statement

"Food outlook remains uncertain on account of subnormal southwest monsoon forecasts and El Niño risks. The projected deficiency from the southwest monsoon will have impact on agricultural products." — RBI Governor Sanjay Malhotra

Source: RBI MPC briefing — June 5, 2026
Confidence: 100%
Official Statement

The RBI retained the repo rate at 5.25%, projected FY27 inflation at 5.1%, and lowered GDP growth forecast to 6.6% from 6.9%, citing West Asia conflict risks alongside monsoon uncertainty.

Source: RBI MPC — June 5, 2026
Confidence: 100%

El Niño Conditions

Verified Fact

Nino 3.4 index climbed to approximately +1.7°C, well past the +0.5°C El Niño threshold. NOAA puts the chance of El Niño development over May-July at >80%.

Source: NOAA, India Today DIU — June 22, 2026
Confidence: 95%
Official Statement

The IMD's second-stage forecast (May 29) declared monsoon seasonal rainfall at 90% of LPA with ±4% model error, and an 84% probability of below-normal or less rainfall.

Source: PIB Press Release (PRID: 2266479) — May 29, 2026
Confidence: 100%

Section 3

The Debate

All significant viewpoints presented with evidence, counterarguments, and consensus levels. No false balance.

What caused the June drought?

Primary View El Niño as the main driver. Nino 3.4 at +1.7°C, well above threshold. Historical correlation between El Niño and weak Indian monsoons (e.g., 2009, 2014).
Counterargument Not all El Niño years produce drought. The 1997 super El Niño coincided with normal monsoon rainfall.
Consensus: High — El Niño is a significant but not sole factor

Is the government response adequate?

Official Position Early warnings were provided (Apr 15, May 29). Fuel restrictions imposed Jun 12. LPG price cut Jul 1.
Criticisms No formal drought declaration. No contingency crop plan. No additional MGNREGS allocation. MSP increases not adjusted for lower yields.
Consensus: Low — significant disagreement between government and independent analysts

Can July rescue the season?

Optimistic View "A weak June does not settle the year; much depends on how July recovers." (India Today) Former IMD DG KJ Ramesh: "Premature to panic" due to groundwater buffer from 2025.
Pessimistic View IMD's own July forecast is "below 94% of LPA." El Niño is strengthening. Reservoir deficits cannot be quickly replenished. Delayed sowing reduces yield potential.
Consensus: Low-Medium — July will be decisive but odds are tilted against rapid recovery
Section 4

The Bigger Picture

📜

Historical Context

June 2026 joins an elite group of failed monsoon starts. The three driest Junes on record — 2009, 1905, and 2026 — share one common feature: each was followed by significant economic disruption. The 2009 drought (22.4% seasonal deficit) triggered drought declarations in 252 districts, food inflation at 15%, and export bans. With a stronger El Niño than 2009 (Nino 3.4 at +1.7°C vs ~+1.0°C), the risk profile is elevated.

🌡️

Climate Change Link

The warming Indian Ocean (SSTs rising ~0.15°C/decade) is making the monsoon more volatile. Research from IITM Pune shows: monsoon rainfall is becoming more extreme when it arrives (higher intensity, shorter duration); the number of dry spells within the monsoon is increasing; and the predictability window for monsoon onset is narrowing.

📊

Economic Impact Channels

Agriculture (42% of workforce): Delayed/reduced kharif output → lower rural incomes → demand destruction in FMCG, tractors, two-wheelers. Food Inflation: Tomato prices already +24%. Pulses, edible oils, cereals will follow if July is weak. Food has 40% weight in CPI. Fiscal: Higher food subsidy, pressure on MGNREGS, potential tax revenue shortfall. External: Higher edible oil imports → wider CAD → rupee under pressure. West Asia supply disruption compounds energy costs.

🌏

International Comparison

El Niño's impact is global: Australia experiences drought/fire risk; Southeast Asia (Indonesia, Malaysia, Thailand) sees drier conditions; East Africa's food security crisis deepens; parts of South America experience rainfall deficits. India's crisis is part of a global climate disruption pattern amplified by record ocean heat.
