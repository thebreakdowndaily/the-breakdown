## The Debate

### What Caused the June Drought?

**1. El Niño as Primary Driver**
- *Evidence:* Nino 3.4 at +1.7°C, well above threshold. Historical correlation between El Niño and weak Indian monsoons (e.g., 2009, 2014).
- *Counterargument:* Not all El Niño years produce drought. 1997 had a strong El Niño but normal rainfall.
- *Consensus Level: High* — Most meteorologists agree El Niño is a significant but not sole factor.

**2. Absence of Supporting Systems**
- *Evidence:* IMD cites five technical reasons: weak Arabian Sea surge, weakened southwesterly winds, reduced cross-equatorial flow, absence of low-pressure systems, weak MJO phase. (Source: BusinessToday, June 23, 2026)
- *Counterargument:* These absences may themselves be caused or exacerbated by El Niño — they may be symptoms rather than independent causes.
- *Consensus Level: Moderate* — Technical factors are well-documented but causality chain remains debated.

**3. Climate Change as Amplifier**
- *Evidence:* Climate change is making the monsoon "harder to read, with more of the season's rain falling in fewer, heavier bursts" (India Today). Rising temperatures increase evaporation and atmospheric moisture-holding capacity.
- *Counterargument:* Attribution of a single month's deficit to climate change is scientifically imprecise. Natural variability remains large.
- *Consensus Level: Moderate* — Long-term trend well-established; monthly attribution uncertain.

### Is the Government Response Adequate?

**Official Position:**
- The IMD provided early warnings (April 15 first-stage forecast, May 29 downgrade)
- Fuel sale restrictions imposed June 12 (West Asia supply disruption)
- LPG cylinder price cut of ₹183.50 on July 1

**Criticisms:**
- No formal drought declaration in affected states
- No contingency crop plan announced for delayed kharif season
- Kharif MSP increases have not been adjusted for likely lower yields
- No additional allocation for MGNREGS to absorb rural distress

**Consensus Level: Low** — Significant disagreement between government communications and independent analysts.

### Can July Rescue the Season?

*Optimistic view:* "A weak June does not settle the year; much depends on how July recovers" (India Today). If rainfall picks up, soil moisture will support late sowing. Former IMD DG KJ Ramesh: "It would be premature to panic."

*Pessimistic view:* IMD's own July forecast is "below 94% of LPA." With El Niño strengthening, the window for recovery is narrow. Reservoir deficits cannot be quickly replenished. Delayed sowing reduces yield potential for all major crops.

*Consensus Level: Low-Medium* — July will be decisive but odds are tilted against rapid recovery.

## The Bigger Picture

### Historical Context

June 2026 joins an elite group of failed monsoon starts. The three driest Junes on record — 2009, 1905, and 2026 — share one common feature: each was followed by significant economic disruption.

- **2009 drought:** 22.4% seasonal deficit, triggered drought declaration in 252 districts, food inflation surged to 15%, government imposed export bans on rice and sugar. The RBI cut rates aggressively.
- **1905 drought:** Pre-independence, but contributed to agricultural distress and policy changes in irrigation investment.
- **2026 trajectory:** With a stronger El Niño than 2009 (Nino 3.4 at +1.7°C vs ~+1.0°C in 2009), the risk profile is elevated.

### Climate Change Link

The warming Indian Ocean (SSTs rising ~0.15°C/decade) and changing atmospheric circulation patterns are making the monsoon more volatile. Research from IITM Pune shows:
- Monsoon rainfall is becoming more extreme when it arrives (higher intensity, shorter duration)
- The number of dry spells within the monsoon season is increasing
- The predictability window for monsoon onset is narrowing

### Economic Impact Channels

**1. Agriculture (42% of workforce)**
- Delayed/reduced kharif output → lower rural incomes → demand destruction in FMCG, tractors, two-wheelers
- Higher input costs (farmers who sowed early may need to replant)
- Soybean and cotton shortfall will increase import dependence

**2. Food Inflation**
- Tomato prices already +24%. Pulses, edible oils, and cereals will follow if July is weak
- Food has 40% weight in CPI basket. RBI's 5.1% inflation projection may prove optimistic
- Rate cut trajectory (repo currently at 5.25%) will be delayed

**3. Fiscal**
- Higher food subsidy bill if government expands free grain distribution
- Pressure to increase MGNREGS allocation
- Potential tax revenue shortfall if rural economy weakens

**4. External Sector**
- Higher edible oil and soybean imports will widen current account deficit
- Rupee under pressure (already ₹86-87/$ range)
- West Asia supply disruption (June 12 fuel restrictions) compounds energy costs

### International Comparison

- **Australia:** La Niña → flooding (opposite ENSO phase)
- **Southeast Asia:** El Niño typically brings drier conditions across Indonesia, Malaysia, Thailand
- **East Africa:** Drier conditions from El Niño compound existing food security crises
- **South America:** Parts of Brazil and Argentina experience rainfall deficits during El Niño
