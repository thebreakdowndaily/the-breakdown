# US-Iran Peace Deal 2026: Full 14-Point Agreement Explained

## Cold Open

On June 17, President Donald Trump sat down at the Palace of Versailles with French President Emmanuel Macron and signed a two-page memorandum of understanding with Iran. The document ended the most expensive four months of American warfare since the Iraq invasion â€” and started a 60-day countdown to negotiate a permanent peace. The agreement followed a devastating conflict that began with Operation Epic Fury on February 22, 2026, when the United States struck IRGC facilities and imposed a naval blockade on the Strait of Hormuz. By the time the MOU was signed, the war had cost an estimated $80 billion, with the Pentagon spending roughly $1.88 billion per day on military operations.

## The Deal

The Versailles Accord is a 14-point agreement that includes an immediate ceasefire on all fronts, including Lebanon; the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz with free passage guaranteed for 60 days; the lifting of the US naval blockade within 30 days; immediate waiver of oil sanctions for 60 days; a schedule for the termination of all remaining sanctions; and the establishment of a $300 billion reconstruction and development fund for Iran. Critically, Iran reaffirmed its commitment not to develop nuclear weapons and agreed to down-blend all enriched uranium on site under the supervision of the International Atomic Energy Agency. The 60-day window is intended to allow for negotiations on a permanent peace agreement, with Vice President JD Vance leading the American negotiating team.

## Key Provisions

The agreement represents a carefully calibrated exchange of concessions. The United States gained an immediate ceasefire, the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, a firm commitment from Iran on nuclear non-proliferation, and IAEA supervision of enrichment activities. What Washington did not secure was any concession on Iran's ballistic missile programme, which remains a point of contention. Iran obtained significant upfront concessions â€” sanctions relief, the lifting of the naval blockade, a $300 billion reconstruction fund, and a defined path towards the full termination of sanctions. What Tehran did not achieve was the immediate and complete removal of all sanctions, which remain subject to the 60-day negotiation timeline.

## Regional Reactions

The response across the region has been mixed. Pakistan Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif, who mediated the initial ceasefire framework on June 13, welcomed the agreement as a diplomatic breakthrough. Gulf states, heavily reliant on the free flow of oil through the Strait of Hormuz, expressed cautious optimism. Iran's Supreme Leader Ayatollah Khamenei endorsed direct talks on June 18, a critical political signal that gave the MOU domestic legitimacy within Iran. European Union sanctions on Iran remain in place, however, creating a layer of complexity that the 14-point agreement does not directly address. The United States' European allies have indicated they will maintain their own sanctions regime pending further negotiations.

## Implementation Challenges

Within days of the signing, significant disagreements have emerged. The IAEA and Iran are at odds over the schedule for inspections. The status of approximately $12 billion in frozen Iranian assets remains disputed. The timeline for down-blending enriched uranium is yet to be mutually agreed upon. EU sanctions, which were not part of the US-Iran MOU, remain a barrier to full economic normalisation. The 60-day timeline is exceptionally tight, and the history of US-Iran negotiations suggests that every clause will be contested. The Pentagon has requested $80 billion to cover war costs, a figure that contrasts sharply with the $300 billion reconstruction fund offered to Iran in the same document.

## What's Next

The Versailles Accord is not a peace deal â€” it is an agreement to try to reach one. Technical talks began on June 23, with the US waiving Iran oil sanctions for the 60-day period. The 60-day clock is ticking, and the coming weeks will determine whether this becomes Trump's Camp David or his Munich. By the August 17 deadline, the world will know whether the Versailles Accord was the beginning of a historic realignment or the prelude to renewed conflict. The stakes could not be higher: a permanent deal would reshape the Middle East, while failure would likely mean a return to hostilities with even greater intensity.
