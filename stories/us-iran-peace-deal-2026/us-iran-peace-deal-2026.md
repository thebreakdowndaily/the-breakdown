## Cold Open

On June 17, at the Palace of Versailles, President Donald Trump signed a two-page memorandum of understanding with Iran — ending four months of conflict that began with Operation Epic Fury on February 22, 2026. The war cost an estimated $80 billion, roughly $1.88 billion per day. The MOU established an immediate ceasefire, reopened the Strait of Hormuz, waived oil sanctions for 60 days, and created a $300 billion reconstruction fund for Iran. It also set a 60-day deadline to negotiate a permanent peace. By August 17, the world will know whether Versailles marks a historic realignment in the Middle East or the prelude to renewed hostilities.

## The Deal

The Versailles Accord contains 14 provisions: an immediate ceasefire on all fronts including Lebanon; reopening of the Strait of Hormuz with free passage guaranteed for 60 days; lifting of the US naval blockade within 30 days; immediate waiver of oil sanctions for 60 days; a schedule for termination of all remaining sanctions; and a $300 billion reconstruction and development fund for Iran. Iran reaffirmed its commitment not to develop nuclear weapons and agreed to down-blend all enriched uranium on site under IAEA supervision. Vice President JD Vance leads the American negotiating team for the permanent agreement.

## Key Provisions

The United States gained an immediate ceasefire, Strait of Hormuz reopening, nuclear non-proliferation commitment, and IAEA supervision of enrichment. It did not secure any concession on Iran's ballistic missile programme, which remains a point of contention. Iran obtained sanctions relief, the lifting of the naval blockade, a $300 billion reconstruction fund, and a defined path towards full sanctions termination. It did not achieve immediate and complete removal of all sanctions, which remain subject to the 60-day negotiation timeline. The $300 billion reconstruction fund contrasts sharply with the $80 billion the Pentagon has requested to cover war costs.

## Regional Reactions

Pakistan Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif, who mediated the initial ceasefire framework on June 13, welcomed the agreement. Gulf states, heavily reliant on oil transit through the Strait of Hormuz, expressed cautious optimism. Iran's Supreme Leader Ayatollah Khamenei endorsed direct talks on June 18, granting the MOU domestic legitimacy within Iran. European Union sanctions on Iran remain in place, however — a layer of complexity the 14-point agreement does not directly address. US European allies have indicated they will maintain their own sanctions regime pending further negotiations.

## Implementation Challenges

Within days of signing, significant disagreements have emerged. The IAEA and Iran differ on the inspection schedule. The status of approximately $12 billion in frozen Iranian assets remains disputed. The timeline for down-blending enriched uranium is yet to be mutually agreed. EU sanctions remain a barrier to full economic normalisation. The 60-day timeline is exceptionally tight, and the history of US-Iran negotiations suggests every clause will be contested.

## What's Next

The Versailles Accord is not a peace deal — it is an agreement to try to reach one. Technical talks began on June 23, with the US waiving Iran oil sanctions for the 60-day period. By the August 17 deadline, the world will know whether this becomes Trump's Camp David or his Munich. A permanent deal would reshape the Middle East. Failure would likely mean a return to hostilities with even greater intensity.
