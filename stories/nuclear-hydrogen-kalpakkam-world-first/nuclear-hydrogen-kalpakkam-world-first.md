## Cold Open

For decades, the hydrogen economy has been trapped in a paradox. The fuel burns clean, but producing it has been expensive, inefficient, or carbon-intensive. Green hydrogen from renewable electricity depends on weather. Grey hydrogen from natural gas emits roughly ten tonnes of CO₂ for every tonne produced — over 900 million tonnes of CO₂ annually, comparable to Germany's total emissions.

On June 26, 2026, India opened a third path. The Department of Atomic Energy commissioned the world's first hydrogen production facility that uses nuclear reactor heat — not electricity, not fossil fuels — to split water. The Copper-Chlorine (Cu-Cl) thermochemical plant, integrated with the Fast Breeder Test Reactor (FBTR) at the Indira Gandhi Centre for Atomic Research in Kalpakkam, Tamil Nadu, produces zero emissions, operates 24 hours a day, and is entirely independent of weather conditions.

## The Problem: Hydrogen's Dirty Secret

The International Energy Agency projects global hydrogen demand will reach 530 million tonnes by 2050, up from roughly 95 million tonnes today. Hydrogen is essential for decarbonising steelmaking, fertiliser production, oil refining, shipping, and aviation — sectors where electrification is difficult or impossible.

Yet nearly all hydrogen produced today comes from fossil fuels. Steam methane reforming releases roughly ten tonnes of CO₂ per tonne of hydrogen. Green hydrogen via electrolysis avoids these emissions but costs two to three times more than grey hydrogen, depends on weather, and requires vast land for solar panels or wind turbines. The world has been searching for a third path.

## The Discovery: A Chemical Loop Driven by Nuclear Heat

The Cu-Cl thermochemical cycle — developed indigenously by the Bhabha Atomic Research Centre in Mumbai — offers that third path. Instead of using electricity to split water, it uses high-temperature heat to drive a closed-loop sequence of chemical reactions involving copper and chlorine compounds. Water goes in; hydrogen and oxygen come out. The copper and chlorine are recycled infinitely.

The cycle operates at a maximum temperature of around 530°C — significantly lower than the 850°C or more required by alternative thermochemical cycles such as the sulfur-iodine process. The electrolysis step within the Cu-Cl cycle requires just 0.5 to 1.0 volts, less than half the voltage of conventional water electrolysis.

What makes the Kalpakkam facility a world first is the source of that heat. The FBTR provides process heat directly to drive the chemical reactions, bypassing the need to convert heat to electricity first. This direct thermal pathway is inherently more efficient than any electrolysis-based system.

## The Science: How It Works

The Cu-Cl thermochemical cycle involves four main steps. **Step 1 — Hydrogen Production:** Copper metal reacts with dry hydrogen chloride gas at 430–475°C, producing hydrogen gas and copper chloride. **Step 2 — Hydrolysis:** Copper chloride reacts with superheated steam at around 400°C to form copper oxychloride, regenerating HCl gas for reuse. **Step 3 — Oxygen Production:** Copper oxychloride is heated to about 500°C using process heat from the FBTR, releasing oxygen gas and producing cuprous chloride. **Step 4 — Electrochemical Regeneration:** Cuprous chloride undergoes low-voltage electrolysis at 0.5–1.0 volts to regenerate copper metal and copper chloride, completing the closed cycle.

Water is the net input. Hydrogen and oxygen are the net outputs. Heat — not fossil fuels, not electricity — drives the reaction. The only by-product is oxygen. Unlike solar or wind-based hydrogen production, the nuclear-powered system operates around the clock, 365 days a year.

## What's Next: From Demonstrator to Scale

The pilot plant is designed to validate the technology and generate operational data for scale-up. Researchers at BARC and IGCAR will study the Cu-Cl process under continuous conditions, optimise reaction parameters, and identify engineering solutions for commercial deployment.

The next phase involves integrating nuclear hydrogen production with advanced reactor systems now under development. The PFBR at Kalpakkam — India's first 500 MWe commercial fast breeder reactor — is nearing grid synchronisation after achieving first criticality in April 2026. Future fast reactors could co-produce electricity and hydrogen, improving overall economics. Further ahead, FBTR-II — a 100-120 MWth advanced test reactor — is in conceptual design, with green hydrogen production identified as a secondary objective alongside isotope production and materials testing.

## The Bigger Picture: Bhabha's Blueprint, 75 Years On

The Kalpakkam hydrogen facility is the latest expression of a vision Homi Bhabha articulated in the 1950s. India's three-stage nuclear programme was designed around a fundamental constraint: the country possesses roughly 2 per cent of the world's uranium but around 25 per cent of its thorium reserves. Stage 1 — pressurised heavy water reactors using natural uranium — produces plutonium as a by-product. Stage 2 — fast breeder reactors like the FBTR — use that plutonium to breed more fuel and prepare the ground for Stage 3 — thorium-based reactors that would give India virtually unlimited clean energy.

The hydrogen facility expands this framework. It demonstrates that fast breeder reactors can produce not just electricity and bred fuel but also industrial products essential for decarbonisation. It turns a 40-year-old test reactor into a multi-purpose clean energy platform — validating the proposition that energy security, technological self-reliance, and climate action are convergent, not competing, priorities.
