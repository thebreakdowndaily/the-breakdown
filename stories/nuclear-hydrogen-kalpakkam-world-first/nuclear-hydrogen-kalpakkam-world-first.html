🇮🇳 INCREDIBLE INDIA

The World's First Nuclear-Powered Hydrogen Plant Is in India. Here's Why That Matters.

KALPAKKAM, Tamil Nadu — For decades, the hydrogen economy has been trapped in a paradox. The fuel burns clean, but making it clean has been expensive and inefficient. Green hydrogen, produced by splitting water with renewable electricity, is weather-dependent. Grey hydrogen, made from natural gas, defeats the purpose.

On June 26, 2026, India walked through a door no other country has opened. The Department of Atomic Energy (DAE) commissioned the world's first hydrogen production facility that replaces electricity with nuclear reactor heat — a Copper-Chlorine (Cu-Cl) thermochemical plant integrated with the Fast Breeder Test Reactor (FBTR) at the Indira Gandhi Centre for Atomic Research (IGCAR) in Kalpakkam, Tamil Nadu.

Unlike every other hydrogen plant on the planet, this one does not use electrolysis. It does not burn fossil fuels. It uses the heat of a nuclear reactor to drive a sequence of chemical reactions that split water into hydrogen and oxygen — producing zero greenhouse gas emissions, operating 24 hours a day, independent of the weather.

The Problem: Hydrogen's Dirty Secret

Hydrogen is often called the fuel of the future. The International Energy Agency projects global hydrogen demand reaching 530 million tonnes by 2050, up from around 95 million tonnes today. It is essential for decarbonising industries where electrification is difficult or impossible — steelmaking, fertiliser production, oil refining, shipping, and aviation.

But there is a catch. Nearly all hydrogen produced today comes from fossil fuels. Steam methane reforming — the dominant method — uses natural gas and releases roughly 10 tonnes of carbon dioxide for every tonne of hydrogen produced. That adds up to over 900 million tonnes of CO₂ annually, comparable to the total emissions of Germany.

Green hydrogen, produced via water electrolysis powered by solar or wind, avoids these emissions but introduces new problems. It is roughly two to three times more expensive than grey hydrogen. It depends on weather conditions. And it requires vast amounts of land for solar panels or wind turbines to generate the electricity needed for electrolysis.

The world has been searching for a third path.

The Discovery: A Chemical Loop Driven by Nuclear Heat

The Cu-Cl thermochemical cycle — developed indigenously by the Bhabha Atomic Research Centre (BARC) in Mumbai — offers that third path.

The process is elegant in its chemistry. Instead of using electricity to split water, it uses high-temperature heat to drive a closed-loop sequence of chemical reactions involving copper and chlorine compounds. Water goes in; hydrogen and oxygen come out. The copper and chlorine are recycled infinitely.

The cycle operates at a maximum temperature of around 530°C — significantly lower than the 850°C or more required by alternative thermochemical cycles such as the sulfur-iodine process. The electrolysis step within the Cu-Cl cycle requires just 0.5 to 1.0 volts, less than half the voltage of conventional water electrolysis.

What makes the Kalpakkam facility a world first is the source of that heat. The FBTR — India's only operational fast breeder test reactor — provides the process heat directly, bypassing the need to first convert heat to electricity and then use that electricity for electrolysis. This direct thermal pathway is inherently more efficient.

The Journey: Four Decades to a World First

The story of this hydrogen plant begins not in 2026, but in 1971, when IGCAR was established in Kalpakkam, 80 kilometres south of Chennai. The centre's mission: develop the technology of sodium-cooled fast reactors, the second stage of India's ambitious three-stage nuclear programme conceived by physicist Homi J. Bhabha.

The FBTR, based on the French Rapsodie design but significantly modified by Indian engineers, achieved first criticality on October 18, 1985 — making India the seventh nation in the world to build and operate a breeder reactor.

The early years were anything but smooth. France withdrew its offer of enriched uranium after India's 1974 nuclear test, forcing Indian scientists at BARC and IGCAR to develop an entirely new mixed carbide fuel — a 70:30 blend of plutonium carbide and uranium carbide — that had never been used in any reactor anywhere in the world. The reactor suffered shutdowns, sodium leaks, and technical setbacks. Between 1987 and 1989, the FBTR was almost entirely offline.

But Indian scientists persisted. The mixed carbide fuel outperformed all expectations, reaching a burn-up of 165 GWd/t without a single fuel pin failure — a world record for this fuel type. The reactor was progressively upgraded, reaching its full design power of 40 MWt in March 2022, more than 36 years after first criticality.

Over four decades, the FBTR generated the operational data, materials knowledge, and engineering confidence that would eventually lead to two parallel achievements: the 500 MW Prototype Fast Breeder Reactor (PFBR), now nearing grid synchronisation at the same site, and the world's first nuclear heat-based hydrogen facility.

Nuclear power, with its unique ability to provide reliable carbon-free electricity as well as high-temperature process heat, is ideally suited to support large-scale hydrogen production while contributing to India's energy security, decarbonisation goals, and long-term sustainable development objectives."

The Science: How It Works

The Cu-Cl thermochemical cycle involves four main steps:

  • Step 1 — Hydrogen Production: Copper metal (Cu) reacts with dry hydrogen chloride (HCl) gas at 430–475°C, producing hydrogen gas (H₂) and copper chloride (CuCl).
  • Step 2 — Hydrolysis: Copper chloride reacts with superheated steam at around 400°C to form copper oxychloride (Cu₂OCl₂), regenerating HCl gas for reuse.
  • Step 3 — Oxygen Production: Copper oxychloride is heated to about 500°C using process heat from the FBTR, releasing oxygen gas (O₂) and producing cuprous chloride (CuCl).
  • Step 4 — Electrochemical Regeneration: Cuprous chloride undergoes low-voltage electrolysis at 0.5–1.0 volts to regenerate copper metal and copper chloride, completing the closed cycle.

The entire process is continuous. Water is the net input. Hydrogen and oxygen are the net outputs. Heat — not fossil fuels, not electricity — drives the reaction. The only by-product is oxygen.

Unlike solar or wind-based hydrogen production, which stops when the sun doesn't shine or the wind doesn't blow, this nuclear-powered system can operate around the clock, 365 days a year.

The Impact: Beyond the Laboratory

The Kalpakkam pilot plant is small — a demonstrator, not a commercial facility. But its implications extend across multiple sectors.

Decarbonising Industry: Clean hydrogen can replace the fossil-fuel-based grey hydrogen currently used in steelmaking, oil refining, and chemical manufacturing. India is the world's second-largest steel producer, and each tonne of steel made via the traditional blast furnace route emits nearly two tonnes of CO₂. Switching to hydrogen-based direct reduced iron (DRI) could eliminate those emissions.

Green Fertiliser: Hydrogen is the essential feedstock for ammonia production, which in turn is used to manufacture urea fertiliser. India consumes roughly 35 million tonnes of urea annually, nearly all of it produced using natural gas-derived hydrogen. On the same day the hydrogen plant was inaugurated, the government unveiled a roadmap to procure 7.24 lakh metric tonnes of green ammonia annually under the National Green Hydrogen Mission — creating a direct demand pipeline for nuclear-generated hydrogen.

Energy Storage: Hydrogen can store energy at scales that batteries cannot match, making it a natural complement to India's rapidly expanding renewable energy capacity. Nuclear-produced hydrogen could serve as a strategic reserve, providing round-the-clock clean power when solar and wind generation dip.

Heavy Transport: Hydrogen fuel cells are emerging as a zero-emission alternative for shipping, aviation, and long-haul trucking — sectors that account for a growing share of global emissions and where battery electrification remains impractical.

The DAE stated that the facility represents "a significant step towards AtmaNirbhar Bharat [Self-Reliant India] through the convergence of nuclear energy and clean hydrogen technologies."

What's Next: From Demonstrator to Scale

The pilot plant is designed to validate the technology and generate the operational data needed for scale-up. Researchers at BARC and IGCAR will study the Cu-Cl process under continuous operating conditions, optimise reaction parameters, and identify engineering solutions for commercial deployment.

The next phase involves integrating nuclear hydrogen production with advanced reactor systems now under development. The PFBR at Kalpakkam — India's first 500 MWe commercial fast breeder reactor — is nearing grid synchronisation after achieving first criticality in April 2026. Future fast reactors could potentially co-produce electricity and hydrogen, improving overall economics.

Further ahead, FBTR-II — a 100-120 MWth advanced test reactor — is in the conceptual design phase, with green hydrogen production identified as one of its secondary objectives alongside isotope production and materials testing.

The Bigger Picture: Bhabha's Blueprint, 75 Years On

The Kalpakkam hydrogen facility is the latest expression of a vision that Homi Bhabha articulated in the 1950s. India's three-stage nuclear programme was designed to work around a fundamental constraint: the country possesses roughly 2 percent of the world's uranium but around 25 percent of its thorium reserves.

Stage 1 — pressurised heavy water reactors using natural uranium — produces plutonium as a by-product. Stage 2 — fast breeder reactors like the FBTR — use that plutonium to breed more fuel and prepare the ground for Stage 3 — thorium-based reactors that would give India virtually unlimited clean energy from its abundant thorium reserves.

The hydrogen facility expands this framework. It demonstrates that India's fast breeder reactors can produce not just electricity and bred fuel, but also industrial products essential for decarbonisation. It turns a 40-year-old test reactor into a multi-purpose clean energy platform.

In doing so, it validates a broader proposition: that energy security, technological self-reliance, and climate action are not competing priorities. They are convergent — and India is proving it with hardware running on the Tamil Nadu coast.
