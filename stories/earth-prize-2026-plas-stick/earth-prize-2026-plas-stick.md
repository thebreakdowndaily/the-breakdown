# Plas-Stick: Three Indian teens win Earth Prize 2026 with tamarind seed powder that removes microplastics from water

Three 16-year-olds from Jaipur, Rajasthan — Vivaan Chhawchharia, Ariana Agarwal and Avyana Mehta — have won the Global Winner title of The Earth Prize 2026 for Plas-Stick, a biodegradable magnetic powder made from discarded tamarind seeds that binds microplastic particles in water for easy removal with a handheld magnet. The process requires no electricity, no filters, and no complex infrastructure.

The team is the first ever from India to win the global title. They were selected through a public vote of approximately 23,000 participants, beating six other Regional Winners from across the world. The Earth Prize is the world's largest environmental sustainability competition for students aged 13–19, run by the Swiss-based Earth Foundation.

## What happened

Plas-Stick was named the Asia Regional Winner of The Earth Prize 2026 on 14 May, receiving a $12,500 grant to scale the solution. On 29 May, it was announced as the Global Winner after a week-long public vote. Official figures: 6,372 entries from 107 countries, narrowed to 35 top teams, then seven Regional Winners (one per continent), and finally one Global Winner by popular vote.

**Verified Fact** — Source: The Earth Prize official website and press release, 29 May 2026. Confidence: 100.

## How it works

Tamarind seeds are a waste product of the food industry — discarded by the tonne after the pulp is harvested for cooking. The team developed a process to modify the seed powder and combine it with iron oxide, giving it magnetic properties and an electrostatic attraction to plastic particles.

When added to contaminated water, the powder binds to suspended microplastics, forming visible clumps. A handheld magnet placed against the container wall draws the clumps out. The recovered material can then be upcycled into small useful items — the team has demonstrated tiles and coasters — preventing the plastic from re-entering the environment.

**Verified Fact** — Source: The Earth Prize official solution description; multiple news reports (NDTV, Mongabay, Economic Times). Confidence: 95.

The team described the origin of the idea in a statement:

> "We came up with the solution by first being very clear about the problem we wanted to solve. Once that was clear, we started looking for materials that were already available around us. Tamarind seeds stood out because they are often treated as waste, but they have natural binding properties."

**Official Statement** — Source: The Earth Prize press release, 29 May 2026; quoted in Mongabay India, 25 May 2026. Confidence: 100.

## The inspiration

The project began after the team visited a rural community where drinking water was stored in shared plastic containers without advanced filtration systems. During the visit, they observed a child drinking from one of these containers — a moment they later cited as the catalyst for their work.

Globally, an estimated 2.2 billion people lack safely managed drinking water infrastructure (WHO/UNICEF, 2023), increasing reliance on stored water that may contain microplastics.

**Verified Fact** — Source: Multiple news outlets (NDTV, Economic Times, Mongabay); Earth Prize press release. Confidence: 95.

## What Plas-Stick is not (yet)

Multiple news reports and the team's own statements make clear that Plas-Stick has not yet undergone large-scale independent scientific testing. The Economic Times, citing the team, noted that "further validation and testing would be required before the solution can be implemented on a wider scale."

Some viral social media posts incorrectly claimed each student received $125,000 individually. Official records confirm the team collectively received $12,500 as the Asia Regional Winner.

## The bigger picture

Microplastic pollution is a rapidly growing global concern. Primary microplastics (beads, abrasives) and secondary microplastics (fragments from bottles, bags, nets, synthetic textiles) accumulate in water bodies, food chains, and human tissue. Studies have detected microplastics in Arctic ice, Himalayan rainfall, and human blood and lungs.

Existing removal technologies rely on membrane filtration, which requires pumps, pressure vessels, and electricity — infrastructure that is scarce in the communities most affected by water contamination. The Plas-Stick approach is notable for substituting material chemistry for energy infrastructure.

The Earth Foundation, which runs The Earth Prize, has now reached over 21,000 students across 169 countries and territories over five competition cycles. Previous winners have been featured in Forbes, BBC, Financial Times, and Business Insider, with several teams advancing to patents, partnerships, and commercial pilots.

**Verified Fact** — Source: The Earth Foundation / Earth Prize impact data, 2026. Confidence: 95.

## What's next

The team plans to use the $12,500 Regional Winner grant to scale Plas-Stick through decentralised production hubs and expand into rural communities across India. Their stated target: grow awareness from approximately 8,000 people reached to 35,000–40,000 by the end of 2026.

The Global Winner title also comes with a sustainably crafted "100-year flag" handsewn from recycled sailboat sails, to be flown at their school and passed on to future winners.

**The team's own estimate**: "By the end of 2026, we hope to grow from educating 8,000 people about it to around 35,000–40,000."

**Official Statement** — Source: The Earth Prize press release, 29 May 2026; Mongabay India interview. Confidence: 100.

## Key questions that remain

- **Efficacy at scale**: Plas-Stick has not been tested independently at scale. Its real-world removal efficiency across different water types and microplastic compositions is not yet established.
- **Production viability**: Scaling from laboratory prototype to decentralised production hubs requires process engineering, quality control, and supply chain logistics that have not been detailed.
- **Cost per treatment**: The team has not published cost projections. The $12,500 grant is modest for the stated ambition of reaching tens of thousands of people.
- **Regulatory pathway**: Water treatment products require regulatory approval in most jurisdictions. No timeline for this process has been disclosed.
