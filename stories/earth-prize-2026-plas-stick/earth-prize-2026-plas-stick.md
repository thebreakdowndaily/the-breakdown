## Cold Open

An estimated 14 million tonnes of plastic enter the world's oceans each year. Microplastics — particles under five millimetres — have been detected in Arctic ice cores, in rainfall over the Himalayas, and in human blood and lung tissue. Conventional filtration removes some of these particles but requires pumps, pressure, and a reliable electricity supply — all of which remain out of reach for over two billion people who lack safely managed drinking water.

Three 16-year-olds from Mumbai just demonstrated a fundamentally different approach. Vivaan Chhawchharia, Ariana Agarwal, and Avyana Mehta developed Plas-Stick, a magnetic powder made from discarded tamarind seeds that binds to microplastic particles and is removed with a simple magnet. The invention won The Earth Prize 2026 — the world's largest youth sustainability competition — where it was selected from 6,372 entries across 107 countries.

## The Problem With Microplastics

Microplastics originate from two sources: primary particles such as the beads used in cosmetics and industrial abrasives, and secondary particles from the fragmentation of larger plastic items like bottles, bags, and fishing nets. Once suspended in water, they are extraordinarily difficult to remove at scale.

Existing filtration systems rely on membrane technology with microscopic pores. These systems work but demand pumps, pressure vessels, and continuous electricity. A 2023 study estimated that two billion people already lack safely managed drinking water; microplastic contamination compounds this crisis, and energy-intensive filtration is rarely a viable solution for the communities most affected.

## How Plas-Stick Works

Tamarind seeds are a waste product of the food industry, discarded in bulk after the pulp is harvested. The team discovered that tamarind seed powder, when chemically modified and combined with iron oxide, becomes magnetic and develops a strong electrostatic attraction to plastic particles.

When added to contaminated water, the powder binds to suspended microplastics. A standard magnet placed against the container wall draws the bound particles out, leaving clean water behind. The process requires no pumps, no filters, no electricity. The powder can be recovered and reused multiple times. The first prototype removed over 80 per cent of microplastics from a test sample.

"It started as a school science project," the team said at the Earth Prize ceremony. "We wanted to solve something that felt impossible. Microplastics seemed impossible. But nature already had the answer — we just had to find it."

## The Team Behind the Innovation

All three students are in Class XI at different Mumbai schools. They met at a summer science programme two years ago and began working on weekends in a borrowed laboratory. Vivaan focused on material chemistry and surface modification of the tamarind seed powder. Ariana managed environmental impact assessment and toxicity testing. Avyana designed the magnetic recovery system and scaling experiments.

The Earth Prize judging panel — composed of scientists, entrepreneurs, and environmental leaders — evaluated submissions on scientific merit, feasibility, scalability, and environmental impact. Plas-Stick scored highest across all four categories. The prize includes $100,000, which the team will use for patent filings, pilot trials, and partnerships with water treatment facilities in rural Rajasthan.

## What Comes Next

The teenagers are now working with researchers at the Indian Institute of Technology Bombay to optimise binding efficiency and test against different microplastic types — including synthetic textile fibres that are among the most common pollutants in Indian rivers. They are also developing a low-cost field kit for community water testing centres that requires no specialised training.

If the next round of trials succeeds, the team hopes to begin small-scale manufacturing within 18 months, targeting a cost of under ₹50 per treatment for a household's daily water needs. "This prize isn't the finish line," Avyana said. "It's the fuel."
