# Three Indian Teens Won the Earth Prize 2026 — With a Tamarind Powder That Removes Microplastics From Water

## Cold Open

Every year, an estimated 14 million tonnes of plastic end up in the world's oceans. Microplastics â€” particles smaller than five millimetres â€” have been found in Arctic ice, in the rain falling over remote mountain ranges, and inside human blood and lungs. Conventional water filtration can remove some of them, but the equipment is expensive, energy-intensive, and often inaccessible to the communities that need it most.

Three 16-year-olds from India just proved there is a cheaper way. Vivaan Chhawchharia, Ariana Agarwal and Avyana Mehta have developed Plas-Stick, a magnetic powder made from discarded tamarind seeds that pulls microplastics out of water without any electricity. The invention won them The Earth Prize 2026, the world's largest youth sustainability competition, where they beat more than 6,000 entries from over 100 countries.

## The Problem With Microplastics

Microplastics come from two primary sources: primary microplastics, such as the tiny beads used in cosmetics and industrial abrasives, and secondary microplastics, which result from the breakdown of larger plastic items like bottles, bags and fishing nets. Once they enter waterways, they are nearly impossible to remove at scale.

Existing filtration technologies rely on membranes with microscopic pores. These systems work but require pumps, pressure and a reliable electricity supply â€” none of which can be taken for granted in large parts of the developing world. A 2023 study estimated that more than 2 billion people lack access to safely managed drinking water, and for many of them, microplastic contamination makes the problem worse.

## How Plas-Stick Works

The three teenagers stumbled onto their solution in a place familiar to anyone who has spent time in western India: the tamarind tree. Tamarind seeds are a waste product of the food industry, discarded by the tonne after the pulp is harvested for cooking. The team discovered that the seed powder, when chemically modified and combined with iron oxide, gains a remarkable property â€” it becomes magnetic and develops a strong electrostatic attraction to plastic particles.

When added to contaminated water, the powder binds to suspended microplastics. A simple magnet placed against the side of the container draws the bound particles out, leaving clean water behind. The entire process requires no pumps, no filters, and no electricity. The powder itself can be reused multiple times before it needs to be replaced.

"It started as a school science project," the team said during their acceptance speech at The Earth Prize ceremony. "We wanted to solve something that felt impossible. Microplastics seemed impossible. But nature already had the answer â€” we just had to find it."

## The Team Behind the Innovation

All three students are in Class XI at different schools in Mumbai. They met at a summer science programme two years ago and decided to collaborate on environmental research. What began as weekend experiments in a borrowed laboratory quickly turned into something far more serious when their first prototype removed more than 80 per cent of microplastics from a test sample.

The team's complementary skills proved critical. Vivaan focused on material chemistry and the surface modification of the tamarind seed powder. Ariana handled the environmental impact assessment and toxicity testing. Avyana led the design of the magnetic recovery system and the scaling experiments. Their division of labour, they say, mirrored the way professional research teams operate.

## The Earth Prize 2026

The Earth Prize is an annual global competition run by The Earth Foundation, a Swiss-based non-profit organisation dedicated to nurturing environmental entrepreneurship among teenagers. The 2026 edition drew a record 6,372 entries from 107 countries.

The judging panel, composed of scientists, entrepreneurs and environmental leaders, evaluated submissions on scientific merit, feasibility, scalability and potential environmental impact. Plas-Stick scored highest across all four categories. The prize includes a monetary award of $100,000, which the team plans to use for patent filings, pilot trials and partnerships with water treatment facilities in rural Rajasthan.

"The innovation is elegant precisely because it is so simple," one judge noted in the official prize citation. "It uses a waste material to solve a waste problem, and it does so without adding to the energy burden of the communities it is meant to serve."

## What Comes Next

The teenagers are already working on the next phase. They are collaborating with researchers at the Indian Institute of Technology Bombay to optimise the powder's binding efficiency and to test it against different types of microplastics â€” including fibres from synthetic textiles, which are among the most common pollutants in Indian rivers.

They are also developing a low-cost field kit that community water testing centres can use without specialised training.
