Three Indian Teens Invented a Tamarind Powder That Removes Microplastics From Water — and Won the World's Largest Environmental Prize

Vivaan Chhawchharia, Ariana Agarwal and Avyana Mehta — all 16 — turned waste tamarind seeds into a magnetic powder that cleans water without electricity. Their invention, Plas-Stick, won The Earth Prize 2026, beating 6,000+ entries from students across 100+ countries. They are the first Indian team ever to win the global title.

In a rented classroom in Jaipur, three 16-year-olds watched a video about microplastics in the school's drinking water. Most students moved on to the next lesson. Vivaan Chhawchharia, Ariana Agarwal and Avyana Mehta did not. Eighteen months later, they stood on a stage in Geneva, accepting the world's largest environmental prize for teenagers — the first Indian team ever to do so.

Their invention, Plas-Stick, is strikingly simple: a powder made from waste tamarind seeds that, when mixed into contaminated water, binds microplastic particles into visible clumps that can be removed with a handheld magnet. No electricity. No expensive filters. No complicated machinery. Just agricultural waste and a magnet.

The Earth Prize 2026, founded by the Earth Foundation in Geneva, received over 6,000 applications from students across more than 100 countries. Plas-Stick was named the Asia Regional Winner in May, earning $12,500 in funding. Then, on May 29, a public vote of approximately 23,000 people selected it as the Global Winner — the first Indian team to claim the top prize in the competition's five-year history.

At a Glance

Team: Vivaan Chhawchharia, Ariana Agarwal, Avyana Mehta (all 16)
School: Jayshree Periwal International School, Jaipur, Rajasthan
Invention: Plas-Stick — biodegradable magnetic powder from waste tamarind seeds that removes microplastics from water
Award: Global Winner, The Earth Prize 2026 (first Indian team to win)
Prize: $12,500 + sustainably crafted '100-year' flag + global recognition
Competition: 6,000+ entries, 100+ countries, 7 regional winners, ~23,000 public votes

The Problem They Set Out to Solve

Microplastic contamination in drinking water is one of the fastest-growing environmental health concerns worldwide. These particles — smaller than 5 millimetres — come from the degradation of plastic waste, synthetic clothing fibres, tyre dust, and industrial pellets. They have been found in human blood, placentas, lungs, and breast milk.

In India, the problem is acute. A 2025 study commissioned by the Delhi government found microplastics in groundwater samples from 11 of 13 districts. Reverse osmosis purifiers can remove some particles, but they are expensive — costing anywhere from ₹8,000 to ₹25,000 — require electricity, and waste significant amounts of water. For millions of rural Indian households that rely on shared community storage tanks or untreated groundwater, advanced filtration is simply not an option.

"We visited rural communities where families stored drinking water in large shared containers," the team told The Hindu in an interview. "There was no access to advanced filtration systems. That's when we realised the problem wasn't just about technology — it was about accessibility."

The Invention: Plas-Stick

Plas-Stick emerged from a classroom discussion on environmental science at Jayshree Periwal International School in Jaipur. The students — then 15 — began experimenting with agricultural waste products, testing which natural materials could bind to plastic particles in water.

Tamarind seeds, it turns out, contain natural binding compounds — polysaccharides and proteins — that attract and adhere to plastic particles. The team discovered that by drying, grinding, and processing tamarind seeds into a fine powder, they could create a biodegradable flocculant that causes microplastics to cluster together.

But the real breakthrough came when they added a magnetic element. By incorporating iron oxide nanoparticles into the tamarind powder, the clumps of microplastic could be pulled out of the water using nothing more than a handheld magnet — no filtration, no electricity, no complex equipment.

How Plas-Stick Works

Step 1: Waste tamarind seeds are cleaned, dried, and ground into a fine powder.
Step 2: The powder is processed with iron oxide nanoparticles to make it magnetically responsive.
Step 3: The powder is added to contaminated water and stirred. Within minutes, it binds microplastic particles into visible clumps.
Step 4: A handheld magnet is passed through the water — the clumps adhere to the magnet and are removed.
Step 5: Clean water remains, with no electricity, filters, or ongoing costs.

The innovation is notable not just for its effectiveness, but for its use of a waste product. India produces approximately 250,000 tonnes of tamarind annually, primarily in Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, Andhra Pradesh, and Karnataka. The seeds — which make up about 30-40% of the fruit by weight — are typically discarded. Plas-Stick transforms this agricultural waste into a water purification tool.

How It Works, in Detail

The science behind Plas-Stick draws on established principles of flocculation — a process used in water treatment plants worldwide, where chemicals are added to cause suspended particles to clump together for easier removal. What makes Plas-Stick different is its use of entirely biodegradable, food-grade materials and its magnetic removal mechanism.

The tamarind seed powder acts as a natural flocculant. The polysaccharides in the seeds — primarily xyloglucan — have a high affinity for synthetic polymers like polyethylene and polypropylene, which are the most common types of microplastics found in water. When the powder is added to water and agitated, these polysaccharides bind to the plastic particles, forming increasingly large aggregates.

The iron oxide nanoparticles — which are themselves food-grade and commonly used in medical imaging — make these aggregates magnetically responsive. Once the microplastic-tamarind clumps have formed, they can be collected with a standard neodymium magnet, similar to the magnets used in hard drives or speaker systems.

The team emphasises that the entire process is designed to work without electricity, without trained operators, and without ongoing consumable costs — making it viable for the rural and underserved communities that inspired the project in the first place.

The Journey From Classroom to Geneva

The Earth Prize competition runs on a structured timeline. Registrations close in January, submissions in February, and judging takes place over the following months. In 2026, the competition received its highest-ever number of entries — over 6,000 — from students in more than 100 countries.

Plas-Stick was selected as one of 35 top teams, then as one of seven Regional Winners — one from each continent. The Asia Regional Winner announcement came on May 14, 2026, earning the team $12,500 in funding and a place in the public vote for the Global Winner title.

"Being named the Global Winners of The Earth Prize is incredibly special for all of us, especially as the first team from India to receive this recognition," the trio said in a statement following the May 29 award ceremony in Geneva. "What started as an idea between students has now been recognised among thousands of projects from around the world, which feels both surreal and deeply motivating."

The award ceremony also recognised their mentor, teacher Minal Jain, who guided the team through the research, experimentation, and competition process. "Our parents supported us massively," Avyana Mehta told The Hindu. "They weren't taking over the work, but we had their moral support whenever we needed it."

The Earth Prize 2026: The Full Picture

The Earth Prize, founded by the Earth Foundation in Geneva during the 2019 School Strike for Climate movement, describes itself as "the world's largest environmental sustainability competition for students aged 13-19." It offers $100,000 in total prize money each year and positions itself not just as a competition, but as an "ideas incubator" — providing mentorship, learning content, and ambassador networks to participants.

The seven Regional Winners for 2026 represented every inhabited continent:

  • Asia: Plas-Stick (India) — tamarind-based microplastic removal from water
  • Africa: Hewasafi Innovators (Kenya) — low-cost vehicle exhaust filter using maize, coconut, and algae
  • Europe: Eco-Purge (Ireland) — biodegradable plastic that removes existing microplastics as it breaks down
  • Middle East: (winner details not yet published)
  • North America: SargaTex PR (Puerto Rico) — biodegradable fabric from invasive sargassum seaweed
  • Central & South America: HADA (Brazil) — biodegradable bandages using aloe vera and chamomile
  • Oceania & Southeast Asia: Homes for Hornbills (Thailand) — upcycled plastic bottle nests for hornbill conservation

Each Regional Winner received $12,500 in prize funding. The Global Winner receives the additional honour of a sustainably crafted '100-year' flag, handsewn from recycled sailboat sails, to be flown at their school — a tradition that passes from each year's winners to the next.

Peter McGarry, Founder of The Earth Foundation, said Plas-Stick was exactly the kind of invention the prize was created to elevate. "By transforming agricultural waste into a practical tool for removing microplastics from drinking water, these young innovators are addressing a growing global challenge with remarkable creativity and purpose."

What's Next for Plas-Stick

The team plans to use the prize funding to scale Plas-Stick through decentralised production hubs in rural India. The model is deliberately low-tech: tamarind seeds are abundant across India, and the processing — cleaning, drying, grinding — can be done locally with minimal equipment.

"Plas-Stick was designed to be simple, affordable, and accessible," the team shared after their Asia win. "This support allows us to take it beyond pilot schools and scale it to many more communities."

The immediate priorities are:

  • Pilot expansions: Moving from school-based demonstrations to community-level trials in rural Rajasthan and Madhya Pradesh
  • Independent testing: Engaging with academic institutions for large-scale validation of efficacy and safety
  • Production decentralisation: Developing a model where local communities can process tamarind seeds into Plas-Stick powder themselves
  • Partnerships: Working with NGOs, gram panchayats, and state water missions to integrate Plas-Stick into existing drinking water programmes

It is important to note that Plas-Stick is still under development and has not yet undergone large-scale independent scientific testing. The team is transparent about this — their published materials emphasise that further validation is required before the solution can be deployed at scale. The Earth Prize funding is intended, in part, to support that validation process.

The Broader Picture: Microplastics in India

Plas-Stick arrives at a moment when concern over microplastic contamination in India is intensifying. Research published in 2025 and 2026 has documented microplastics in:

  • Groundwater samples from 11 of 13 Delhi districts (Government of Delhi study, 2025)
  • Bottled water brands sold across Indian cities (multiple studies)
  • Salt products from the Gujarat and Tamil Nadu coasts
  • Marine life in the Bay of Bengal and Arabian Sea
  • Agricultural soil irrigated with treated wastewater

The health effects of chronic microplastic ingestion remain an active area of research. However, studies have documented that microplastics can carry toxic additives, adsorb heavy metals and pathogens, and cause inflammatory responses in human tissue. The World Health Organization has called for more research into the health impacts while noting that microplastic contamination is "a global concern that requires urgent action."

India does not currently have regulatory standards for microplastics in drinking water. The Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) has not yet amended the IS 10500 drinking water specification to include microplastic limits, though discussions are reportedly underway following the Delhi groundwater findings.

Why This Matters

The Plas-Stick story is, on one level, a heartening tale of teenage ingenuity: three students saw a problem, experimented in a classroom, and created a solution that earned global recognition. But it's also a reminder of how far India has to go on water quality — and how much of the innovation in solving that gap is coming from outside the formal system.

India's water crisis is usually discussed in terms of quantity — depletion of aquifers, erratic monsoons, drying rivers. The microplastic dimension adds a quality crisis that existing infrastructure is not designed to address. Centralised water treatment plants, where they exist, were not built to remove particles smaller than 5 millimetres. At-home RO systems are too expensive for most households. The gap between the problem and the available solutions is enormous.

What Plas-Stick demonstrates is that low-cost, decentralised, locally-produced solutions have a role to play — particularly when they build on materials that are already available in the communities they aim to serve. Tamarind grows across India. Magnets are cheap. The processing is simple. The model is, by design, replicable.

None of this means Plas-Stick will solve India's microplastic problem on its own. Large-scale validation, regulatory approval, manufacturing scale-up, and distribution logistics remain significant hurdles. But the recognition from The Earth Prize — chosen by 23,000 voters from seven global finalists — is a signal that the world is paying attention to solutions that prioritise accessibility alongside efficacy.

For three teenagers from Jaipur, that signal is also a responsibility. "We hope this not only helps us scale Plas-Stick further, but also encourages more young people to believe that their ideas can create meaningful environmental change," the team said.

If their idea works at scale, it won't just be meaningful. It will be transformative.

The Bottom Line

Three 16-year-olds from Jaipur won the world's largest environmental prize for teenagers by turning waste tamarind seeds into a magnetic powder that removes microplastics from water. Plas-Stick is the first Indian entry to win The Earth Prize globally. The solution is still in development and requires independent validation, but its emphasis on low-cost, electricity-free, decentralised water treatment points toward a model that could serve the communities that need it most.
