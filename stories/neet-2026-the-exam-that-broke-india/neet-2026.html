NEET 2026: The Exam That Broke India

A PDF on WhatsApp, 410 leaked questions, 13 CBI arrests, and 22 lakh students caught between two exams. The inside story of how India’s biggest medical entrance test collapsed — and what it reveals about a system that failed the very aspirants it was meant to serve.

The Lede

On May 3, 2026, more than 22 lakh students sat for the NEET-UG examination — India’s gateway to medical education and, for millions of families, the closest thing to a guaranteed future. Nine days later, the government cancelled the entire exam.

The trigger: a PDF file circulating on WhatsApp in the coaching belt of Sikar, Rajasthan. It contained 410 questions. Of those, at least 120 matched the actual NEET paper in Chemistry and Biology. The paper had been in circulation approximately 45 hours before the exam began.

The cancellation set off a chain of events that would expose a syndicate inside the testing agency itself, trigger a CBI investigation with 49 searches and 13 arrests, force a re-test under the heaviest security ever deployed for an Indian examination, and leave 22 lakh students trapped between two exams, two sets of results, and one broken system.

The question source was inside NTA itself — experts appointed by the agency to set and translate papers were the leak point.

Context: A System Under Strain

The NEET-UG is not just an exam. It is the largest medical entrance test in the world, growing from 8 lakh applicants in 2016 to over 24 lakh in 2024. The number of MBBS seats has not kept pace. In 2025, 22.09 lakh students appeared for approximately 1.1 lakh MBBS seats — a ratio of roughly 20 candidates per seat.

The stakes are existential. Medical education in India is dominated by private colleges where annual fees can exceed ₹20 lakh. A government medical seat — costing ₹10,000–₹50,000 per year — is the only financially viable path for most families. NEET scores determine who gets those seats.

This pressure has created a parallel economy. Coaching centres charge upwards of ₹3 lakh for two-year programs. In Kota alone, an estimated 2 lakh students live away from home, studying 14-hour days. When the system fails, it does not fail abstractly — it fails specific students who have mortgaged their families’ savings on a single exam.

The Institutional Failure

The NEET-UG 2026 leak is not the story of a lone hacker or a rogue printing press operator. It is the story of a system where the guardians of the exam were themselves the source of the breach.

The CBI investigation revealed that three individuals appointed by the NTA as experts to set and translate question papers — Manisha Mandhare (senior botany teacher), P.V. Kulkarni (retired chemistry teacher), and Manisha Havaldar (physics teacher) — had complete access to the final question sets. They shared questions with associates, who then distributed them through a network spanning Pune, Nashik, Gurugram, and Jaipur.

A BAMS student in Nashik received a hard copy of the paper from Pune. A father in Rajasthan paid ₹10–12 lakh for his son’s access. A salon owner in Pune facilitated logistics. A doctor in Latur connected students to the leaked chemistry questions. The network was not sophisticated — it was human, exploiting a system with insufficient controls at every level.

The Security Aftermath

The re-examination on June 21 was held under what the NTA called “unprecedented security.” The numbers are striking: 1,38,560 CCTV cameras, 51,311 signal jammers, 48,448 biometric verification staff, 38,795 frisking personnel, and roughly 7 lakh officials mobilised across 5,440 centres. The exam was conducted in 13 languages. A nationwide mock drill was held the day before.

The NTA declared the re-test a success, dismissing a viral video alleging a fresh leak as “fake” and warning of legal action. But the human cost was visible at centre gates across India.

What Both Sides Are Missing

What the Government Sees

The NTA’s position: the system worked. A whistleblower complaint led to detection, cancellation, CBI investigation, 13 arrests, and a re-test within 37 days — a logistical feat involving 7 lakh personnel. AI-based CCTV monitoring, Aadhaar-authenticated biometric verification, and real-time command centres at the Ministry of Education level represent genuine institutional upgrades. PM Modi reportedly delayed his airport departure to avoid traffic inconveniences for students. The message: the government treats NEET integrity as a top-tier priority.

What the Students Saw

Students experienced something different. At centres in Bengaluru, Mumbai, Bhopal, Hyderabad, and Delhi, candidates were denied entry for arriving minutes after the 1:30 PM cutoff — some due to Google Maps routing them to wrong locations, others caught in traffic. A student in Mumbai reached at 1:31 PM, one minute late, and was turned away. Video footage showed aspirants attempting to climb gates.

In Ajmer, 18-year-old Kulsum Bano was told to remove her burqa to enter — despite having worn it during the May 3 exam without issue. She refused: “The exam doesn’t matter to me; what matters is my burqa and my identity.” In Ahmedabad, students were made to remove religious threads. The NTA later claimed the burqa issue was “resolved” — after the exam had started.

“If such security arrangements had been in place the last time, the chances of a paper leak would have been lower, and students would not have had to face so much trouble.”

— NEET aspirant Sonia, after the re-exam

What the Data Reveals

The loss of trust is quantifiable. NEET registrations dropped from 24,06,079 in 2024 to 22,76,069 in 2025 — a decline of 1,30,010, breaking a nine-year growth streak. While 2026 saw a marginal recovery (+3,000), the 2024 paper leak and subsequent grace marks controversy had already eroded confidence. The 2026 repeat has crystallised a view among aspirants that the system is structurally compromised.

What To Watch Next

1. CBI final report: A chargesheet is expected within 60 days. The agency is investigating whether the same syndicate operated the 2024 NEET leak — which would indicate a systemic, not isolated, failure.

2. Supreme Court petitions: Multiple PILs seek NTA’s dissolution or a statutory overhaul. The court’s response could reshape India’s entire examination architecture.

3. NEET 2026 results: Expected in July. Normalization of scores across the original May 3 and re-test June 21 cycles will be technically and politically contentious.

4. Exam reform: The government is reportedly considering a shift to Computer-Based Testing (CBT) for NEET — a move that could reduce but not eliminate leak risks. The deeper question is whether any technology can secure an exam when the people setting it are the leak point.

The Bottom Line

The Verdict

The NEET-UG 2026 episode is not a story of a single leak. It is the story of a system where the guardians of the exam — NTA-appointed experts — were themselves the source of the breach. Where a father could purchase his son’s medical career for ₹10 lakh. Where 22 lakh students must pin their futures on an agency that has now experienced major paper leaks in consecutive cycles (2024 and 2026).

The re-test was conducted. The CBI made arrests. But the underlying question remains: if the question paper can be leaked while experts are setting it, before it even reaches a printing press, what security measure can possibly prevent the next breach?

The answer — whether a transition to computer-based testing, a statutory overhaul of the NTA, or a fundamental re-examination of the single-exam model — will determine the fate not just of future medical aspirants, but of the idea that high-stakes examinations in India can be fair.
