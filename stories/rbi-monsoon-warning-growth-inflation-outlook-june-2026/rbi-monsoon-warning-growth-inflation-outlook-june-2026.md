## Cold Open

The Reserve Bank of India's June 2026 bulletin carries a stark warning: an adverse south-west monsoon could significantly worsen India's growth-inflation calculus. The bulletin comes on the heels of the Monetary Policy Committee's June 5 decision to cut the GDP growth forecast while raising inflation projections, even as it held the repo rate steady.

## The Warning

The RBI's analysis highlights that the monsoon shortfall is not merely a seasonal Aberration but a structural risk to the economic outlook. A below-normal monsoon could push food inflation above 6 per cent in the coming quarters, the bulletin warns, with potential second-round effects on wages and broader inflation expectations. The central bank cautioned that sustained price pressures in cereals, vegetables, pulses, and edible oils could lead to a "generalisation of inflation through second-round effects on expectations and wages." Reservoir storage levels have also fallen below the decadal average, raising concerns about Rabi sowing and water availability for industry.

## Growth Outlook

The MPC on June 5 revised its GDP growth forecast for FY27 downwards to 6.6 per cent from the earlier projection of 6.9 per cent. The bulletin projects Q2 as the weakest quarter at 6.3 per cent, with a gradual recovery in the second half of the financial year. The downward revision reflects multiple headwinds: weakening rural demand due to monsoon deficiency, elevated global oil prices, and geopolitical uncertainties stemming from ongoing conflicts. The RBI noted that private consumption, which accounts for nearly 60 per cent of GDP, could face additional pressure if food inflation erodes household purchasing power. Industrial output growth has also shown signs of moderation.

## Inflation Outlook

CPI inflation is now projected at 5.1 per cent for FY27, up sharply from the previous estimate of 4.6 per cent. The bulletin expects inflation to peak at 5.9 per cent in Q3 before moderating. Actual CPI inflation rose to 3.9 per cent in May from 3.5 per cent in April, driven by food price pressures. Core inflation, which excludes volatile food and fuel components, is projected at 4.7 per cent for the full year. The RBI's warning of second-round effects is particularly significant: if food price increases feed into wage demands and broader price-setting behaviour, inflation could prove stickier than currently anticipated, complicating the MPC's policy path.

## Policy Response

The MPC kept the repo rate unchanged at 5.25 per cent with a neutral stance, signalling a wait-and-watch approach. Governor Sanjay Malhotra emphasised that the central bank remains committed to aligning inflation with the 4 per cent target while supporting growth. The RBI's forex reserves, standing at a robust $682.3 billion, provide a comfortable buffer against external shocks, but the central bank acknowledged that the monsoon outcome will be a critical input for future policy decisions. Market participants now expect the MPC to remain on hold through the August review, with a potential rate cut in October if inflation moderates and the monsoon situation improves. The government, for its part, has begun contingency planning, including accelerated distribution of subsidised food grains and enhanced procurement operations to stabilise prices.
