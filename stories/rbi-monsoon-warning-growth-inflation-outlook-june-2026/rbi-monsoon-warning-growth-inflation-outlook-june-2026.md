## Cold Open

India's rainfall deficit hit 42.2 per cent below the long-period average on June 21 — nearly double the 28.4 per cent recorded just a week earlier. The Reserve Bank of India's June bulletin warns this is not a seasonal blip but a structural threat that could push food inflation above 6 per cent and delay any rate cut until at least October. The bulletin landed two weeks after the Monetary Policy Committee cut the FY27 GDP growth forecast to 6.6 per cent and raised the CPI inflation projection to 5.1 per cent, holding the repo rate at 5.25 per cent.

## The Warning

A below-normal monsoon threatens agricultural output, rural demand, and the broader inflation trajectory through sustained price pressures in cereals, vegetables, pulses, and edible oils. The RBI cautioned that if food-price increases feed into wage demands, India could face a "generalisation of inflation through second-round effects on expectations and wages." Reservoir levels have fallen below the decadal average, endangering Rabi sowing and industrial water availability. The monsoon shortfall compounds existing headwinds: elevated global oil prices, geopolitical uncertainty, and moderating industrial output.

## Growth Outlook

The MPC revised GDP growth down to 6.6 per cent from 6.9 per cent, with Q2 projected as the weakest quarter at 6.3 per cent. Private consumption accounts for nearly 60 per cent of GDP, and food-inflation-driven purchasing-power erosion directly threatens that engine. The bulletin expects a gradual second-half recovery, contingent on the monsoon improving and global conditions stabilising. The 0.3-percentage-point cut reflects the central bank's assessment that the monsoon deficit alone could shave a comparable amount off full-year growth.

## Inflation Outlook

CPI inflation is projected to peak at 5.9 per cent in Q3 before easing, with the full-year forecast raised sharply to 5.1 per cent from 4.6 per cent. May CPI hit 3.9 per cent, up from 3.5 per cent in April, driven entirely by food. Core inflation is pegged at 4.7 per cent for the year. The critical variable is whether food-price pressure remains transient or becomes entrenched: if second-round effects materialise, the MPC's path to its 4 per cent target lengthens considerably, pushing any rate easing into the second half of FY27.

## Policy Response

The repo rate stays at 5.25 per cent with a neutral stance, signalling the MPC is buying time. Governor Sanjay Malhotra reiterated the dual mandate — aligning inflation with 4 per cent while supporting growth — but the monsoon outcome will determine the August review's tone. Forex reserves of $682.3 billion provide external cover, but domestic inflation dynamics, not external vulnerability, now constrain policy. Markets expect a hold through August and a potential cut in October if inflation moderates and rains recover. The government has begun contingency steps: accelerated subsidised grain distribution and enhanced procurement to stabilise prices. The real question is whether administrative measures can offset a climatic shock that is already 42.2 per cent deep.
