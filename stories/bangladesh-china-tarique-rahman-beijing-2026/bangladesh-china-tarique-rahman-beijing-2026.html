Bangladesh's China Pivot: How Tarique Rahman's Beijing Deals Reshape South Asia

Bangladesh PM Tarique Rahman signed 16 agreements in Beijing — Teesta River project ($1B+), Mongla port operational cooperation, and Chinese backing for BRICS membership. The strategic consequences for India are profound.

On June 26, Bangladesh Prime Minister Tarique Rahman left Beijing with a 16-point joint communiqué, over $1 billion in infrastructure commitments, and a question hanging over South Asia: is Bangladesh pivoting decisively into China's orbit?

The visit — the longest and most consequential by a Bangladeshi leader to China in a decade — produced agreements that touch nearly every dimension of bilateral relations: water sharing on the Teesta River, operational control of the Mongla port, a China-Myanmar-Bangladesh Economic Corridor, military cooperation, and public Chinese backing for Dhaka's membership in BRICS and the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation.

For New Delhi, watching from across the border, the communiqué reads like a catalogue of strategic anxieties. The timing is particularly acute: the agreements come just days after NSA Ajit Doval and Chinese FM Wang Yi met in New Delhi to discuss "gradual normalisation" of India-China ties — suggesting Beijing is simultaneously improving relations with New Delhi while tightening its embrace of Dhaka.

Bottom Line

Tarique Rahman's June 22–26 Beijing visit produced the most significant shift in Bangladesh's foreign policy orientation since independence in 1971. China secured: (1) a $1B+ infrastructure project on the Teesta River — a transboundary waterway flowing through India's Siliguri Corridor; (2) operational cooperation at Mongla port — India's lost infrastructure prize; (3) a proposed trilateral economic corridor linking Yunnan to Chittagong; and (4) a military and diplomatic partnership that threatens to displace India as Bangladesh's primary security partner. For India, the message from Dhaka is clear: China delivers, India delays.

The Visit

Tarique Rahman arrived in Beijing on June 22 for a six-day state visit — his first to China since assuming office in December 2024. The scale of preparation was unusual: Chinese President Xi Jinping hosted Rahman at the Great Hall of the People for a bilateral summit that ran three hours longer than scheduled. The official welcoming ceremony included a 21-gun salute and a guard of honour — protocol normally reserved for heads of state of China's closest allies.

Flanked by a high-level delegation that included Foreign Minister Touhid Hossain, Finance Minister Salehuddin Ahmed, and the chiefs of the Bangladesh Army and Navy, Rahman participated in the China-Bangladesh Business Forum, toured Chinese industrial zones in Guangdong and Jiangsu, and held a bilateral meeting with Premier Li Qiang focused on trade and infrastructure financing.

The joint communiqué, released on June 26, ran to 16 clauses covering water resources, port development, trade, investment, defence, counter-terrorism, cyber security, cultural exchanges, and multilateral cooperation. It was, by any measure, the most comprehensive bilateral agreement Bangladesh has signed with any country.

"This visit was designed to signal that Bangladesh's foreign policy is no longer defined by its relationship with India alone," says a Dhaka-based political analyst who tracks Bangladesh-China relations. "The message to New Delhi is not hostile — it's transactional. Bangladesh has options, and those who deliver on its core interests will get preferential access."

The Teesta Gambit

What Is the Teesta River?

The Teesta is a 315-km transboundary river that originates in the glaciers of Sikkim (India), flows through the Siliguri Corridor — a 22-km-wide strip of Indian territory connecting the northeast to the rest of the country — and enters Bangladesh in the Rangpur division before joining the Brahmaputra. It is the lifeblood of northern Bangladesh's agriculture, supporting irrigation for millions of farmers. A water-sharing agreement between India and Bangladesh has been pending since 2011.

The most immediately consequential agreement is China's commitment to fund and execute the Teesta River comprehensive management and restoration project — estimated at over $1 billion. The project scope includes riverbank stabilisation, dredging, flood control embankments, and irrigation infrastructure. Chinese survey teams are expected to arrive in Bangladesh within 60 days of the agreement's signing.

The Teesta has been a flashpoint in India-Bangladesh relations for over a decade. In 2011, then-PM Manmohan Singh and Bangladesh PM Sheikh Hasina were on the verge of signing a water-sharing treaty when West Bengal Chief Minister Mamata Banerjee objected, causing the agreement to collapse at the last minute. Despite repeated attempts by successive Indian governments — including a 2018 draft treaty and a 2022 joint rivers commission meeting — the treaty has never been finalised due to opposition from the state government.

Now, China is stepping in where India could not deliver. The geostrategic implication is stark: Chinese engineers, equipment, and financing on the Teesta — a river that flows through a 22-kilometre-wide corridor connecting India's northeast to the rest of the country. For Indian strategic planners, it is a vulnerability with few historical precedents.

"There's no precedent for this. Every previous transboundary river project involving a third party was rejected by India as a violation of the 1996 Ganges Treaty. This changes the game completely."

Former Indian diplomat who served in Dhaka

China's Ministry of Foreign Affairs, in a June 27 press briefing, defended the Teesta project as "purely for civilian purposes" and "in full compliance with international law." Spokesperson Wang Wenbin said the project would "bring tangible benefits to the people of the Teesta basin" and called India's concerns "unwarranted."

"The Chinese are very smart about this," says the former diplomat. "They've positioned the Teesta project as a humanitarian water-management initiative. Any Indian objection looks like opposing flood control and irrigation for Bangladeshi farmers. It's a diplomatic trap."

Mongla Port: The Prize China Always Wanted

Buried in clause 6 of the joint communiqué is an agreement on "operational cooperation" at the Mongla port — Bangladesh's second-largest maritime gateway, located 50 kilometres from the Sundarbans in the southwest. The phrasing is deliberately vague, but sources familiar with the negotiations say it involves Chinese investment in port modernisation, extended operational concessions, and preferential berthing rights for Chinese vessels.

Mongla was originally an Indian project. In 2018, India committed $260 million to develop terminal facilities and dredge the channel, securing preferential access for Indian cargo. But the project languished — delayed by land acquisition disputes, environmental clearances, and bureaucratic inertia. In 2023, Bangladesh quietly invited China to bid on the same port.

By 2025, China had committed $430 million to Mongla's development — nearly double India's original pledge. The June 2026 agreement formalises Beijing's dominant position. The timeline tells the story:

"Mongla is the clearest metric of India's diminishing influence in Bangladesh. India had the first-mover advantage and lost it through slow execution. China didn't muscle India out — India walked away and China walked in."

South Asia analyst, European think tank

The strategic significance extends beyond commerce. Mongla sits on the Bay of Bengal, giving China a second deep-sea port in the region alongside Hambantota in Sri Lanka and Chittagong. It sits 350 kilometres from India's Visakhapatnam naval base and 250 kilometres from the Eastern Naval Command.

The China-Myanmar-Bangladesh Economic Corridor

The most ambitious project in the communiqué is a proposed trilateral economic corridor linking China's Yunnan province through Myanmar to Bangladesh's Chittagong port. The corridor — still in feasibility stage — would create an alternative trade route bypassing the Strait of Malacca, giving Beijing direct overland access to the Bay of Bengal.

For Bangladesh, the corridor promises to unlock its position as a transit hub between South Asia and Southeast Asia. But the route passes through conflict zones in Myanmar's Rakhine and Chin states, where the Myanmar military has been fighting a multi-front insurgency since the 2021 coup. The corridor also runs within 100 kilometres of India's Mizoram border.

The Bangladesh-Myanmar-China road alignment was already part of a 2013 memorandum. What is new is the scale of Chinese commitment and the explicit inclusion of Bangladesh in Beijing's Belt and Road Initiative master plan for continental connectivity.

China's South Asian port network now forms an arc around India's coastline. The Mongla agreement and the proposed China-Myanmar-Bangladesh corridor complete a strategic chain from Gwadar to Chittagong.

Military & Diplomatic Alignment

The communiqué contains several clauses with direct implications for India's security calculus:

Clause 12 — Defence Cooperation: China and Bangladesh will deepen defence exchanges, joint exercises, and personnel training. China has pledged $100 million in defence grant assistance over five years. The Bangladesh Army chief was part of the Beijing delegation — a signal that military-to-military relations are being elevated alongside political and economic ties.

Clause 10 — BRICS and SCO Membership: China explicitly supports Bangladesh's membership in BRICS and the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation — giving Dhaka a pathway into both groupings with Beijing as its patron. If admitted, Bangladesh would be the first South Asian country to join BRICS after India's founding membership, potentially creating friction within the bloc.

Clause 11 — Security Cooperation: Both sides agreed to enhance cooperation on counter-terrorism, cyber security, and combating transnational crime. These are areas where India has traditionally been Bangladesh's primary security partner — including intelligence sharing on insurgent groups operating in India's northeast.

Clause 9 — 2+2 Dialogue: A new mechanism at the foreign secretary and defence secretary level, mirroring China's existing 2+2 mechanisms with Pakistan and Cambodia. This institutionalises defence and strategic dialogue at the highest bureaucratic level, creating a permanent channel for security cooperation independent of political leadership changes.

One-China Reaffirmation: Bangladesh reaffirmed its commitment to the One-China principle and opposition to "Taiwan independence" — standard language, but significant given Bangladesh's traditionally non-committal posture on cross-strait issues under previous governments.

The Domestic Context

Tarique Rahman came to power in Bangladesh at a moment of profound transition. The collapse of the Sheikh Hasina government in the summer of 2024 — triggered by the student-led quota reform protests and the subsequent army intervention — fundamentally restructured Bangladesh's political landscape. Rahman, the son of former Prime Minister Khaleda Zia and acting chairman of the Bangladesh Nationalist Party (BNP), won the December 2024 general election on a platform of economic revival, anti-corruption, and foreign policy rebalancing.

Hasina's 15-year rule (2009–2024) was marked by a carefully calibrated alignment with India — on security, trade, and water. The bilateral relationship was arguably the warmest in Bangladesh's history: India extended a $7 billion line of credit in 2022, signed the historic Land Boundary Agreement in 2015, and cooperated closely on counter-terrorism and border management. The two countries' security establishments shared intelligence on insurgent groups, coordinated border patrols, and conducted joint military exercises.

But the relationship also bred resentment. The Teesta water-sharing treaty remained unsigned — repeatedly blocked by West Bengal's political opposition. Trade imbalances widened: Bangladesh's trade deficit with India reached $8.8 billion in 2025. And many Bangladeshis felt India had backed Hasina's authoritarian turn, alienating the political opposition that Rahman now leads.

"Rahman's foreign policy is not anti-India in the ideological sense. It's transactional. He's signalling that Bangladesh has options, and those who deliver on Bangladesh's core interests will get preferential access. China made commitments; India made promises it couldn't keep."

Dhaka-based political analyst

The domestic political calculus is also important. Rahman's BNP-led government faces significant economic headwinds: inflation running at 9.4%, declining foreign exchange reserves ($18 billion as of May 2026, down from $46 billion in 2021), and the need to create 2 million jobs annually for a young population. Chinese infrastructure financing — which comes with fewer conditionalities than World Bank or ADB loans — offers a path to visible deliverables that Rahman can present to voters ahead of the 2029 general election.

The Economic Calculus

The economic dimensions of the China-Bangladesh relationship are substantial and growing. China is now Bangladesh's largest trading partner — a position India held as recently as 2019. The gap has widened significantly in the intervening years.

The asymmetry is particularly stark in infrastructure financing. Chinese loans to Bangladesh — estimated at $40 billion cumulatively over the past decade — have funded the Padma Bridge (built by Chinese contractors), the Dhaka metro system, coal-fired power plants producing 8,000 MW, and a network of highways connecting Dhaka to regional economic zones. India's $7 billion line of credit, announced with fanfare in 2022, has been disbursed at a fraction of that rate, with projects delayed by land acquisition issues and environmental clearance bottlenecks.

Chinese FDI stock in Bangladesh has surpassed $15 billion, concentrated in power, textiles, infrastructure, and special economic zones. India's cumulative FDI in Bangladesh is estimated at under $4 billion, primarily in banking, pharmaceuticals, and consumer goods. The gap is not marginal — it is structural.

The Regional Picture

Bangladesh's China pivot does not exist in isolation. It is part of a broader realignment of South Asian states toward Beijing that has accelerated over the past decade:

Pakistan
CPEC investments exceed $60 billion. Gwadar port under Chinese operation. China is Pakistan's largest trading partner and arms supplier.
Sri Lanka
Hambantota port under 99-year Chinese lease after debt restructuring. $5 billion in Chinese loans. Chinese-owned Colombo Port City special economic zone.
Nepal
Joined BRI in 2017. Chinese-funded hydropower projects (Budhi Gandaki), airports (Pokhara International), and cross-border railways in feasibility stage.
Maldives
Signed Free Trade Agreement with China in 2024. Chinese port investments at Male. China is Maldives' largest external creditor.
Bangladesh
Now: Teesta, Mongla, corridor, BRICS backing, defence pact. Bangladesh was the last major South Asian state to maintain strategic autonomy.

"Bangladesh was the last major South Asian country to maintain strategic autonomy between India and China. With the June 2026 agreements, that era is over. The idea that South Asian countries can maintain equidistance was always a fiction. The question was never whether they would tilt, but when — and on which issues. For Bangladesh, the Teesta was the issue that broke the balance."

Regional analyst, on condition of anonymity

The pattern across the region is consistent: China offers infrastructure financing with minimal conditionalities, rapid execution, and no demands on governance or human rights. India offers smaller lines of credit, slower disbursement, and — from the perspective of recipient countries — intermittent political attention. The cumulative effect is a slow but steady transfer of influence.

What Comes Next

The Tarique Rahman-Beijing agreements create a new strategic reality in South Asia that will unfold over the next 12–24 months:

Watch Points: 2026–2028

  • Teesta implementation (2026–2028): Chinese survey teams expected in Bangladesh within 60 days. Feasibility studies and environmental assessments will take 12–18 months. Actual construction could begin in early 2028. India must decide whether to engage, object, or accommodate.
  • BRICS membership path (2026–2027): Bangladesh has applied for BRICS membership with China's backing. If accepted, Bangladesh would be the first South Asian BRICS member after India, potentially creating friction within the bloc's internal dynamics.
  • Mongla operationalisation (2026–2027): Port expansion will take 2–3 years. Chinese commercial and potentially naval access to the Bay of Bengal will become a reality within this timeframe.
  • India-Bangladesh recalibration (2026 onwards): India must assess how to manage a Bangladesh more aligned with China while maintaining cooperation on border security, counter-terrorism, and people-to-people ties. The timing is particularly sensitive: the 1996 Ganges Treaty is due for review in 2027.

The next milestone will be the BRICS Leaders Summit in September 2026, hosted by India. If Bangladesh's membership application is formally considered at that summit — with China pushing for approval and India holding reservations — it would create a direct diplomatic confrontation between New Delhi and Beijing within a multilateral forum that India chairs.

The Outlook

FINAL ANALYSIS

The June 2026 Beijing agreements represent the most significant shift in Bangladesh's foreign policy orientation since independence in 1971. They do not mean Bangladesh has become an anti-India state — the relationship retains deep cultural, economic, and security linkages. But they do mean China is now an equal, and in some domains dominant, player in Bangladesh's strategic calculus.

For India, the challenge is not just about Bangladesh. It is about what Bangladesh's tilt signals to other South Asian states: that China delivers, India delays. The message has been heard in Colombo, Kathmandu, and Thimphu.

The Teesta River — a 315-kilometre waterway that begins in the glaciers of Sikkim and empties into the Brahmaputra in Bangladesh — is now the site of the most consequential infrastructure competition in South Asia. It is not a war, not a crisis, not a confrontation. It is a quiet, irreversible transfer of influence — and it is happening while New Delhi is watching.

India's response will determine whether this is the beginning of a broader strategic marginalisation of Indian influence in its neighbourhood — or a wake-up call that forces a fundamental recalibration of how New Delhi engages with its smallest, closest neighbours.
