## Cold Open

On June 26, 2026, Bangladesh Prime Minister Tarique Rahman left Beijing with a 16-point joint communiqué, over $1 billion in infrastructure commitments, and a question hanging over South Asia: has Bangladesh pivoted decisively into China's orbit?

The visit — the longest and most consequential by a Bangladeshi leader to China in a decade — produced agreements spanning water sharing on the Teesta River, operational control of the Mongla port, a China-Myanmar-Bangladesh Economic Corridor, military cooperation, and public Chinese backing for Bangladesh's membership in BRICS and the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation. For New Delhi, the communiqué reads like a catalogue of strategic anxieties.

## The Teesta Gambit

The most immediately consequential agreement is China's commitment to fund and execute the Teesta River comprehensive management and restoration project — estimated at over $1 billion. The Teesta, which flows from Sikkim through the Siliguri Corridor (India's "chicken's neck") into northern Bangladesh, has been a flashpoint in India-Bangladesh relations for over a decade.

India has repeatedly failed to finalise a water-sharing treaty on the Teesta due to opposition from West Bengal. Bangladesh has sought a solution since 2011, when a proposed agreement collapsed after Chief Minister Mamata Banerjee objected. Now China is stepping in where India could not deliver. The geostrategic implication: Chinese engineers, equipment, and financing on a river flowing through a 22-kilometre-wide corridor connecting India's northeast to the rest of the country.

"There's no precedent for this," says a former Indian diplomat who served in Dhaka. "Every previous transboundary river project involving a third party was rejected by India as a violation of the 1996 Ganges Treaty. This changes the game completely."

## Mongla Port: The Prize China Always Wanted

Clause 6 of the joint communiqué contains an agreement on "operational cooperation" at Mongla port — Bangladesh's second-largest maritime gateway, located 50 kilometres from the Sundarbans. Sources familiar with the negotiations say it involves Chinese investment in port modernisation, extended operational concessions, and preferential berthing rights for Chinese vessels.

Mongla was originally an Indian project. In 2018, India committed $260 million to develop terminal facilities and dredge the channel, securing preferential access for Indian cargo. But the project languished — delayed by land acquisition disputes, environmental clearances, and bureaucratic inertia. By 2025, China had committed $430 million to Mongla's development — nearly double India's original pledge. The June 2026 agreement formalises Beijing's dominant position.

"Mongla is the clearest metric of India's diminishing influence in Bangladesh," says a South Asia analyst at a European think tank. "India had the first-mover advantage and lost it through slow execution. China didn't muscle India out — India walked away and China walked in."

Mongla sits on the Bay of Bengal, 350 kilometres from India's Visakhapatnam naval base and 250 kilometres from the Eastern Naval Command, giving China a second deep-sea port in the region alongside Hambantota in Sri Lanka.

## The China-Myanmar-Bangladesh Economic Corridor

The most ambitious project in the communiqué is a proposed trilateral economic corridor linking China's Yunnan province through Myanmar to Bangladesh's Chittagong port. Still in feasibility stage, the corridor would create an alternative trade route bypassing the Strait of Malacca, giving Beijing direct overland access to the Bay of Bengal.

For Bangladesh, the corridor promises to unlock its position as a transit hub between South Asia and Southeast Asia. But the route passes through conflict zones in Myanmar's Rakhine and Chin states, where the military has been fighting a multi-front insurgency since the 2021 coup. The corridor also runs within 100 kilometres of India's Mizoram border.

## Military and Diplomatic Alignment

Several clauses have direct implications for India's security calculus. China and Bangladesh will deepen defence exchanges, joint exercises, and personnel training, with China pledging $100 million in defence grant assistance over five years. China explicitly supports Bangladesh's BRICS and SCO membership — giving Dhaka a pathway into both groupings with Beijing as its patron. A new 2+2 dialogue mechanism at the foreign and defence secretary level mirrors China's existing mechanisms with Pakistan and Cambodia. Bangladesh reaffirmed its commitment to the One-China principle — significant given the country's traditionally non-committal posture on cross-strait issues under previous governments.

## The Domestic Context

Tarique Rahman came to power at a moment of profound transition. The collapse of the Sheikh Hasina government in mid-2024 — triggered by student-led quota reform protests and subsequent army intervention — restructured Bangladesh's political landscape. Rahman, son of former Prime Minister Khaleda Zia, won the December 2024 general election on a platform of economic revival, anti-corruption, and foreign policy rebalancing.

Hasina's 15-year rule maintained warm ties with India — a $7 billion line of credit, the Land Boundary Agreement, close counter-terrorism cooperation. But the relationship also bred resentment. The Teesta treaty remained unsigned. Trade imbalances widened. Many Bangladeshis felt India backed Hasina's authoritarian turn.

"Rahman's foreign policy is not anti-India in the ideological sense," says a Dhaka-based political analyst. "It's transactional. He's signalling that Bangladesh has options, and those who deliver on core interests will get preferential access. China made commitments; India made promises it couldn't keep."

Chinese FDI stock in Bangladesh has surpassed $15 billion, concentrated in power, textiles, infrastructure, and special economic zones. India's cumulative FDI in Bangladesh is estimated at under $4 billion. Chinese loans — estimated at $40 billion cumulatively over the past decade — have funded the Padma Bridge, Dhaka metro, power plants, and a network of highways. India's $7 billion line of credit, announced in 2022, has been disbursed at a fraction of that rate.

## The Response

India's official response has been muted. The Ministry of External Affairs said India was "aware of the developments" and would "take necessary measures." Privately, officials express deep concern — particularly about the precedent: if China funds a water-management project on a transboundary river, what stops Beijing from doing the same on the Brahmaputra, which flows through Tibet into Arunachal Pradesh and Assam before reaching Bangladesh?

China's Ministry of Foreign Affairs defended the Teesta project as "purely for civilian purposes" and "in full compliance with international law." The Chinese have positioned the project as a humanitarian water-management initiative — any Indian objection looks like opposing flood control and irrigation for Bangladeshi farmers.

## What Comes Next

Over the next 12–24 months: Chinese survey teams are expected to arrive in Bangladesh within 60 days for Teesta feasibility studies, with construction possible in early 2028. Bangladesh has applied for BRICS membership with China's backing. If accepted, it would be the first South Asian country to join after India's founding membership. The Mongla port expansion will take 2–3 years, giving Chinese commercial and potentially naval access to the Bay of Bengal. India and Bangladesh are due to review the 1996 Ganges Treaty in 2027.

Bangladesh was the last major South Asian country to maintain strategic autonomy between India and China. With the June 2026 agreements, that era is over.
