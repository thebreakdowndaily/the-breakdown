China's LineShine Supercomputer Retakes World #1 — Built Without Any Foreign Chips

The Breakdown · June 25, 2026 · 8 min read

On June 23, at the International Supercomputing Conference in Hamburg, the TOP500 list was announced with a surprise. A previously unreported Chinese system called LineShine debuted at #1, achieving 2.198 exaflops — 20% faster than the American El Capitan. It was China's first #1 supercomputer since 2017. But the real story is not the speed. It is how LineShine got there: with no foreign chips, no GPUs, and no accelerators of any kind. It is a machine built to prove that US export controls cannot contain Chinese computing ambition.

Each LX2 processor contains two compute dies, each divided into four NUMA domains. Each domain packs 38 ARMv9 cores and 4 GB of high-bandwidth memory. A Smart Direct Memory Access engine shuttles data between the HBM and 128 GB of off-package DDR memory per die. The LingQi interconnect uses a dual-plane, multi-rail fat-tree topology. Every component — from the processor to the interconnect to the operating system to the memory — is Chinese-designed.

Why No GPUs Matters

Every other exascale system in the top 5 relies heavily on GPU accelerators — AMD's MI300A in El Capitan and Frontier, Intel's Ponte Vecchio in Aurora, NVIDIA's GPUs in JUPITER. These specialist chips excel at the parallel calculations that supercomputing and AI demand. They are also almost exclusively designed by American companies and manufactured on processes that US export controls restrict from reaching China.

LineShine's CPU-only architecture is a direct response to those controls. By building a machine with 13.79 million ARMv9 cores, a proprietary interconnect, domestic HBM, and homegrown software, China demonstrated that it can reach the top of the supercomputing pyramid without importing a single sanctioned component. As TOP500 co-founder Jack Dongarra told SCMP: "China can adapt to develop its own version of technology as good as — or maybe even better than — existing technology, despite US export controls."

LineShine's all-CPU design sacrifices AI mixed-precision performance (7.92 vs 16.7 Exaflop/s) but wins on double-precision and eliminates sanctions exposure

The Sanctions Story

China stopped submitting Linpack results to the TOP500 around 2019, when US export controls began restricting access to advanced chips. For years, the global HPC community knew China was building exascale systems — it was widely reported that China fielded several unreported exascale machines — but there was no official benchmark confirmation. LineShine marks China's return to the TOP500, and it does so with a machine that makes a political statement as clearly as a technical one.

"The accelerators China cannot reliably buy are precisely the ones LineShine does not use," wrote Supercomputing News. "The all-CPU design need not be the compromise a constrained program settled for. It can be read as the demonstration the program set out to make: that a leading-class supercomputer can be assembled today with nothing on the other side of an export-control line."

"The whole point of US export controls was to stop China reaching this level. China hasn't held the top spot since 2017, so this is a real statement."

Where It Excels — and Where It Doesn't

LineShine's design trades off one thing: AI performance. On the HPL-MxP benchmark (mixed precision, relevant for AI), LineShine ranks #4 at 7.92 exaflops — a modest 3.6x speedup over its double-precision score. El Capitan, by contrast, achieves 16.7 exaflops on HPL-MxP, a 9.2x speedup. The gap reflects the absence of GPU-style tensor cores optimized for low-precision AI workloads.

But LineShine leads on the HPCG benchmark (22.00 petaflops), which better represents real scientific computing workloads. "A CPU-only machine leading both the LINPACK ranking and the workload-representative one is the technical story the flag-counting coverage skipped," noted Supercomputing News. "The memory-forward design is doing exactly what it was built to do."

The system has already been used for climate modeling, ocean-atmosphere science, engineering simulation, materials research, drug discovery, brain science, and AI. Its software platform integrates supercomputing and AI workloads, allowing researchers to use the same system for both simulation and data-intensive discovery.

The Bottom Line

THE VERDICT

LineShine is not just a faster supercomputer. It is a demonstration that the US strategy of containing Chinese chip development through export controls has a blind spot. China could not buy the GPUs that power every other leading supercomputer. So it built a machine that does not need them — 13.79 million ARM cores, a proprietary interconnect, domestic memory, and a homegrown operating system, all assembled into the most powerful scientific computer ever measured. The cost is power (42.2 MW — the highest in the top 10) and AI performance (half of El Capitan's mixed-precision score). The achievement is sovereignty. LineShine is the sound of a country proving it can compute without permission.
