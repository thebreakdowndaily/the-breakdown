# VB-G RAM G: MGNREGA Replaced After 21 Years — 125 Days Guaranteed, ₹300 Wage Floor, States Now Pay 40%

## THE OPENING

At 12:00 AM on July 1, 2026, the Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act, 2005 — India's flagship rural employment programme for 21 years — ceased to exist.

In its place came the Viksit Bharat – Guarantee for Rozgar and Ajeevika Mission (Gramin): VB-G RAM G Act, 2025.

The change is not cosmetic. It is the most significant restructuring of India's rural employment architecture since the original law was passed unanimously by Parliament in 2005.

This is what changed, what stayed, what it costs, and what it means for 15 crore rural households.

---

## 1. THE EVIDENCE

### 1.1 What the Act Changes

The VB-G RAM G Act (full Hindi title: विकसित भारत—जी राम जी) was passed by Parliament in December 2025 amid Opposition protests, received presidential assent on December 21, 2025, and came into force on July 1, 2026. It simultaneously repealed MGNREGA.

| Parameter | MGNREGA (2005-2026) | VB-G RAM G (2026-) | Change |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|--------|
| Guaranteed workdays | 100 per household/year | 125 per household/year | +25 days |
| Wage floor | None (state-specific) | ₹300/day statutory minimum | New |
| National avg. wage | ₹298.8/day | ₹327.4/day | +9.5% |
| Centre wage share | 100% | 60% (general states) | -40% |
| State wage share | 0% | 40% (general states) | New burden |
| Funding model | Open-ended (demand-driven) | Normative allocation (capped) | Structural shift |
| Job cards | Paper-based MGNREGA cards | Smart cards with face auth | Upgrade |
| Work categories | Broad rural works | 4 focus areas | Narrowed |
| Seasonal pause | None | 60-day agri peak window | New |

Sources: VB-G RAM G Act, 2025 (Act No. 36 of 2025); PIB notification May 11, 2026; Ministry of Rural Development notification June 30, 2026.

### 1.2 The Wage Floor — ₹300/Day

For the first time in the 21-year history of India's rural employment guarantee, the central government has notified a statutory minimum wage floor. Effective July 1, 2026, no worker under the programme may be paid less than ₹300 per day.

The national average notified wage rose to ₹327.4 per day, a 9.5% increase from ₹298.8 under MGNREGA.

### 1.3 Who Pays Now — The 60:40 Shift

Under MGNREGA, the central government bore 100% of the wage cost for unskilled labour. State governments shared only the material and administrative costs (25% of material component).

Under VB-G RAM G, the funding architecture is fundamentally different:

**General states (majority):** Centre 60% : State 40%
**NE & Himalayan states + J&K, HP, Uttarakhand:** Centre 90% : State 10%
**UTs without legislature:** Centre 100%

This means states must now budget for 40% of wage costs — a new recurring expenditure item for state finances.

**Additional financial rules:**
- Material component capped at 40% at the district level
- Normative allocation determined by 16th Finance Commission formula
- Any expenditure beyond normative allocation must be borne entirely by the state
- A portion of allocation may be withheld and redistributed based on performance (timely wages, social audit compliance, work completion)

Source: VB-G RAM G Act, Section 22(1); Indian Express, "VB-G RAM G Rules Explained," May 27, 2026.

The Ministry of Rural Development released an interim allocation of ₹95,692.31 crore to states and Union Territories for the transition. This is the highest-ever Budget Estimate allocation for a rural employment programme in India.

## 3. THE DEBATE

### 3.1 The Government's Case

Union Rural Development Minister Shivraj Singh Chouhan called the rollout "a historic and unprecedented day," stating on X: "This is not just an act, but a new chapter of dignity, self-respect, and secure livelihood for crores of our rural worker brothers and sisters."

The government's arguments in favour:

**More days, more money.** 125 days vs 100 is a 25% increase in the statutory guarantee. The wage floor ensures no worker is paid below ₹300 anywhere in the country.

**Asset creation.** The Act focuses on four categories of works — water security, core rural infrastructure, livelihood-related infrastructure, and extreme weather mitigation — designed to build durable community assets.

**Technology for transparency.** Smart job cards with face recognition, geo-tagged works, and DBT wage payments reduce leakages. The old system lost an estimated 15-20% to ghost workers and inflated muster rolls.

**Village-level planning.** Viksit Gram Panchayat Plans (VGPPs), approved by Gram Sabhas, give local communities a formal role in deciding which works to undertake.

**Seamless transition.** 25 states allocated funds. 24 notified state schemes. Existing MGNREGA job cards remain valid. Ongoing works were carried over.

### 3.2 The Critics' Case

Five opposition-ruled states — Karnataka, Kerala, Punjab, Telangana, and Jharkhand — passed resolutions opposing the Act. Labour rights activists and economists raised structural concerns:

**Rights-based to supply-driven.** MGNREGA was a demand-driven, open-ended entitlement. If a household demanded work, the government was obligated to provide it (or pay unemployment allowance). VB-G RAM G replaces this with normative allocations — a capped budget determined by the Centre. If demand exceeds the allocation, the state must fund the excess.

**Fiscal burden on states.** The 40% wage cost share is a new, recurring expenditure for states. For Bihar — where 43% of rural households depend on MGNREGA — this means an estimated ₹3,500-5,000 crore additional annual burden.

**Digitisation as barrier.** Smart job cards with face-recognition attendance may exclude elderly, illiterate, and disabled workers who cannot complete e-KYC. While the government has said workers cannot be denied employment due to pending e-KYC, activists warn of de facto exclusion.

**Performance-linked allocation.** The provision to withhold a portion of normative allocation based on "performance" (timely wages, social audit compliance) could penalize states with weaker administrative capacity — precisely those with the highest rural poverty.

**Unemployment allowance diluted.** Under MGNREGA, unemployment allowance was the Centre's liability if work was not provided within 15 days. Under VB-G RAM G, it is the state's liability — payable at 1/4th wage for first 30 days and 1/2 thereafter.

**60-day seasonal pause.** The Act allows a 60-day pause during peak sowing and harvesting. Critics argue this could be used to deny work during the months when rural households need it most — and that it undermines the "guarantee" character of the entitlement.

### 3.3 The Numbers That Cut Both Ways

| Argument | Government | Critics |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| 125 days | 25% increase in guarantee | But average was only 50 days; demand constraint is not legal |
| ₹300 floor | Historic wage protection | 11 states already paid above; floor mostly helps lowest — which are also poorest states |
| ₹95,692 Cr budget | Highest ever allocation | But states must now contribute ~₹38,000 Cr from their own budgets |
| Smart cards | Transparency, less leakage | Digital barriers for vulnerable workers |
| Normative allocation | Predictable budgeting | Capped funding breaks the "guarantee" character |

## 4. THE BIGGER PICTURE

### 4.1 Two Decades of Rural Employment Policy

MGNREGA was enacted in 2005 as a rights-based response to rural distress. It was novel in three respects: it created a justiciable right to work, it was demand-driven (uncapped), and it was funded almost entirely by the Centre.

For its first decade (2005-2015), the scheme was widely praised for reducing rural poverty, empowering women (33% participation mandate, later 50%), and creating productive assets. The World Bank called it "the largest public works programme in the world."

From 2015 onward, budgetary constraints, administrative tightening, and shifting political priorities reduced the scheme's effectiveness. Real wages stagnated. Payment delays became chronic. The average days worked per household fell from 54 in 2009-10 to 50 in 2024-25 — well below the 100-day guarantee.

VB-G RAM G represents both an expansion (more days, wage floor) and a restructuring (funding shift, capped allocation). Whether it represents progress or regression depends on which side of the ledger one weighs more heavily.

### 4.2 What Other Countries Do

| Country | Programme | Days/Year | Funding Model |
|---------|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| India | VB-G RAM G | 125 | Shared (60:40 C:S) |
| Ethiopia | Productive Safety Net | 180 (6 months) | Donor + government |
| Kenya | Cash for Work | Variable | Donor-funded |
| Bangladesh | EGPP | 100 | Government + donor |
| Indonesia | Padat Karya | Variable | Government |
| South Africa | Expanded Public Works | Variable | Government |

Only India and Ethiopia operate rights-based national rural employment guarantees of this scale.

### 4.3 The Fiscal Context

India's total expenditure on MGNREGA peaked at ₹1.11 lakh crore in 2020-21 (COVID year). As a percentage of GDP, it averaged 0.3-0.4% — modest by international standards for social protection.

The ₹95,692 crore VB-G RAM G allocation for FY27 is approximately 0.28% of India's projected GDP ($4.15tn). With states contributing an estimated ₹38,000 crore (40% wage share), total programme spending could reach ₹1.34 lakh crore.

## 6. THE BOTTOM LINE

MGNREGA was not a perfect programme. It suffered from payment delays, administrative corruption, and declining real wages. But it was a rights-based entitlement: if a rural household demanded work, the government was legally obliged to provide it.

VB-G RAM G expands the guarantee in days (125) and establishes a wage floor (₹300) — both genuine improvements. But it transforms the fundamental character of the programme from an open-ended, demand-driven entitlement to a capped, supply-driven scheme with shared funding.

The question is not whether more days and a wage floor are good. They are. The question is whether the structural changes — the 40% state share, the normative allocation cap, the performance-linked withholding, the seasonal pause — will, in practice, reduce access for the poorest households in the poorest states.

The answer depends on whether states can pay their share and whether the Centre's normative allocations match actual demand. The first quarterly wage data, due in September 2026, will provide the earliest signal.
