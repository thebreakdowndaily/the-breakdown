VB-G RAM G: MGNREGA Replaced After 21 Years — 125 Days Guaranteed, ₹300 Wage Floor, States Now Pay 40%

The Breakdown · July 1, 2026 · 16 min read · Editorial Intelligence Unit
Rural workers at a MGNREGA worksite. The 21-year-old programme was replaced by VB-G RAM G on July 1, 2026. | Representational image
✓ FACT-CHECKED Every claim labeled with evidence type, source, date, and confidence score (0-100). Primary sources include the VB-G RAM G Act text, PIB notifications, Ministry of Rural Development orders, and major news reports.

At midnight on July 1, 2026, the Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act, 2005 — India’s flagship rural employment programme for 21 years — ceased to exist.

In its place came the Viksit Bharat – Guarantee for Rozgar and Ajeevika Mission (Gramin): VB-G RAM G Act, 2025.

The change is not cosmetic. It is the most significant restructuring of India’s rural employment architecture since the original law was passed unanimously by Parliament in 2005.

This is what changed, what stayed, what it costs, and what it means for 15 crore rural households.

1.2 The Wage Floor — ₹300/Day

Official Notification Source: Ministry of Rural Development, Wage Rate Notification, June 30, 2026. Confidence: 100

For the first time in the 21-year history of India’s rural employment guarantee, the central government has notified a statutory minimum wage floor. Effective July 1, 2026, no worker under the programme may be paid less than ₹300 per day.

The national average notified wage rose to ₹327.4 per day, a 9.5% increase from ₹298.8 under MGNREGA.

1.3 Who Pays Now — The 60:40 Shift

Verified Fact Source: VB-G RAM G Act, Section 22(1); Indian Express, May 27, 2026. Confidence: 100

Under MGNREGA, the central government bore 100% of the wage cost for unskilled labour. State governments shared only material and administrative costs (25% of material component).

Under VB-G RAM G, the funding architecture is fundamentally different:

OLD: MGNREGA

Centre 100%

States paid 0% of wages. Centre bore the entire wage bill.

NEW: VB-G RAM G

Centre 60% · State 40%

States must now budget for 40% of wage costs. NE/Himalayan states: 90:10.

Additional financial rules:

  • Material component capped at 40% at the district level
  • Normative allocation determined by 16th Finance Commission formula
  • Any expenditure beyond normative allocation must be borne entirely by the state
  • A portion of allocation may be withheld and redistributed based on performance (timely wages, social audit compliance, work completion)

3. The Debate

The Government's Record

Union Rural Development Minister Shivraj Singh Chouhan called the rollout "a historic and unprecedented day," stating: "This is not just an act, but a new chapter of dignity, self-respect, and secure livelihood for crores of our rural worker brothers and sisters."

The government's case:

  • More days, more money. 125 days vs 100 is a 25% increase. The wage floor ensures no worker is paid below ₹300 anywhere.
  • Asset creation. Four work categories — water security, core rural infrastructure, livelihood infrastructure, extreme weather mitigation.
  • Technology for transparency. Smart job cards with face recognition, geo-tagged works, DBT wage payments reduce leakages.
  • Village-level planning. Viksit Gram Panchayat Plans (VGPPs) give Gram Sabhas a formal role.
  • Seamless transition. 25 states allocated funds. Existing job cards remain valid. Ongoing works carried over.

The Critics' Case

Five opposition-ruled states — Karnataka, Kerala, Punjab, Telangana, Jharkhand — passed resolutions opposing the Act. Labour rights activists and economists raised structural concerns:

  • Rights-based to supply-driven. MGNREGA was a demand-driven, open-ended entitlement. VB-G RAM G replaces this with normative allocations — a capped budget. If demand exceeds allocation, the state must fund the excess.
  • Fiscal burden on states. The 40% wage cost share is a new, recurring expenditure. For Bihar — where 43% of rural households depend on the scheme — this means an estimated ₹3,500-5,000 crore additional annual burden.
  • Digitisation as barrier. Smart job cards with face-recognition attendance may exclude elderly, illiterate, and disabled workers. While the government says workers cannot be denied employment due to pending e-KYC, activists warn of de facto exclusion.
  • Performance-linked allocation. Withholding funds based on "performance" could penalise states with weaker administrative capacity — precisely those with the highest rural poverty.
  • Unemployment allowance diluted. Under MGNREGA, the Centre paid if work was not provided. Under VB-G RAM G, the state pays — at 1/4th wage for first 30 days, 1/2 thereafter.
  • 60-day seasonal pause. Critics argue this could be used to deny work during months when rural households need it most.

The question is not whether more days and a wage floor are good. They are. The question is whether the structural changes — the 40% state share, the normative allocation cap, the performance-linked withholding, the seasonal pause — will, in practice, reduce access for the poorest households in the poorest states.

4. The Bigger Picture

Two Decades of Rural Employment Policy

Historical Record Source: PRS India; World Bank India Social Protection Overview. Confidence: 100

MGNREGA was enacted in 2005 as a rights-based response to rural distress. It created a justiciable right to work, was demand-driven (uncapped), and funded almost entirely by the Centre. For its first decade, the scheme was widely praised for reducing rural poverty, empowering women, and creating productive assets. The World Bank called it "the largest public works programme in the world."

From 2015 onward, budgetary constraints, administrative tightening, and shifting political priorities reduced effectiveness. Real wages stagnated. Payment delays became chronic. The average days worked per household fell from 54 in 2009-10 to 50 in 2024-25.

VB-G RAM G represents both an expansion (more days, wage floor) and a restructuring (funding shift, capped allocation).

The Fiscal Context

India’s total expenditure on MGNREGA peaked at ₹1.11 lakh crore in 2020-21 (COVID year). As a percentage of GDP, it averaged 0.3-0.4% — modest by international standards for social protection.

The ₹95,692 crore VB-G RAM G allocation for FY27 is approximately 0.28% of India’s projected GDP ($4.15 tn). With states contributing an estimated ₹38,000 crore (40% wage share), total programme spending could reach ₹1.34 lakh crore.

THE BOTTOM LINE

MGNREGA was not a perfect programme. It suffered from payment delays, administrative corruption, and declining real wages. But it was a rights-based entitlement: if a rural household demanded work, the government was legally obliged to provide it.

VB-G RAM G expands the guarantee in days (125) and establishes a wage floor (₹300) — both genuine improvements. But it transforms the fundamental character of the programme from an open-ended, demand-driven entitlement to a capped, supply-driven scheme with shared funding.

The question is not whether more days and a wage floor are good. They are. The question is whether the structural changes — the 40% state share, the normative allocation cap, the performance-linked withholding, the seasonal pause — will, in practice, reduce access for the poorest households in the poorest states.

The answer depends on whether states can pay their share and whether the Centre’s normative allocations match actual demand. The first quarterly wage data, due in September 2026, will provide the earliest signal.
