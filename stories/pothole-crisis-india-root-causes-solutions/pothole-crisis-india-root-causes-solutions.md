## Cold Open

Between 2020 and 2024, 9,438 people lost their lives on Indian roads because of potholes. That is six people every single day â€” a number that has barely registered in the national conversation. Road accidents overall kill more than 150,000 Indians annually, but pothole-related deaths are distinct: they are almost entirely preventable. The engineering to build roads that last exists. The materials are available. The knowhow is not the bottleneck.

The bottleneck is a system that rewards contractors for laying asphalt cheaply, penalises no one when it washes away in the first monsoon, and leaves citizens with no practical way to demand repair. India does not have a pothole problem. It has a governance problem.

## The Scale of the Crisis

The 9,438 figure comes from National Crime Records Bureau data compiled across five financial years. The actual number is almost certainly higher, because pothole-related accidents are often classified under generic categories like "skidding" or "loss of control". A two-wheeler hitting a water-filled crater at night â€” the most common pothole fatality scenario â€” rarely leaves a record that names the pothole as the cause.

The geography of the crisis tracks closely with India's monsoon patterns. States with heavy rainfall â€” Kerala, Maharashtra, Karnataka, West Bengal, Assam â€” consistently report the highest numbers. But the problem is not limited to the wet season. During dry months, potholes are filled with loose gravel and dust, causing vehicles to swerve and collide.

In 2023, the Ministry of Road Transport and Highways admitted that nearly 10 per cent of India's national highways had "poor" or "very poor" pavement condition. State highways and rural roads, which account for the vast majority of the network, are significantly worse.

## Why Technology Isn't the Answer

There is no shortage of technical solutions available to Indian road agencies. Polymer-modified bitumen, which mixes plastic waste into asphalt, produces roads that last two to three times longer than conventional surfaces. Cold-mix asphalt allows pothole repairs to be done in the rain. Geotextile reinforcement prevents reflective cracking. Sensor-based road assessment vehicles can survey entire state highway networks in days rather than months.

All of these technologies are commercially available in India. Many have been mandated in central government guidelines for years. The National Rural Roads Development Agency has published detailed specifications for climate-resilient pavement design. The Indian Roads Congress has issued codes for every conceivable road type and climate zone.

The gap between what is specified and what is actually built is not a technical gap. It is a gap in enforcement.

## The Real Problem: Governance

Road construction and maintenance in India is contracted out through a tender system that has deep structural flaws. Contracts are awarded to the lowest bidder almost by default. The lowest bidder, in turn, has every incentive to cut corners â€” thinner asphalt layers, poorer compaction, substandard materials â€” because the inspection regime is weak and the penalties for poor workmanship are rarely imposed.

When a road fails within the defect liability period â€” typically five years for national highways â€” the contractor is supposed to bear the cost of repair. In practice, contractors exploit legal loopholes, arbitration delays, and political connections to avoid liability. A 2024 report by the Comptroller and Auditor General of India found that more than â‚¹12,000 crore in penalty amounts were pending recovery from defaulting road contractors across just 10 states.

The other half of the governance problem is maintenance funding. Indian states spend roughly 0.5 per cent of their road asset value on maintenance each year, against a recommended benchmark of 2 to 3 per cent. The shortfall means that even well-built roads deteriorate rapidly once small cracks appear, because there is no money to seal them before monsoon season turns them into craters.

## Accountability and Citizens

The third leg of the stool is accountability â€” or the lack of it. The typical Indian citizen has no reliable mechanism to report a pothole and see it fixed within a reasonable timeframe. Centralised grievance portals exist but are poorly publicised, understaffed, and produce no binding timeline for action. A 2025 RTI-based study across 12 municipal corporations found that only 17 per cent of pothole complaints received a response within 30 days.

Some states have experimented with better models. Kerala's Suchitwa Mission and the Haritha Keralam Mission have deployed rapid-response road repair teams with decentralised budgets. Gujarat's Road and Building Department introduced GPS-tagged pothole tracking and contractor scorecards. In both cases, the result was a measurable decline in repeat complaints.

## What Will Actually Fix Them

The fixes are known. They have been proven in pilot programmes, in academic studies, and in the handful of Indian states that have shown improvement. The first requirement is independent quality audits â€” paid for by the state but conducted by third-party engineering firms with public reporting. The second is automatic penalty deduction from contractor bills when defect liability is triggered, without waiting for court orders. The third is ring-fenced maintenance budgets indexed to road asset value, so that routine repairs are not cut when other departments overspend.

None of these require new legislation. All three are within the executive power of state governments today. What is missing is political will â€” the willingness to tell contractors that the old way of doing business is finished.

Until that changes, six Indians will keep dying on the roads every day. And each death will be as preventable as the one before it.
