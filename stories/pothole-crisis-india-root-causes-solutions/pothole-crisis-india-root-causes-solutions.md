## Cold Open

Between 2020 and 2024, 9,438 people died on Indian roads because of potholes — six every single day, a death toll that has drawn remarkably little national attention. Road accidents overall kill over 150,000 Indians annually, but pothole-related fatalities are distinct: they are almost entirely preventable. The engineering, materials, and construction techniques to build durable roads are commercially available in India today.

The bottleneck is not technical. It is a system that awards road contracts to the lowest bidder, imposes negligible penalties for substandard work, and leaves citizens with no practical avenue to demand repair. India does not have a pothole problem. It has a governance problem.

## The Scale of the Crisis

The figure of 9,438 comes from National Crime Records Bureau data across five financial years and is almost certainly an undercount. Pothole-related accidents are frequently classified under generic categories such as "skidding" or "loss of control." The most common fatal scenario — a two-wheeler hitting a water-filled crater at night — rarely produces a record that identifies the pothole as the cause.

The geography of the crisis tracks closely with India's monsoon patterns. States with heavy rainfall — Kerala, Maharashtra, Karnataka, West Bengal, Assam — report the highest numbers. But the problem persists year-round: during dry months, potholes fill with loose gravel and dust, causing vehicles to swerve and collide.

In 2023, the Ministry of Road Transport and Highways admitted that nearly 10 per cent of India's national highways had "poor" or "very poor" pavement condition. State highways and rural roads, which constitute the vast majority of the network, are significantly worse.

## Why Technology Isn't the Answer

Polymer-modified bitumen, which mixes plastic waste into asphalt, produces roads that last two to three times longer than conventional surfaces. Cold-mix asphalt allows pothole repairs in the rain. Geotextile reinforcement prevents reflective cracking. Sensor-based road assessment vehicles can survey entire state highway networks in days.

All of these technologies are commercially available in India. Many have been mandated in central government guidelines for years. The National Rural Roads Development Agency has published detailed specifications for climate-resilient pavement design. The Indian Roads Congress has issued codes for every conceivable road type and climatic zone.

The gap between what is specified and what is actually built is not a technical gap. It is a gap in enforcement.

## The Real Problem: Governance

Road construction and maintenance in India is contracted through a tender system with deep structural flaws. Contracts are awarded to the lowest bidder almost by default. The lowest bidder has every incentive to cut corners — thinner asphalt layers, poorer compaction, substandard materials — because inspections are weak and penalties for poor workmanship are rarely imposed.

When a road fails within the defect liability period — typically five years for national highways — the contractor is supposed to bear the repair cost. In practice, contractors exploit legal loopholes, arbitration delays, and political connections to avoid liability. A 2024 Comptroller and Auditor General report found that over ₹12,000 crore in penalty amounts were pending recovery from defaulting road contractors across just ten states.

The other half of the governance problem is maintenance funding. Indian states spend roughly 0.5 per cent of their road asset value on maintenance each year, against a recommended benchmark of 2 to 3 per cent. The shortfall means that even well-built roads deteriorate rapidly once small cracks appear — there is no money to seal them before monsoon season turns them into craters.

## Accountability and Citizens

The typical Indian citizen has no reliable mechanism to report a pothole and see it fixed within a reasonable timeframe. Centralised grievance portals exist but are poorly publicised, understaffed, and produce no binding timeline for action. A 2025 RTI-based study across 12 municipal corporations found that only 17 per cent of pothole complaints received a response within 30 days.

Some states have demonstrated better models. Kerala's Suchitwa Mission deployed rapid-response road repair teams with decentralised budgets. Gujarat's Road and Building Department introduced GPS-tagged pothole tracking and contractor scorecards. In both cases, repeat complaints declined measurably.

## What Will Actually Fix Them

The solutions are known and proven in pilot programmes. First: independent quality audits — paid for by the state but conducted by third-party engineering firms with public reporting. Second: automatic penalty deduction from contractor bills when defect liability is triggered, without waiting for court orders. Third: ring-fenced maintenance budgets indexed to road asset value.

None of these require new legislation. All three are within the executive power of state governments today. What is missing is political will — the willingness to tell contractors that the old way of doing business is finished. Until that changes, six Indians will keep dying on the roads every day. Each death is as preventable as the one before it.
