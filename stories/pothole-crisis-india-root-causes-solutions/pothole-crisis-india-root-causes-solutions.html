Why Potholes Keep Killing 6 Indians Every Day — And What Will Actually Fix Them

Between 2020 and 2024, potholes killed 9,438 people in India — a 53% rise. The central government spends only 1-2% of its road budget on maintenance. Countries like Singapore, Japan and the UK have solved this through governance reform, not new technology. India already has the tools. What it lacks is accountability.

TB The Breakdown Editorial Team June 28, 2026 14 min read Fact-checked

What happened

Pothole deaths rose 53% in five years (2020-2024), with 9,438 fatalities. Uttar Pradesh accounts for 54% of all pothole deaths. MoRTH data shows 23,056 accidents and 19,956 injuries over the same period.

Why it matters

India spends Rs 2.87 lakh crore annually on roads but allocates just 1-2% to maintenance — far below the NITI Aayog-recommended 10%. The same roads are built, neglected, and rebuilt in a self-perpetuating cycle that costs thousands of lives.

Key takeaway

The solutions are not technical. India's IRC codes match global standards. CSIR-CRRI has developed cold-mix pothole repair machines and 20-minute ECOFIX material. The problem is incentive design: L1 bidding, short defect liability, fragmented accountability, and weak enforcement. Countries that solved this did so through governance reform, not silver bullets.

The Problem

On December 21, 2024, Vinoth, a 36-year-old software engineer, was riding home on Chennai's GST Road when his motorcycle hit a pothole on the national highway. He fell under a truck and died. NHAI filled the pothole within minutes of his death.

That stretch of road — the Perungalathur section of GST Road — has claimed over 200 lives in five years while collecting more than Rs 600 crore in tolls. The money for maintenance was there. The accountability was not.

Vinoth is one of 9,438 people killed in pothole-related road accidents in India between 2020 and 2024, according to data the Ministry of Road Transport and Highways (MoRTH) submitted to the Lok Sabha on February 12, 2026. The toll has risen every year except the COVID lockdown year, from 1,555 in 2020 to 2,385 in 2024 — a 53% increase.

Pothole deaths in India rose 53% between 2020 and 2024. The only decline was during the COVID lockdown year of 2021. Source: MoRTH Lok Sabha Reply, February 12, 2026

Uttar Pradesh alone accounts for 54.3% of all pothole deaths — 5,127 fatalities over five years, including 1,369 in 2024 alone. Madhya Pradesh (969 deaths), Tamil Nadu (612), Odisha (425), Punjab (414), and Assam (395) follow. Four states — Andhra Pradesh, Bihar, Goa, and Chandigarh — reported zero pothole accidents, a data quality issue experts say masks the true scale.

Every death documented by MoRTH represents a person. In 2025 alone: Priyanka Kumari Poonia, 26, a banker in Bengaluru whose brother braked to avoid a pothole, throwing her under a truck. Raj Niranjan Singh, 19, a college student in Bhiwandi crushed by a container truck after his two-wheeler skidded on a pothole. Dhanushree, 20, a BCom student in Bengaluru who fell trying to avoid a pothole and was run over by a tipper truck. Rashid Khan, 37, who drowned after his motorcycle hit a water-filled pothole in Delhi. Aiyaz Shemale, 21, a techie in Bhiwandi dragged 4-5 meters under a truck after hitting a pothole.

🔧 THE FIX: SCALE OF THE PROBLEM

India has the world's second-largest road network at 6.7 million km. Road accidents are the 8th leading cause of death in the country. Pothole deaths as a share of total road fatalities rose from ~0.9% in 2020 to ~1.35% in 2024. But this is almost certainly an undercount — four large states report zero pothole accidents, which crash data experts call implausible.

Why It Exists

Potholes are not a monsoon problem. They are a systems failure with eight distinct root causes that interact in a self-reinforcing cycle.

1. Drainage failure. Standing water can reduce pavement life by up to 40%, according to CSIR-CRRI research. Poor or obstructed surface and sub-surface drainage is the primary trigger for pothole formation. India's monsoon climate makes this the single most important factor, yet drainage is routinely neglected in road design and maintenance.

2. The L1 (Lowest Bidder) system. India's public procurement system awards road contracts to the lowest bidder. Contractors quote unrealistically low prices, then cut corners on materials, thickness, and compaction to maintain margins. The CAG has repeatedly flagged this. Deepak Baskandi's 2026 study in IJEDR showed L1 bidding is directly correlated with premature road failure.

3. Multi-layered sub-contracting. A Parliamentary Committee report (August 2025) criticised NHAI's practice of allowing main contractors to sub-lease work at lower costs, creating a "profit without responsibility" chain. Each sub-contracting layer extracts profit without adding accountability.

4. Short defect liability periods. Until October 2024, the defect liability period (DLP) for EPC road contracts was just 5 years. Gadkari himself admitted "within 3 years, there are lots of problems on roads." The government announced doubling DLP to 10 years, but implementation is still in progress.

5. Perverse incentives. The most revealing story comes from Pune's JM Road: a contractor built such high-quality work that the road lasted for years without needing repairs. When new tenders were issued, he couldn't win them because his work was too durable. The system rewards failure — patchwork generates steady revenue, durable fixes reduce future contracts.

6. Corruption. The CAG flagged Rs 297 crore in unnecessary expenditure on Maharashtra road projects in March 2026, including Rs 34.56 crore in excess insurance charges. Works started without forest clearances, independent engineers were not appointed during O&M phases, and monitoring targets at Chief Engineer level did not exist.

7. Overloaded vehicles. CRRI estimates that severe overloading on Indian highways can accelerate pavement failure by 10 to 20 times. A truck overloaded by 20% inflicts more than double the damage — rutting appears first in truck lanes, channels rainwater, and deepens failure.

8. Inadequate maintenance budget. India spends approximately 1-2% of its road budget on maintenance. The US spends 48%. Italy spends 72%. NITI Aayog recommends 10%. The gap is not marginal — it is structural.

The System Behind It

India's roads are governed by a fragmented jurisdictional maze. National highways fall under MoRTH/NHAI. State highways and major district roads belong to state PWDs. Urban roads are under municipal corporations. Rural roads fall to Panchayati Raj institutions and PMGSY. When a citizen reports a pothole, four different agencies can plausibly say "not our jurisdiction."

The road construction lifecycle has at least four break points where accountability fails — from L1 contractor selection to defect liability expiry

Road contracts in India follow four main models. EPC (Engineering, Procurement, Construction) covers about 48-50% of national highway awards — fully government-funded, with contractors providing construction, and until recently a 5-year defect liability period. HAM (Hybrid Annuity Model) covers roughly 50%, with the government paying 40% during construction and the remaining 60% as annuity over the concession period. BOT (Build-Operate-Transfer) is now rare at just 1.75% of NH length. PBMC (Performance-Based Maintenance Contracts) exist for the 62% of the national highway network not under any defect liability or concession period.

The result: only 38% (55,448 km) of the national highway network is under defect liability or concession. The remaining 62% is maintained through short-term contracts that lack accountability mechanisms. Contractors escape penalties because defects surface after the liability period expires. Authority engineers rarely face consequences for quality lapses.

Who Is Responsible

The chain of accountability runs from the Union Minister for Road Transport through NHAI chairpersons, state PWD ministers, chief engineers, municipal commissioners, and ultimately to the junior engineer who inspects the road. But at every level, the system diffuses responsibility rather than concentrating it.

Union Government (MoRTH/NHAI): Sets policy, allocates budget, awards national highway contracts. Maintenance budget allocation has remained at 1-2% despite repeated recommendations to increase it. NHAI's debt stood at Rs 3,42,086 crore as of June 2023.

State PWDs: Responsible for state highways and major district roads. Vastly varying quality standards and transparency levels between states. Uttar Pradesh's 54% share of pothole deaths is not a coincidence.

Municipal Corporations: Responsible for urban roads. The most visible layer — citizens see potholes on city streets and blame the municipal corporation. But urban roads receive the smallest share of funding relative to traffic load.

Contractors: The entities that actually build and repair roads. The L1 system selects for the cheapest bid, not the best quality. The CAG has documented that even the low-quality work is rarely checked — self-certification by contractors is common, independent quality checks are inconsistent.

Citizens: Bear the cost in deaths, vehicle damage, lost productivity — and pay taxes that fund the system. The ultimate responsible party, with the weakest tools for enforcement.

🔧 THE FIX: THE JM ROAD PARABLE

A famous anecdote from Pune illustrates the systemic perversity. A contractor built JM Road so well that it lasted for years without a single pothole. When the municipal corporation issued new tenders for road maintenance, this contractor could not win them. His work was too durable — it eliminated the need for the ongoing patchwork contracts that keep other contractors in business. India's procurement system rewards failure.

How Other Countries Solved It

Singapore: AI-powered predictive maintenance. The Land Transport Authority uses a Road Maintenance Management System with AI and video analytics mounted on regular vehicles. Detection accuracy exceeds 90%, and inspection productivity has increased 60%. Expressways are resurfaced every 7-10 years on a preventive schedule. The result: potholes dropped 38.8% between 2022 and 2025, from ~3,200 to ~1,800. Response times are measured in hours, not weeks.

Japan: All-weather repair materials. IKEE Co. has developed cold-mix road repair materials that can be installed simply by inserting into holes and compacting — no heavy equipment, no hot mix plants, works in rain and standing water. Toshiba has demonstrated weakly-supervised AI for pothole detection with 84% accuracy at 80-100 km/h speeds and only 4% false positive rate.

United Kingdom: Performance-based contracting. The UK uses NEC3/4 output specification contracts where contractors are paid for outcomes, not inputs. Defect liability periods extend up to 3 years for standard works, with latent defects liability up to 12 years in some contracts. Performance incentives of up to £300,000 per year motivate contractors to meet targets. Surrey County Council found that 38% of potholes were identified and repaired proactively — before any public report.

Germany: Legally binding standards. Germany's FGSV develops unified technical standards with legal backing. Mandatory quality control tests occur every 3,000-6,000 square metres. Condition recording is standardised nationwide. Research institutions like BASt are independent and influential. And critically, Germany ring-fences maintenance funding.

The key difference is not technology. India's CRRI has developed cold-mix pothole repair machines, ECOFIX for waterlogged conditions, and two-pack on-site filling mixes. The IRC codes match global standards. India has 26 Indian Road Congress codes on drainage alone. What India lacks is ring-fenced maintenance budgets, performance-based contracting, and real accountability for contractors and officials.

What Government Can Do

Immediate (within 30 days): Issue a circular mandating all pending pothole complaints on municipal and PG Portal to be resolved within 48 hours, with geo-tagged photographic evidence of completion. Activate emergency pothole response teams in all municipal corporations before the next monsoon.

Short-term (within 6 months): Implement the 10-year defect liability period for all new EPC contracts, not just national highways. Mandate independent quality checks by third-party agencies for all road contracts above Rs 10 crore, with results made publicly available. Appoint independent engineers for all HAM projects during O&M phases (CAG found this was not happening).

Medium-term (within 1-2 years): Increase maintenance budget allocation to at least 5% of MoRTH's total budget — a first step toward NITI Aayog's recommended 10%. Deploy AI-driven road condition monitoring on all national highways. Establish a single national pothole reporting portal that routes complaints to the correct jurisdiction automatically (existing prototypes like Kerala's Kuzhiyundo prove this is feasible).

Long-term (within 5 years): Reform the L1 procurement system to require quality-weighting in bid evaluation, as recommended by multiple CAG reports. Ring-fence maintenance funding through a dedicated road maintenance fund with hypothecated fuel tax revenue. Scale performance-based maintenance contracts across the entire NH network. Debar contractors with catastrophic failure records for at least 3 years (new rules from March 2026 exist but enforcement will determine effectiveness).

There are precedents for every one of these recommendations. Delhi's June 2026 Rs 657 crore road repair plan includes geo-tagged tracking, 5-year contractor liability, and a 48-hour pothole fix guarantee. The new catastrophic failure classification (March 2026) allows 30-mark deduction in contractor ratings for pavement failures requiring full-depth replacement within 5 years. These are exactly the right ideas — they need to be scaled nationally and enforced consistently.

What Citizens Can Do

1. File a complaint on the municipal portal. Mumbai's Pothole QuickFix app (BMC), Bengaluru's BBMP Fix My Street, and Karnataka's PCRS all accept geo-tagged photo complaints with real-time tracking. Kerala's community-driven Kuzhiyundo map automatically identifies the responsible authority using OpenStreetMap data. Screenshot your complaint number.

2. Escalate through the PG Portal. If the municipal portal fails, the central government's PG Portal (pgportal.gov.in) allows any citizen to file a grievance against any government department. CPGRAMS is the parallel system for central government services.

3. File an RTI. Ask the local PWD or municipal engineer: Who has jurisdiction over this road? Has a repair work order been issued? Who is the contractor? What is the last inspection report? A template is available on righttoinformation.wiki.

4. Use social media. #YeThikKarkeDikhao (47,000+ followers, started July 2025) is a civic movement where citizens post geo-tagged photos of broken infrastructure and tag local politicians. Politicians have responded — Rajasthan Minister Rajyavardhan Rathore inspected a drainage site after being tagged. #FixItIfYouCan is the English version. #KnowYourRoad demands QR codes on all roads showing contractor name, project cost, and maintenance agency. A volunteer built a working "Know My Road" app prototype in 48 hours.

5. Escalate legally. Seven-step escalation: Complaint on portal → Municipal Commissioner (7 days) → RTI application → Consumer forum (National Consumer Helpline 1915 for service deficiency) → Lok Ayukta (for corruption) → High Court Article 226 (for systemic non-action) → PIL (for sector-wide issues).

Estimated Cost

India spent Rs 17,900 crore on highway maintenance between 2021 and 2024 — averaging Rs 6,000 crore per year against a total MoRTH budget that exceeded Rs 2.7 lakh crore annually. That is approximately 1% of the road budget spent on keeping existing roads safe.

India spends ~1-2% of its road budget on maintenance. Italy spends 72%. Austria 61%. The US 48%. NITI Aayog recommends 10%. Source: PRS India, ITF-OECD

The economic cost of poor road conditions is far higher than the cost of maintenance. The TCI-IIM Kolkata study found that India incurs $21.3 billion (approximately Rs 1.77 lakh crore) annually in delays and additional fuel consumption due to poor road conditions — $6.6 billion in delays and $14.7 billion in additional fuel. Bosch India estimated the total socio-economic cost of road accidents at $15.71-38.81 billion (0.55-1.35% of GDP), with vehicle damage alone costing $0.57-1.81 billion.

The gap between what India spends on maintenance (Rs 4,595 crore in FY25-26) and what NITI Aayog recommends (10% of Rs 2.87 lakh crore = Rs 28,700 crore) is approximately Rs 24,000 crore per year. That is about 0.08% of GDP — a fraction of the $21.3 billion (Rs 1.77 lakh crore) India loses annually to poor road conditions.

🔧 THE FIX: THE ARITHMETIC

India loses approximately Rs 1.77 lakh crore per year to poor road conditions (TCI-IIM). The additional maintenance funding needed to meet NITI Aayog's 10% recommendation is roughly Rs 24,000 crore. For every rupee India does not spend on maintenance, it loses approximately Rs 7 in economic costs. Not maintaining roads is the expensive choice.

Potential Impact

If India adopted Singapore's preventive maintenance approach — AI-driven inspections, mandatory resurfacing schedules, and performance-based contracts — the potential impact over five years would be substantial.

Lives saved: Singapore reduced potholes by 38.8% in three years. Applied to India, a similar reduction could prevent approximately 900 deaths annually (38.8% of 2,385 = 925). Over five years: 4,500-5,000 lives.

Economic savings: Every percentage point increase in maintenance budget (from 1% to 2% of MoRTH spending = approximately Rs 2,800 crore additional) could reduce the $21.3 billion economic loss by an estimated 5-10% based on international data — saving Rs 8,000-17,000 crore annually.

Vehicle damage reduction: Bosch's vehicle damage estimate of $0.57-1.81 billion annually could be reduced by 20-30% with consistent road quality, saving Rs 9,500-45,000 crore over five years.

Fuel savings: A Pune city study using HDM-4 modeling found that timely maintenance could reduce fuel consumption by 2.32% — saving 0.128 million litres per 1,000 vehicle-km over a 15-year period. Scaled nationally, the fuel savings alone would run into thousands of crores.
