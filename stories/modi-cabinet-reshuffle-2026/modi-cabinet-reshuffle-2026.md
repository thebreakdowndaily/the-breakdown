# Modi Cabinet Reshuffle 2026

## Cold Open

On June 23, 2026, Prime Minister Narendra Modi met President Droupadi Murmu at Rashtrapati Bhavan in New Delhi, setting in motion what is expected to be the most consequential reorganisation of his third-term council of ministers. Hours earlier, George Kurian, the Minister of State for Minority Affairs, had resigned â€” the first domino to fall in a sequence that closely follows the pattern Modi established with reshuffles in July 2016 and July 2021. This time, however, the stakes are considerably higher: the reshuffle is widely expected to be the last major reorganisation of the Union Council of Ministers before the 2029 general elections, and it comes at a moment when the BJP is simultaneously managing a historic influx of defectors from opposition parties, preparing for a crucial set of assembly elections due in 2027, and signalling its intent to consolidate power ahead of the 2029 contest.

## The Reshuffle

The process began in earnest in mid-June 2026, with speculation that Modi would follow the precedent set by his earlier reshuffles. On June 16, Harsh Malhotra was appointed president of the Delhi BJP unit, a move that required his eventual removal from the Union council. On June 18, the BJP announced its Rajya Sabha candidate list, notably excluding Ravneet Bittu and George Kurian, signalling that their ministerial careers were drawing to a close. Their Rajya Sabha terms expired on June 21. By June 23, Kurian had resigned as Minister of State, and Modi had met the President to formally set the reshuffle machinery in motion. On June 24, the Union Cabinet met in what was widely interpreted as a preparatory session for the broader reorganisation. The reshuffle is expected to be formally announced in the days preceding the Monsoon Session of Parliament, which is scheduled for July.

## Key Appointments

The reshuffle is being driven in large part by the need to accommodate a historic wave of defectors who have joined the BJP and its allied parties in recent months. In April 2026, seven Aam Aadmi Party Rajya Sabha MPs â€” including prominent faces such as Raghav Chadha, former cricketer Harbhajan Singh, and Sandeep Pathak â€” merged with the BJP, and all are expected to receive ministerial positions. In June 2026, twenty Trinamool Congress rebel MPs merged with the Nationalist Congress Party (Indrajit), signalling their intent to join the National Democratic Alliance; among them, Kakoli Ghosh Dastidar and Sudeep Bandyopadhyay are tipped for berths. Six Shiv Sena (UBT) MPs joined the Eknath Shinde-led Shiv Sena as part of what has been dubbed "Operation Tiger," providing Maharashtra with additional ministerial representation. The reshuffle is also expected to address the current gender imbalance in the council â€” only seven of the current seventy-two ministers are women â€” and to introduce younger, Generation Z faces to appeal to first-time voters ahead of 2029.

## Portfolio Changes

The most significant portfolio change under discussion is the potential elevation of Piyush Goyal from Commerce to Finance, replacing Nirmala Sitharaman, who has held the finance portfolio since 2019. Ashwini Vaishnaw, currently Minister of Railways and Electronics and Information Technology, may be moved to Commerce. Dharmendra Pradhan's position is understood to be uncertain following the NEET paper leak controversy that has caused significant political embarrassment for the government. Devendra Fadnavis, currently in Maharashtra, is being discussed for a possible return to the Centre. Several Ministers of State are expected to be dropped for underperformance â€” the Prime Minister's Office has compiled detailed performance data on every minister, and the message is clear: perform, or be replaced.

## Political Significance

This reshuffle is driven by three interconnected political forces. First, the need to accommodate the largest influx of defectors from opposition parties in Indian political history, rewarding them with ministerial positions while integrating them into the NDA's governance framework. Second, the imperative to signal intent for the 2027 assembly elections in key states including Uttar Pradesh, Punjab, Gujarat, Uttarakhand, Goa, Manipur, and Himachal Pradesh â€” elections that will serve as a semi-final before the 2029 general election. Third, the need to build a team capable of delivering a fourth consecutive term for Modi in 2029, which would require a council of ministers that combines performance orientation, regional representation, and demographic appeal. The reshuffle is, in essence, the beginning of the 2029 campaign.
