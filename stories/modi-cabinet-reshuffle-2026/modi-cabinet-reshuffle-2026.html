Modi Cabinet Reshuffle 2026 — The Last Big Rejig Before 2029

TB The Breakdown June 26, 2026 10 min read
Evolving Story As of June 26, 2026 — No Official Swearing-In Yet

This is a live, unfolding story. The cabinet reshuffle is imminent — Home Minister Amit Shah reportedly met President Murmu on June 25–26 to finalise the date — but no official swearing-in has been scheduled by Rashtrapati Bhavan. Six independent sources point to June 28–29 as the likely window, but this remains unconfirmed. This article distinguishes between verified facts (high confidence), reported claims (medium confidence), and speculation (low confidence). We will update as events firm up.

On the evening of June 23, Prime Minister Narendra Modi met President Droupadi Murmu at Rashtrapati Bhavan. The President's Office shared pictures on X: "Prime Minister Shri @narendramodi called on President Droupadi Murmu at Rashtrapati Bhavan." On the same platform that day, Commerce Minister Piyush Goyal posted his farewell to USTR Jamieson Greer — a scheduling coincidence that Delhi's political grapevine treated as anything but. Hours earlier, the President had accepted the resignation of George Kurian, Minister of State for Minority Affairs, Fisheries, Animal Husbandry & Dairying. The sequence — a resignation, then a meeting with the constitutional head of state — followed a pattern Modi has used twice before: in July 2016 and July 2021, when he reshuffled his council of ministers just after completing two years in office. This time, the stakes are higher. The reshuffle expected in the coming days will be the first major reorganisation of Modi's third term — and likely the last before the 2029 Lok Sabha elections.

The Ministers Leaving

Four ministers are confirmed or expected to exit in this reshuffle. Their departures fall into three categories: those whose Rajya Sabha terms expired without renewal, those who have taken party organisational roles, and those being moved for political strategy.

Confirmed exit: Kurian is the only minister whose resignation the President has formally accepted. Bittu's RS term has ended and his exclusion from the BJP's Rajya Sabha candidate list (announced June 4/18) signals his ministerial tenure is effectively over, but unlike Kurian, his formal resignation has not been published by Rashtrapati Bhavan as of June 26. Bittu has stated he intends to contest the 2027 Punjab Assembly elections. Malhotra and Chaudhary are expected to resign under the BJP's "one man, one post" principle after being assigned state party leadership roles — Malhotra as Delhi BJP president (appointed May 28) and Chaudhary as UP BJP president (appointed December 2025). Beyond these four, insiders say at least half a dozen more ministers could be dropped, though none of these additional names have been confirmed through official channels.

The Defectors Being Rewarded

The most striking feature of this reshuffle is the number of opposition politicians who have crossed over to the BJP and its allies and are now in line for ministerial positions. The influx totals at least 33 opposition MPs — including defectors from the Aam Aadmi Party, Trinamool Congress, and Shiv Sena (UBT) — in what amounts to the largest absorption of rival politicians into the NDA fold since Modi took office.

AAP Defectors (7 MPs — HIGH confidence)

On April 24, seven Rajya Sabha MPs from the Aam Aadmi Party "merged" with the BJP, confirmed by BJP national president Nitin Nabin on X. The full list: Raghav Chadha, Sandeep Pathak, Ashok Mittal, Harbhajan Singh, Swati Maliwal, Vikram Sahney, and Rajinder Gupta — six from Punjab and Maliwal from Delhi. Chadha, a former AAP star who has since praised Modi fulsomely, and Mittal, the LPU chancellor, are seen as frontrunners for ministerial berths, according to The Week (June 10) and The Statesman (June 26). Tarun Chugh, the BJP national general secretary who was nominated to the Rajya Sabha from Madhya Pradesh in Bittu's place, is also being positioned as a Punjab-face contender for a ministerial role, per NDTV and The Week.

TMC Rebels Join NCPI (20 MPs — HIGH confidence)

On June 14, 20 rebel Lok Sabha MPs from the Trinamool Congress, led by Kakoli Ghosh Dastidar and Sudip Bandyopadhyay, merged with the Nationalist Citizens Party of India (NCPI) — a Tripura-based party registered in January 2023 — and declared their support for the NDA. The move was preceded by Dastidar's election as NCPI president on May 30, after incumbent Shewly Kundu resigned — effectively the rebel MPs took over the party before announcing the merger (Times of India, June 16). The group claims a two-thirds majority of TMC's Lok Sabha strength (20 of 28 MPs), which under anti-defection law would permit a merger by two-thirds of members. TMC loyalist MPs Kirti Azad and Sagarika Ghose have challenged the merger in the Lok Sabha Speaker's court. Dastidar and Bandyopadhyay are reportedly under consideration for Cabinet-rank positions, though their absorption through a third-party mechanism (NCPI, not directly BJP) makes the path legally and politically complex.

Operation Tiger: Six Shiv Sena (UBT) MPs Defect (HIGH confidence)

Between June 17 and 22, six of nine Shiv Sena (UBT) Lok Sabha MPs formally joined Eknath Shinde's Shiv Sena in what the leaders themselves called "Operation Tiger." The six defectors — all of whom skipped the Sena (UBT) parliamentary party meeting in Delhi on June 16 — are:

  • Sanjay Deshmukh — Yavatmal (Ramtek) constituency
  • Sanjay Jadhav — Parbhani constituency
  • Sanjay Dina Patil — Mumbai North East constituency
  • Nagesh Patil-Ashtikar — Hingoli constituency
  • Omprakash Rajenimbalkar — Dharashiv (Osmanabad) constituency
  • Bhausaheb Wakchaure — Shirdi constituency

Deputy Chief Minister Eknath Shinde declared: "Operation Tiger is now complete and successful… My operations are foolproof." Chief Minister Devendra Fadnavis confirmed: "The operation has been successful, and the body is in very good health." The defections reduce Sena (UBT) to just 3 Lok Sabha MPs and make Shinde's Shiv Sena the second-largest party in Maharashtra's Lok Sabha contingent. All six MPs have pledged to contest 2029 on the Shiv Sena symbol. Expectations of cabinet representation for the group are high, with speculation that Shrikant Shinde (Eknath Shinde's son and Kalyan MP) could be inducted as a reward for Operation Tiger, though this remains low-confidence speculation.

The Big Portfolio Chessboard

The most consequential — and most uncertain — speculation revolves around the finance portfolio.

Low-to-medium confidence: Commerce Minister Piyush Goyal is among the candidates being discussed to replace Nirmala Sitharaman as Finance Minister. Goyal, who has led the India-US trade negotiations and built a strong rapport with USTR Jamieson Greer, is seen by the PMO as someone who can bring commercial pragmatism to the finance ministry at a time when the economy is projected to slow — India's FY26 GDP grew 7.7% (MoSPI official data, June 2026) but is forecast to decelerate to 6.6% in FY27 (RBI, World Bank consensus). The RBI's Monetary Policy Committee held the repo rate at 5.25% on June 5 amid the Iran-West Asia conflict and oil price volatility. However, sources are divided on Goyal: The Raisina Hills (June 24) reported that he is "likely to persist with Commerce," while Indian Mandarins (June 18) and other outlets have him tipped for Finance. OneIndia (June 26) floated former RBI Governor Shaktikanta Das as a possible Finance Minister — an unprecedented move from central bank governor to cabinet minister that remains low-confidence speculation from just two sources.

Low confidence: Sitharaman's fate is entirely unclear. Conflicting reports suggest she could be moved to Defence, Human Resource Development, or assigned an organisational role in the BJP. No official signal has emerged from the PMO. She presented her ninth consecutive Union Budget in February 2026 and remains Finance Minister as of this writing.

Medium confidence: Bihar Chief Minister Nitish Kumar, who was elected unopposed to the Rajya Sabha from Bihar in March 2026, is reportedly in line for a Cabinet berth — a move that would strengthen JD(U) representation at the Centre ahead of the 2027 state elections.

Low-to-medium confidence: Other major moves being discussed:

  • Ashwini Vaishnaw — currently holds three portfolios (Railways, I&B, Electronics & IT). The Federal (June 24) and Indian Mandarins mention him as a possible successor to Goyal at Commerce, or a candidate for Finance. He is expected to be divested of some portfolios regardless.
  • Dharmendra Pradhan — Education Minister under fire over the NEET-UG 2026 paper leak (CBI has arrested 13 people; the exam was cancelled May 12 and a re-exam held June 21). Multiple sources say his removal is being considered. However, The Raisina Hills reported Modi's birthday wish for Pradhan on June 26, suggesting the PM may still back him. Pradhan actively managed the NEET re-exam, giving interviews defending his position. His fate is genuinely uncertain.
  • Hardeep Puri & BL Verma — Their Rajya Sabha terms end on November 25, 2026. The reshuffle presents a natural decision point for the party on their future. OneIndia reports Puri "could be shown the door."
  • Anurag Thakur — The former minister (dropped in June 2024 when Modi 3.0 was formed) is being mentioned for a comeback, per multiple sources though with low-to-medium confidence.

Why This Reshuffle Matters

Three factors make this reorganisation different from the July 2021 or July 2016 exercises.

First, the defector influx. Modi's third-term government, constituted on June 10, 2024, had 72 ministers. The BJP fell short of a parliamentary majority (240 seats, short of 272), making NDA allies crucial. Since then, the party has absorbed 7 AAP MPs (April 24), 20 TMC rebel MPs via NCPI (June 14), and 6 Shiv Sena (UBT) defectors (June 17–22) — a total of 33 opposition MPs aligned with the NDA. The reshuffle must reward these newcomers without alienating existing allies — a delicate balance.

Second, the electoral calendar. Assembly elections are due in early 2027 in Uttar Pradesh, Punjab, Uttarakhand, Gujarat, Goa, Himachal Pradesh, and Manipur. The BJP has already asked state units to begin preparations in UP, Punjab, Goa, and Uttarakhand. The reshuffle is designed to signal to these states that they have a voice in the Centre. New faces from poll-bound states — particularly Punjab — are a near-certainty. Women's representation — currently just seven out of 72 ministers (9.7%) — is expected to increase, though the scale of increase is unclear. The Union Cabinet cleared amendments to the Women's Reservation Act (Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam) in April 2026, expanding the Lok Sabha from 543 to 816 seats with 273 seats (33%) reserved for women. The Act came into force on April 17, 2026. The current cabinet's 9.7% women's representation sits dramatically short of this target.

Third, the 2029 horizon. This is likely the last opportunity for a major Cabinet overhaul before the next general election. The 2024 reshuffle was just a formation. This one is a recalibration. According to a senior BJP functionary speaking to The Raisina Hills, the PMO has compiled detailed datasets on file pendency and ministerial performance — metrics that have been used to decide who stays and who goes.

"The PM seeks to jolt the ministers out of inertia. A sense has been felt within the government that some of the ministers have delivered far below performances. Many will be dropped."

Senior BJP functionary, speaking to The Raisina Hills (June 24, 2026)

The Precedent

Modi reshuffled his council of ministers on July 6, 2016 and July 7, 2021 — each time just over a month after completing two years in office. The 2021 reshuffle was the most extensive of his second term: 43 ministers sworn in (15 new Cabinet ministers including promotions for seven ministers of state, and 28 new ministers of state). Senior ministers including Ravi Shankar Prasad, Harsh Vardhan, Thawar Chand Gehlot, and Prakash Javadekar were dropped. Nirmala Sitharaman, Piyush Goyal, and Dharmendra Pradhan were elevated to Cabinet rank. The pattern suggests the PM prefers a single major mid-term overhaul rather than frequent minor changes.

This time, the organisational reset is happening in parallel. The BJP's new national office-bearers under party president Nitin Nabin — who was elected to the Rajya Sabha from Bihar in March 2026, replacing JP Nadda — are expected to be announced by June 30. Several outgoing ministers are expected to move into organisational roles, creating a revolving door between the government and the party apparatus. Harsh Malhotra's appointment as Delhi BJP president and Pankaj Chaudhary's as UP BJP president exemplify this pattern.

The Bottom Line

The Verdict

Modi's 2026 Cabinet reshuffle is not a routine reorganisation. It is a political recalibration driven by three forces: the need to accommodate a historic influx of defectors (33 opposition MPs absorbed into the NDA), the imperative to signal intent ahead of 2027 state elections, and the recognition that this is the last chance to shape the team that will fight the 2029 general election. The ministers being dropped are not just those who have underperformed — they are those who no longer fit the electoral arithmetic. The ministers reportedly under consideration for induction — Raghav Chadha, Kakoli Ghosh Dastidar, Shinde Sena representatives, Tarun Chugh, Nitish Kumar — are not just new faces. They are trophies in a strategy of competitive co-option. The PMO has described the exercise as a way to "jolt the ministers out of inertia." But the bigger message is to the opposition: join, or be made irrelevant.
