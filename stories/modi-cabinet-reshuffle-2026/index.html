Modi Cabinet Reshuffle 2026 — The Last Big Rejig Before 2029

The Breakdown · June 25, 2026 · 8 min read

On the evening of June 23, Prime Minister Narendra Modi met President Droupadi Murmu at Rashtrapati Bhavan. The President's Office shared pictures on X: "Prime Minister Shri @narendramodi called on President Droupadi Murmu at Rashtrapati Bhavan." On the same platform that day, Commerce Minister Piyush Goyal posted his farewell to USTR Jamieson Greer — a scheduling coincidence that Delhi's political grapevine treated as anything but. Hours earlier, the President had accepted the resignation of George Kurian, Minister of State for Minority Affairs. The sequence — a resignation, then a meeting with the constitutional head of state — followed a pattern Modi has used twice before: in July 2016 and July 2021, when he reshuffled his councils of ministers just after completing two years in office. This time, the stakes are higher. The reshuffle expected in the coming days will likely be the last major reorganisation of Modi's government before the 2029 Lok Sabha elections.

The Ministers Leaving

At least four ministers are confirmed to exit, with more expected. The departures fall into three categories: those whose Rajya Sabha terms expired without renewal, those who have taken party organisational roles, and those being moved for performance reasons.

Bittu and Kurian were left out of the BJP's Rajya Sabha candidate list announced on June 18, effectively ending their ministerial tenures. Malhotra and Chaudhary are expected to step down under the party's "one man, one post" principle after being assigned state party leadership roles. Beyond these four, insiders say at least half a dozen more ministers could be dropped — some for underperformance, others to make room for the wave of defectors the BJP has absorbed from rival parties.

The Defectors Being Rewarded

The most striking feature of this reshuffle is the number of opposition politicians who have crossed over to the BJP and are now in line for ministerial positions. In April, seven Rajya Sabha MPs from the Aam Aadmi Party "merged" with the BJP, led by the prominent face of Raghav Chadha — a former AAP star who has since praised Modi so fulsomely that he has drawn criticism for comparing him favourably to Jawaharlal Nehru. Chadha, along with former cricketer Harbhajan Singh, former AAP strategist Sandeep Pathak, and business figures Ashok Mittal, Rajinder Gupta, and Vikramjit Sahni — all six from Punjab — are potential inductees. Swati Maliwal, from Delhi, is also in the mix.

From West Bengal, the rupture in the Trinamool Congress has produced 20 rebel Lok Sabha MPs who merged with the National Citizens Party of India (NCPI) and signalled their intent to join the NDA. Kakoli Ghosh Dastidar and Sudeep Bandyopadhyay are in the reckoning for Cabinet-rank positions. From Maharashtra, the six Shiv Sena (UBT) MPs who joined Eknath Shinde's faction in "Operation Tiger" have also created expectations of representation.

The Big Portfolio Chessboard

The most consequential speculation revolves around the finance portfolio. Sources across multiple publications have indicated that Commerce Minister Piyush Goyal is the leading candidate to replace Nirmala Sitharaman as Finance Minister. Goyal, who has led the India-US trade negotiations and built a strong rapport with USTR Jamieson Greer, is seen by the PMO as someone who can bring commercial pragmatism to the finance ministry at a time when India is navigating the post-Iran-war economic landscape, oil price volatility, and a slowing GDP growth rate of 6.6%.

Sitharaman's fate is unclear. She could be moved to another portfolio — defence is one possibility — or assigned an organisational role. Railways Minister Ashwini Vaishnaw, who currently holds multiple portfolios including electronics and IT, is being mentioned as a possible successor to Goyal at Commerce, or as a candidate for Finance if the calculus shifts.

Other major moves being discussed:

  • Dharmendra Pradhan — Education Minister under fire over the NEET paper leak and a series of examination controversies. BJP insiders say his removal is being seriously considered, with the party's Odisha in-charge BJ Panda seen as a potential replacement.
  • Devendra Fadnavis — The Maharashtra Chief Minister may be brought to the Centre as a Cabinet minister, a move that would reshape both state and central politics.
  • Ashwini Vaishnaw, Prahlad Joshi, Mansukh Mandaviya — All three currently hold multiple portfolios and are expected to be divested of some to make way for new inductees.
  • Hardeep Puri & BL Verma — Their Rajya Sabha terms end in November. The BJP will take a call on their continuation soon.

Why This Reshuffle Matters

Three factors make this reorganisation different from the July 2021 or July 2016 exercises.

First, the defector influx. Modi's third-term government, constituted on June 9, 2024, had 72 ministers. The BJP fell short of a parliamentary majority, making NDA allies crucial. Since then, the party has absorbed AAP MPs, TMC rebels, and Shiv Sena (UBT) defectors in numbers that have shifted the arithmetic of Parliament. The reshuffle must reward these newcomers without alienating existing allies — a delicate balance.

Second, the electoral calendar. Assembly elections are due in early 2027 in Uttar Pradesh, Punjab, Uttarakhand, Gujarat, Goa, Himachal Pradesh, and Manipur. The reshuffle is designed to signal to these states that they have a voice in the Centre. New faces from poll-bound states are a near-certainty. Women's representation — currently just seven out of 72 ministers — is expected to increase significantly, partly as a messaging exercise and partly because Modi has committed to operationalising women's reservation by 2029.

Third, the 2029 horizon. This is likely the last opportunity for a major Cabinet overhaul before the next general election. The 2024 reshuffle was just a formation. This one is a recalibration. Sources say the PMO has compiled detailed datasets on file pendency and ministerial performance — the kind of metrics that have been used to decide who stays and who goes.

"The PM seeks to jolt the ministers out of inertia. A sense has been felt within the government that some of the ministers have delivered far below performances. Many will be dropped."

The Precedent

Modi reshuffled his council of ministers on July 6, 2016 and July 7, 2021 — each time just over a month after completing two years in office. The 2021 reshuffle was the most extensive of his second term: 15 new Cabinet ministers (including promotions for seven ministers of state) and 28 new ministers of state were sworn in. Senior ministers including Ravi Shankar Prasad, Harsh Vardhan, Thawar Chand Gehlot, and Prakash Javadekar were dropped. Nirmala Sitharaman, Piyush Goyal, and Dharmendra Pradhan were elevated to Cabinet rank. The pattern suggests the PM prefers a single major mid-term overhaul rather than frequent minor changes.

This time, the organisational reset is happening in parallel. The BJP's new national office-bearers under party president Nitin Nabin are expected to be announced by June 30, replacing the Team JP Nadda structure. Several outgoing ministers are expected to move into organisational roles, creating a revolving door between the government and the party apparatus.

The Bottom Line

THE VERDICT

Modi's 2026 Cabinet reshuffle is not a routine reorganisation. It is a political recalibration driven by three forces: the need to accommodate a historic influx of defectors, the imperative to signal intent ahead of 2027 state elections, and the recognition that this is the last chance to shape the team that will fight the 2029 general election. The ministers being dropped are not just those who have underperformed — they are those who no longer fit the electoral arithmetic. The ministers being inducted — Raghav Chadha, Kakoli Ghosh Dastidar, the Shinde Sena defectors — are not just new faces. They are trophies in a strategy of competitive co-option. The PMO has described the exercise as a way to "jolt the ministers out of inertia." But the bigger message is to the opposition: join, or be made irrelevant.
