India's rural employment guarantee now promises 125 days of work per household — up from 100. But the average household received only about 50 days under the old law. The gap between promise and delivery is about to get a new test.

On July 1, 2026, the **Viksit Bharat — Guarantee for Rozgar and Ajeevika Mission (Gramin) Act, 2025** — VB-G RAM G — came into force nationwide, repealing the Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (MGNREGA) that had been in operation since February 2006.

**Verified Fact.** Source: Gazette of India notification, Ministry of Rural Development, July 1, 2026. Confidence: 100.

The new Act raises the statutory guarantee from 100 to 125 days. It sets a national wage floor of ₹300 per day — the first in the programme's history — and lifts the national average wage from ₹298.8 to ₹327.4 per day, a 9.5% increase. But it also restructures the programme's core: funding shifts from demand-driven to supply-driven, and states shoulder more of the cost.

**Verified Fact.** Source: PIB release, Ministry of Rural Development, July 1, 2026; Times of India, July 1, 2026. Confidence: 100.

## Why This Matters

MGNREGA was the world's largest public works programme. In 2024-25, it covered **15.99 crore registered households** — more people than the population of Brazil and Russia combined. It generated **290.60 crore person-days of employment** and spent approximately ₹86,000 crore. To put that in perspective: ₹86,000 crore is roughly 0.3% of India's GDP, or about half of what the central government spends on fertilizer subsidies in a year. For millions of landless labourers, small farmers, and women in rural India, this programme is the primary safety net between subsistence and destitution.

**Verified Fact.** Source: PIB, Ministry of Rural Development, August 2025; MGNREGA MIS dashboard. Confidence: 100.

Any change to this programme affects more people than most laws passed by Parliament. The replacement — pushed through in the winter session of 2025 amid opposition protests — alters the legal character of the employment guarantee, the financial burden on states, and the method by which funds reach workers.

**Official Statement.** Source: Lok Sabha debates, December 2025; Presidential assent December 21, 2025. Confidence: 100.

## The System

To understand what changes, first understand how the old machine worked.

Under MGNREGA, the system was **demand-driven**. A rural household could demand work by submitting Form-6 — written, oral, or eventually digital. Once demanded, the government was legally obligated to provide employment within 15 days. If it failed, the household was entitled to an unemployment allowance. The budget followed demand: Section 23 of the 2005 Act appropriated funds "as may be required," meaning no fixed ceiling existed.

**Historical Record.** Source: MGNREGA Act, 2005, Section 23; Parliamentary budget documents, various years. Confidence: 100.

Money flowed from the Centre to states on a 90:10 cost-sharing basis. The Union government bore 100% of wage costs and 75% of material costs. States paid the remaining 10%. Gram panchayats implemented at least 50% of works. Wages were paid via Direct Benefit Transfer to bank or post office accounts. Social audits were mandated but unevenly enforced.

**Verified Fact.** Source: MGNREGA Act, 2005; CAG reports on MGNREGA implementation. Confidence: 100.

Here is how VB-G RAM G changes each part of this machine:

| Component | MGNREGA (Old) | VB-G RAM G (New) |
|---|---|---|
| Entitlement | Demand-driven; budget followed demand | Supply-driven; centrally determined allocation |
| Funding ratio | 90:10 (Centre:State) for most states | 60:40 for general states; 90:10 for NE/Himalayan; 100% for UTs |
| Wage guarantee | 100 days per household per year | 125 days per household per year |
| Wage floor | No statutory national floor | ₹300/day interim national floor |
| Budget provision | "As may be required" (no fixed ceiling) | Normative allocation based on objective parameters |
| Seasonal exclusion | None | Up to 60 non-working days during peak agriculture |
| Attendance | Manual muster rolls (later e-muster) | Face-authentication mandatory |
| Unemployment allowance | 1/4th wage for first 30 days, 1/2 thereafter | Same (retained) |
| Delay compensation | No statutory provision | 0.05% per day beyond 15 days |

**Verified Fact.** Source: VB-G RAM G Act, 2025; MGNREGA Act, 2005; PIB FAQ, May 2026. Confidence: 100.

## The Changes

### 1. More Days, Same Delivery Gap?

The 125-day guarantee sounds like a straightforward expansion. The catch: under the old 100-day ceiling, the average household received only about 50 days per year — roughly half the entitlement. This gap persisted for nearly two decades despite the legal guarantee.

**Verified Fact.** Source: Jean Drèze and Rahul R, "A Short History of MGNREGA: Twenty Years in Ten Charts," Levy Economics Institute Working Paper No. 1095, October 2025. Confidence: 95.

The question is not whether 125 is larger than 100. It is whether the new supply-driven allocation model will close the gap or widen it. If the normative allocation falls short of demand, states must bear the additional cost themselves — an expensive proposition for Bihar, Uttar Pradesh, and other high-demand states.

### 2. The Wage Floor: ₹300 for 21 States

For the first time, the law sets a national minimum wage for rural employment. The **interim base wage of ₹300 per day** brings 21 states and union territories that previously paid below ₹300 up to the floor. The national average wage simultaneously rose to ₹327.4 per day.

**Verified Fact.** Source: Ministry of Rural Development notification, June 30, 2026; News18, July 1, 2026. Confidence: 100.

State-wise wages under VB-G RAM G (effective July 1, 2026):

| Haryana | 409 |
| Goa | 406 |
| Kerala | 401 |
| Karnataka | 382 |
| Himachal Pradesh (Scheduled Areas) | 375 |

**Verified Fact.** Source: Ministry of Rural Development wage notification; News18 state-wise table; Times of India, July 1, 2026. Confidence: 100.

What does this mean on the ground? A worker in Bihar who earned ₹241 per day — the lowest pre-July wage — now gets ₹300. That is a 24.5% increase, the highest percentage hike in the country (shared with Arunachal Pradesh and Nagaland). But a worker in Haryana who earned ₹374 now gets ₹409 — still earning 36% more per day than the Bihar worker. The floor narrowed the gap but did not eliminate it.

The largest percentage increases went to states with historically lowest wages — Arunachal Pradesh and Nagaland saw nearly 24.5% hikes. States that already paid above the floor (Kerala, Haryana, Punjab, Karnataka) received smaller indexation-linked increases.

### 3. Funding: From 90:10 to 60:40

This is the most consequential change — and the most contested.

Under MGNREGA, the Centre bore 90% of the effective cost. Under VB-G RAM G:

- **North-Eastern and Himalayan states**: 90:10 (Centre:State) — unchanged
- **Other states and UTs with legislature**: 60:40 — a fourfold increase in state share
- **UTs without legislature**: 100% central funding

**Verified Fact.** Source: VB-G RAM G Act, 2025, Section 12; PIB FAQ document, May 2026; Indian Express, May 27, 2026. Confidence: 100.

Consider what 40% means for a state like Bihar. Under the old system, Bihar's share of its MGNREGA programme was about 10% of roughly ₹4,000 crore — around ₹400 crore. Under the new system, Bihar must find 40% of a larger wage bill (since wages rose 24.5%) — potentially ₹2,000 crore or more from its own budget. For a state with limited fiscal headroom, that competes directly with education, health, and infrastructure spending.

Five opposition-ruled states — Karnataka, Kerala, Punjab, Telangana, and Jharkhand — have passed resolutions opposing the Act.

**Political Claim.** Source: Scroll.in, July 1, 2026; News18. States cited: Karnataka (Congress), Kerala (CPI(M)-led LDF), Punjab (AAP), Telangana (Congress), Jharkhand (JMM-Congress). Confidence: 100.

### 4. Allocation: Demand-Driven → Supply-Driven

Under MGNREGA, if a household demanded work, the government was legally obligated to provide it. The budget followed the demand — there was no fixed upper limit.

VB-G RAM G replaces this with a **normative allocation model**. The Centre determines each state's allocation based on "objective parameters" — including the Sixteenth Finance Commission's horizontal devolution formula — and any expenditure beyond that allocation must be borne entirely by the state.

**Verified Fact.** Source: VB-G RAM G Act, 2025, Section 5; draft Objective Parameters for Normative Allocation Rules, 2026; Indian Express, May 27, 2026. Confidence: 100.

The government argues this brings fiscal discipline. Critics counter it is no longer a right-based entitlement but a capped-budget programme. If demand exceeds the allocation in a drought year, for instance, a state either pays the difference or denies work — which was legally impossible under MGNREGA.

**Expert Opinion.** Source: The Hindu, June 29, 2026 — "Why are States opposing VB-G RAM G"; economists cited by BusinessLine, May 11, 2026. Confidence: 90.

### 5. Agricultural Blackout Period

The Act permits the government to notify up to **60 non-working days** during peak agricultural seasons — effectively pausing the programme when farm labour demand is high. Under MGNREGA, workers could seek employment year-round.

**Verified Fact.** Source: VB-G RAM G Act, 2025, Section 8(3); The Hindu, June 29, 2026. Confidence: 100.

The government's argument: this ensures labour availability for farmers during sowing and harvesting. The counterargument: landless labourers depend on MGNREGA income even during agricultural seasons, and the blackout period reduces their earning options. At least four states — Punjab, Karnataka, Telangana, and Kerala — have sought reconsideration.

### 6. Technology and Transparency

The Act mandates:
- **Face-authentication attendance** — replacing manual muster rolls
- **Geo-referencing** of all worksites
- **Direct Benefit Transfer** of wages to bank or post office accounts
- Mandatory **social audits** every six months per gram panchayat
- Delay compensation at **0.05% per day** for unpaid wages beyond 15 days
- Unemployment allowance at **1/4th of wage for first 30 days**, **1/2 for remainder**

**Verified Fact.** Source: VB-G RAM G Act, 2025, Sections 14-18; PIB FAQ, May 2026. Confidence: 100.

The delay compensation provision is new under VB-G RAM G — MGNREGA had no equivalent. But civil society organisations have raised concerns that mandatory face authentication could exclude elderly workers and those in areas with poor digital connectivity.

**Expert Opinion.** Source: Economic Times, May 11, 2026; The Hindu, June 29, 2026. Confidence: 90.

## What Stays the Same

Not everything changes. Key continuities:

- **Job cards**: Existing e-KYC verified MGNREGA job cards remain valid until new Gramin Rozgar Guarantee Cards are issued.
- **Work within 15 days**: Employment must still be provided within 15 days of demand, failing which unemployment allowance is payable.
- **5 km radius**: Work must be provided within 5 km of the applicant's village; beyond that, 10% transport allowance applies.
- **Contractor ban**: No contractors or machinery may be used — employment must be manual.
- **Gram Panchayat role**: At least 50% of works must be implemented by gram panchayats.
- **Women's participation**: Minimum 33% of workers per worksite must be women (actual participation is now 58% nationally).
- **DBT wages**: Wages continue through bank or post office accounts.
- **Oral demand**: Workers may still demand employment orally, in writing (Form-6), or digitally.

**Verified Fact.** Source: VB-G RAM G Act, 2025, transitional provisions; PIB, May 2026; Ministry of Rural Development FAQ. Confidence: 100.

## The Debate

### The Government's Case — and the Counterarguments

**Argument 1: Higher wages benefit the poorest workers.**
The government points to the ₹300 floor and 24.5% hikes for low-wage states.
- **Evidence quality**: Verified fact. The wage increases are confirmed by official notifications.
- **Limitation**: Higher nominal wages mean little if inflation erodes real gains, or if budget allocations fall short and households receive fewer days.
- **Counterargument**: A worker in Bihar now earns ₹300 per day — but if the allocation cap reduces their total working days from 50 to 30, their annual earnings could actually fall.

**Argument 2: 125-day guarantee is a 25% expansion of entitlements.**
- **Evidence quality**: Verified fact. The statutory ceiling rises.
- **Limitation**: The old ceiling of 100 days was never reached — average was 50. A higher ceiling means nothing if the funding model does not support it.
- **Counterargument**: Under the old demand-driven system, the government could not legally refuse work. Under the new supply-driven model, if the allocation runs out, the state either pays or denies work. The guarantee may be stronger on paper but weaker in practice.

**Argument 3: Normative allocation brings fiscal discipline.**
- **Evidence quality**: Government position. The Act's budget mechanism is documented.
- **Limitation**: Discipline assumes the allocation accurately estimates demand. If it underestimates, the gap falls on states.
- **Counterargument**: MGNREGA's demand-driven model automatically expanded during crises — it generated 389 crore person-days during COVID-19. The new model's fixed allocation removes this countercyclical capacity.

**Argument 4: Face authentication reduces leakages.**
- **Evidence quality**: Plausible. Aadhaar-based systems have reduced ghost beneficiaries in other schemes.
- **Limitation**: Studies show biometric authentication in MGNREGA had mixed results — it reduced some fraud but also excluded legitimate workers due to fingerprint failures.
- **Counterargument**: In states with poor connectivity — Bihar, Odisha, Jharkhand — mandatory face authentication could function as a de facto barrier to access. The tech infrastructure does not exist uniformly.

**Official Statement.** Source: Ministry of Rural Development press release, July 1, 2026; Times of India, July 1, 2026. Confidence: 100.

### The Opposition's Case

Five opposition-ruled states have passed resolutions against the Act. Their four principal objections:

1. **Financial burden on states**: The 60:40 ratio disproportionately affects states with higher rural populations and weaker fiscal positions. Bihar, which had the lowest MGNREGA wage (₹241), now must find 40% of a larger programme cost.
   - **Evidence quality**: Verified — the funding ratio is in the Act. The fiscal impact calculation depends on state-level assumptions.
   - **Limitation**: North-Eastern and Himalayan states are exempted from the 60:40 ratio, but the states most affected (UP, Bihar, Rajasthan, MP, Odisha) are also the ones with the highest rural poverty.

2. **End of demand-driven guarantee**: "It is no longer a right-based entitlement but a centrally capped allocation."
   - **Evidence quality**: Expert opinion, supported by legal analysis in The Hindu.
   - **Counterargument from government**: The normative allocation will be sufficient — the Finance Commission formula accounts for population and need.

3. **Centralisation**: Section 5(1) empowers the Centre to notify "rural areas in a State" where the scheme applies — departing from MGNREGA's universal coverage.
   - **Evidence quality**: Verified — the Act contains this provision.
   - **Implication**: The scheme could theoretically be applied selectively, though the government has not indicated it will do so.

4. **Blackout period**: Reduces earning opportunities for landless labourers who depend on MGNREGA even during agricultural seasons.
   - **Evidence quality**: Verified — Section 8(3) permits up to 60 non-working days.
   - **Counterargument from government**: The provision ensures labour supply for farmers and prevents MGNREGA from competing with agricultural wages.

**Political Claim / Expert Opinion.** Source: The Hindu, June 29, 2026; Scroll.in, July 1, 2026; BusinessLine, May 11, 2026. Confidence: 95.

## 20 Years of MGNREGA: A Brief History

The Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act was passed on September 7, 2005 — a flagship of the Congress-led United Progressive Alliance government, championed by Sonia Gandhi's National Advisory Council. It rolled out in three phases:

- **February 2006**: 200 most backward districts
- **2007**: 330 additional districts
- **April 2008**: All 619 rural districts of India
- **2009**: Renamed from NREGA to MGNREGA (Mahatma Gandhi appended)

**Historical Record.** Source: Drèze & Rahul R, Levy Institute WP 1095; PIB historical releases; Parliament records. Confidence: 100.

Over two decades, MGNREGA evolved from a rural employment programme into something larger: a countercyclical stabiliser, a women's empowerment tool (58% of workers are women, far above the legal minimum of 33%), and a legal precedent for the right to work. During the COVID-19 crisis in 2020-21, it generated a record 389 crore person-days and absorbed millions of returning migrants.

Yet the programme faced persistent structural problems: average days per household stagnated around 50 despite the 100-day ceiling, wage payments were frequently delayed, the share of GDP allocated to the programme fell from 0.6% in 2009-10 to 0.3-0.4% in recent years, and budget releases often fell short of demand.

**Historical Record.** Source: Drèze & Rahul R, Levy Institute WP 1095; PIB historical releases; Parliament records. Confidence: 100.

### India's Employment Challenge

The replacement of MGNREGA comes at a time when India's rural economy is under multiple pressures: agricultural fragmentation, climate-induced distress, stagnant real wages, and insufficient non-farm employment. The unemployment rate in rural India was 5.5% as of May 2026 (PLFS). Youth unemployment — especially among educated rural youth — runs significantly higher.

**Verified Fact.** Source: PLFS Bulletin, May 2026; Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation. Confidence: 95.

The economist Jean Drèze and Rahul R have argued that the key question is not 100 vs 125 days, but whether the new funding model enables or constrains the programme's countercyclical role — its ability to expand precisely when the economy contracts and private employment disappears.

**Expert Opinion.** Source: Drèze & Rahul R, Levy Institute WP 1095; Outlook India analysis, December 2025. Confidence: 90.

## Global Context

India is not alone in rethinking employment guarantee programmes. Argentina's Jefes de Hogar plan, South Africa's Expanded Public Works Programme, and pilot programmes in the United States have all grappled with similar tensions between legal entitlements and fiscal constraints.

The VB-G RAM G model — shifting from demand-driven to supply-driven — moves India closer to a fixed-budget public works model and away from the rights-based framework that made MGNREGA distinctive internationally. Most countries that adopted job guarantee programmes during economic crises eventually retreated from open-ended entitlements. Argentina's Jefes de Hogar, launched during the 2002 economic collapse, was demand-driven but was later restructured with tighter eligibility. South Africa's EPWP was supply-driven from the start.

**Independent Verification.** Source: Levy Economics Institute research on job guarantee programmes; ILO World Social Protection Report, 2024-26. Confidence: 80.

India is now running a natural experiment: can a rights-based framework be replaced by a formula-based allocation without losing the protection it provided to the most vulnerable?

## What's Next

### Immediate (July-September 2026)

- **Transition period**: Ongoing MGNREGA works as of June 30 carried over into the new framework. New Gramin Rozgar Guarantee Cards to be issued, replacing MGNREGA job cards.
- **Wage notifications**: States to notify their VB-G RAM G state schemes within six months (by January 1, 2027). Twenty-four states have already notified schemes.
- **Budget release**: ₹95,692.31 crore interim allocation released to states for transition.
- **Political challenge**: Five opposition states continue to press resolutions against the Act. Legal challenges cannot be ruled out.

### Short Term (FY 2026-27)

- **First full year** of VB-G RAM G implementation. The real test: whether the 125-day guarantee is backed by adequate allocations.
- **State fiscal stress**: States with higher rural populations and weak revenues face the 40% cost burden. Watch for delays in wage payments as states adjust to the new funding model.
- **Normative allocation formula**: Full details of the "objective parameters" to be operationalised. The proportion of allocation kept aside for "performance-linked" distribution will determine how much flexibility states have.

### Medium Term (2027-2029)

- **Gramin Rozgar Guarantee Cards** fully rolled out. Face-authentication attendance becomes mandatory — digital exclusion risks to be assessed.
- **Agricultural blackout period** implementation — which states opt to use it and for how long.
- **Budget trajectory**: If actual allocations under VB-G RAM G fall below historical MGNREGA expenditure levels, the government will face pressure to explain how the expanded guarantee is being honoured.

### Long Term (2030+)

- The VB-G RAM G Act is positioned by the government as part of the **Viksit Bharat @2047** vision framework. The question: does it evolve into a more comprehensive rural livelihood programme or remain primarily a wage employment scheme?
- **Climate resilience**: The Act's focus on water security, disaster preparedness, and natural resource management aligns with climate adaptation needs — potentially a growth area for the programme.
