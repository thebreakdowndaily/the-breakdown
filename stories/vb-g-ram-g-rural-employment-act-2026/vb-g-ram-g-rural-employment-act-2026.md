## What Happened

India's flagship rural employment programme changed for the first time in 20 years on July 1, 2026. The **Viksit Bharat — Guarantee for Rozgar and Ajeevika Mission (Gramin) Act, 2025** — abbreviated as VB-G RAM G — came into force nationwide, repealing the Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (MGNREGA) that had been in operation since February 2006.

**Verified Fact.** Source: Gazette of India notification, Ministry of Rural Development, July 1, 2026. Confidence: 100.

The new Act increases the statutory guarantee from 100 to 125 days of unskilled wage employment per rural household per year, sets a national wage floor of ₹300 per day for the first time, and raises the national average wage by 9.5% from ₹298.8 to ₹327.4 per day. But it also fundamentally restructures how the programme is funded and managed — shifting from a demand-driven legal entitlement to a supply-driven framework with centrally determined allocations.

**Verified Fact.** Source: PIB release, Ministry of Rural Development, July 1, 2026; Times of India, July 1, 2026. Confidence: 100.

## Why This Matters

MGNREGA was the world's largest public works programme. In 2024-25 alone, it covered **15.99 crore registered households**, generated **290.60 crore person-days of employment**, and spent approximately ₹86,000 crore. For millions of landless labourers, small farmers, and women in rural India, the programme is not a welfare scheme — it is the primary safety net between subsistence and destitution.

**Verified Fact.** Source: PIB, Ministry of Rural Development, August 2025; MGNREGA MIS dashboard. Confidence: 100.

Any change to this programme affects more people than most laws passed by Parliament. The replacement — pushed through in the winter session of 2025 amid opposition protests — alters the legal character of the employment guarantee, the financial burden on states, and the method by which funds reach workers.

**Official Statement.** Source: Lok Sabha debates, December 2025; Presidential assent December 21, 2025. Confidence: 100.

## The Key Changes

### 1. Workdays: 100 → 125

The headline change is straightforward: the statutory guarantee rises from 100 days per rural household per financial year to 125 days. In practice, however, the average household under MGNREGA received only about 50 days of work per year — far below even the old 100-day ceiling.

**Verified Fact.** Source: Jean Drèze and Rahul R, "A Short History of MGNREGA: Twenty Years in Ten Charts," Levy Economics Institute Working Paper No. 1095, October 2025. Confidence: 95.

Whether the 125-day guarantee translates into more actual workdays depends on budget allocations and state implementation capacity — both of which are now structured differently under the new Act.

### 2. Wage Floor: First-Time National Minimum

The government introduced an **interim base wage of ₹300 per day** — the first statutory wage floor in the programme's history. The national average wage was simultaneously raised to ₹327.4 per day. Twenty-one states and union territories that previously paid below ₹300 were brought up to the new floor.

**Verified Fact.** Source: Ministry of Rural Development notification, June 30, 2026; News18, July 1, 2026. Confidence: 100.

The largest percentage increases went to states with historically lowest wages — Arunachal Pradesh and Nagaland saw nearly 24.5% hikes. States that already paid above the floor (Kerala, Haryana, Punjab, Karnataka) received smaller indexation-linked increases.

### 3. Funding Structure: From 90:10 to 60:40

This is the most consequential change — and the most contested.

Under MGNREGA, the Union government bore 100% of wage costs and 75% of material costs, resulting in an effective 90:10 cost-sharing ratio between Centre and states. Under VB-G RAM G:

- **North-Eastern and Himalayan states**: 90:10 (Centre:State)
- **Other states and UTs with legislature**: 60:40
- **UTs without legislature**: 100% central funding

**Verified Fact.** Source: VB-G RAM G Act, 2025, Section 12; PIB FAQ document, May 2026; Indian Express, May 27, 2026. Confidence: 100.

This means most states must now contribute 40% of total programme expenditure — a fourfold increase in their financial responsibility. Five opposition-ruled states — Karnataka, Kerala, Punjab, Telangana, and Jharkhand — have passed resolutions opposing the Act.

**Political Claim.** Source: Scroll.in, July 1, 2026; News18. States cited: Karnataka (Congress), Kerala (CPI(M)-led LDF), Punjab (AAP), Telangana (Congress), Jharkhand (JMM-Congress). Confidence: 100.

### 4. Allocation Model: Demand-Driven → Supply-Driven

Under MGNREGA, the programme was demand-driven: if a household demanded work, the government was legally obligated to provide it, and the budget had to follow. The Act appropriated funds "as may be required" — meaning there was no fixed upper limit.

**Historical Record.** Source: MGNREGA Act, 2005, Section 23; Parliamentary budget documents, various years. Confidence: 100.

VB-G RAM G replaces this with a **normative allocation model**. The Centre determines each state's allocation based on "objective parameters" — including the Sixteenth Finance Commission's horizontal devolution formula — and any expenditure beyond that allocation must be borne entirely by the state.

**Verified Fact.** Source: VB-G RAM G Act, 2025, Section 5; draft Objective Parameters for Normative Allocation Rules, 2026; Indian Express, May 27, 2026. Confidence: 100.

This change transforms the nature of the guarantee. Critics argue it is no longer a legal entitlement backed by central funding, but a fixed-budget programme with state-level top-up risk.

**Expert Opinion.** Source: The Hindu, June 29, 2026 — "Why are States opposing VB-G RAM G"; economists cited by BusinessLine, May 11, 2026. Confidence: 90.

### 5. Agricultural Blackout Period

The Act permits the government to notify a period of up to **60 non-working days** during peak agricultural seasons — effectively allowing the programme to be paused when farm labour demand is high. Under MGNREGA, workers could seek employment year-round, without seasonal exclusions.

**Verified Fact.** Source: VB-G RAM G Act, 2025, Section 8(3); The Hindu, June 29, 2026. Confidence: 100.

At least four states — Punjab, Karnataka, Telangana, and Kerala — have sought reconsideration of this provision, arguing it reduces earning opportunities for landless labourers who depend on MGNREGA even during agricultural seasons.

### 6. Technology and Transparency

The Act mandates:
- **Face-authentication attendance** for workers — replacing manual muster rolls
- **Geo-referencing** of all worksites
- **Direct Benefit Transfer** of wages to bank or post office accounts
- Mandatory **social audits** every six months per gram panchayat
- Delay compensation at **0.05% per day** for unpaid wages beyond 15 days
- Unemployment allowance at **1/4th of wage for first 30 days**, **1/2 for remainder**

**Verified Fact.** Source: VB-G RAM G Act, 2025, Sections 14-18; PIB FAQ, May 2026. Confidence: 100.

Civil society organisations have raised concerns that mandatory face authentication could exclude elderly workers and those in areas with poor digital connectivity.

**Expert Opinion.** Source: Economic Times, May 11, 2026; The Hindu, June 29, 2026. Confidence: 90.

## What Stays the Same

Not everything changes. Key continuities:

- **Job cards**: Existing e-KYC verified MGNREGA job cards remain valid until new Gramin Rozgar Guarantee Cards are issued.
- **Work within 15 days**: Employment must still be provided within 15 days of demand, failing which unemployment allowance is payable.
- **5 km radius**: Work must be provided within 5 km of the applicant's village; beyond that, 10% transport allowance applies.
- **Contractor ban**: No contractors or machinery may be used — employment must be manual.
- **Gram Panchayat role**: At least 50% of works must be implemented by gram panchayats.
- **Women's participation**: Minimum 33% of workers per worksite must be women (actual participation is now 58% nationally).
- **DBT wages**: Wages continue through bank or post office accounts.
- **Oral demand**: Workers may still demand employment orally, in writing (Form-6), or digitally.

**Verified Fact.** Source: VB-G RAM G Act, 2025, transitional provisions; PIB, May 2026; Ministry of Rural Development FAQ. Confidence: 100.

## The Debate

### The Government's Case

The government presents VB-G RAM G as a modernization of a two-decade-old law. Rural Development Minister Shivraj Singh Chouhan described it as "a historic step towards building a Viksit Bharat through prosperous villages." The key arguments:

- **Higher wages**: "We have ensured better wages for rural workers. The largest increase has been provided to states where wages were historically low, so those who need the greatest support benefit the most." — Shivraj Singh Chouhan, July 1, 2026.
- **More workdays**: The 125-day guarantee is a 25% increase in the legal entitlement.
- **Fiscal discipline**: Normative allocation prevents budget overruns and ensures predictable funding.
- **Technology**: Face authentication and geo-tagging reduce leakages and ghost workers.
- **Agricultural sensitivity**: The blackout period ensures labour availability during peak farming seasons.

**Official Statement.** Source: Ministry of Rural Development press release, July 1, 2026; Times of India, July 1, 2026. Confidence: 100.

### The Opposition's Case

Five opposition-ruled states have passed resolutions against the Act. Critics raise four principal objections:

- **Financial burden on states**: The shift from 90:10 to 60:40 disproportionately affects states with higher rural populations and weaker fiscal positions. Bihar, which had the lowest MGNREGA wage (₹241), now must find 40% of a larger programme cost.
- **End of demand-driven guarantee**: "It is no longer a right-based entitlement but a centrally capped allocation. If demand exceeds the normative allocation, the state must pay — effectively negating the guarantee." — Analysis in The Hindu, June 29, 2026.
- **Centralisation**: Section 5(1) empowers the Centre to notify "rural areas in a State" where the scheme applies — departing from MGNREGA's universal coverage. This raises concerns that the scheme could be selectively applied.
- **Blackout period**: Reducing earning opportunities for landless labourers during agricultural seasons, when they often need supplementary income the most.

**Political Claim / Expert Opinion.** Source: The Hindu, June 29, 2026; Scroll.in, July 1, 2026; BusinessLine, May 11, 2026. Confidence: 95.

## The Bigger Picture

### 20 Years of MGNREGA: A Brief History

The Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act was passed on September 7, 2005 — a flagship of the Congress-led United Progressive Alliance government, championed by Sonia Gandhi's National Advisory Council. It rolled out in three phases:

- **February 2006**: 200 most backward districts
- **2007**: 330 additional districts
- **April 2008**: All 619 rural districts of India
- **2009**: Renamed from NREGA to MGNREGA (Mahatma Gandhi appended)

Over two decades, MGNREGA evolved from a rural employment programme into something larger: a countercyclical stabiliser, a women's empowerment tool, and a legal precedent for the right to work. During the COVID-19 crisis in 2020-21, it generated a record 389 crore person-days and absorbed millions of returning migrants.

**Historical Record.** Source: Drèze & Rahul R, Levy Institute WP 1095; PIB historical releases; Parliament records. Confidence: 100.

Yet the programme faced persistent criticism: average days per household stagnated around 50 (half the legal entitlement), wage payments were frequently delayed, administrative spending was cut sharply after 2014-15, and budget releases often fell short of actual demand despite the legal obligation to fund it.

### India's Employment Challenge

The replacement of MGNREGA comes at a time when India's rural economy is under multiple pressures: agricultural fragmentation, climate-induced distress, stagnant real wages, and insufficient non-farm employment. The unemployment rate in rural India was 5.5% as of May 2026 (PLFS). Youth unemployment — especially among educated rural youth — runs significantly higher.

**Verified Fact.** Source: PLFS Bulletin, May 2026; Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation. Confidence: 95.

Economists argue that the key question is not whether 125 days is better than 100, but whether the new funding model will enable or constrain the programme's countercyclical role — its ability to expand precisely when the economy contracts and private employment disappears.

**Expert Opinion.** Source: Drèze & Rahul R, Levy Institute WP 1095; Outlook India analysis, December 2025. Confidence: 90.

### International Context

India is not alone in rethinking employment guarantee programmes. Argentina's Jefes de Hogar plan, South Africa's Expanded Public Works Programme, and pilot programmes in the United States have all grappled with similar tensions between legal entitlements and fiscal constraints. The VB-G RAM G model — shifting from demand-driven to supply-driven — moves India closer to a fixed-budget public works model and away from the rights-based framework that made MGNREGA distinctive internationally.

**Independent Verification.** Source: Levy Economics Institute research on job guarantee programmes; ILO World Social Protection Report, 2024-26. Confidence: 80.

## What's Next

### Immediate (July-September 2026)

- **Transition period**: Ongoing MGNREGA works as of June 30 carried over into the new framework. New Gramin Rozgar Guarantee Cards to be issued, replacing MGNREGA job cards.
- **Wage notifications**: States to notify their VB-G RAM G state schemes within six months (by January 1, 2027). Twenty-four states have already notified schemes.
- **Budget release**: ₹95,692.31 crore interim allocation released to states for transition.
- **Political challenge**: Five opposition states continue to press resolutions against the Act. Legal challenges cannot be ruled out.

### Short Term (FY 2026-27)

- **First full year** of VB-G RAM G implementation. Real test of whether the 125-day guarantee is backed by adequate allocations.
- **State fiscal stress**: States with higher rural populations and weak revenues face the 40% cost burden. Watch for delays in wage payments as states adjust to the new funding model.
- **Normative allocation formula**: Full details of the "objective parameters" to be operationalised. The proportion of allocation kept aside for "performance-linked" distribution will determine how much flexibility states have.

### Medium Term (2027-2029)

- **Gramin Rozgar Guarantee Cards** fully rolled out. Face-authentication attendance becomes mandatory — digital exclusion risks to be assessed.
- **Agricultural blackout period** implementation — which states opt to use it and for how long.
- **Budget trajectory**: If actual allocations under VB-G RAM G fall below historical MGNREGA expenditure levels, the government will face pressure to explain how the expanded guarantee is being honoured.

### Long Term (2030+)

- The VB-G RAM G Act is positioned by the government as part of the **Viksit Bharat @2047** vision framework. The question is whether it evolves into a more comprehensive rural livelihood programme or remains primarily a wage employment scheme.
- **Climate resilience**: The Act's focus on water security, disaster preparedness, and natural resource management aligns with climate adaptation needs — potentially a growth area for the programme.
