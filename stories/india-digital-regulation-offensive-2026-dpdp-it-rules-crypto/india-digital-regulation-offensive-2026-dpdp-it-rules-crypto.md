## Cold Open

India is tightening digital regulation on three fronts simultaneously — in what analysts describe as the most aggressive push for technology governance since the Information Technology Act itself. The Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology is fast-tracking DPDP Act compliance from 18 months to 12 months, overhauling the IT Rules to expand the Digital Media Ethics Code to individual news-sharing accounts, and the Financial Intelligence Unit is demanding OTC crypto records above $10,000 with mandatory live-selfie verification. The convergence means a single platform can simultaneously be a data fiduciary under DPDP, an intermediary under IT Rules, and a reporting entity under FIU guidelines — a compliance trilemma that will reshape how global technology companies operate in India.

## DPDP Act Rules

MeitY is pushing Big Tech to comply with the DPDP Act on an accelerated timeline, potentially cutting implementation from 18 months to just 12. The Data Protection Board — the adjudicatory body for violations — is being constituted, but several critical provisions remain pending: cross-border transfer restrictions, Significant Data Fiduciary designations, and data localisation requirements have not yet been notified. The government expects the full DPDP framework to be operational by May 2027. Technology companies argue that compliance infrastructure — data mapping, consent management platforms, breach notification systems — cannot be built overnight. Industry bodies have requested phased implementation, but the government is signalling limited flexibility.

## IT Rules Amendments

The draft Second Amendment to the Information Technology Rules, 2021, represents the most significant expansion of the Digital Media Ethics Code. For the first time, the code will apply to non-publisher users who regularly share news on social media — the "shadow news" ecosystem of WhatsApp groups, YouTube channels, and individual accounts that curate and distribute news content. The amendment makes government advisories legally binding, meaning platforms lose safe harbour protection under Section 79 of the IT Act if they fail to comply. Reduced takedown timelines accelerate removal of unlawful content. Civil society organisations argue the amendments could lead to over-censorship and chill legitimate political speech — a concern that directly echoes the pre-Shreya Singhal era.

## Crypto Regulation

The FIU has escalated enforcement in the cryptocurrency space, requesting OTC transaction records above $10,000 from three major exchanges. The focus is on off-exchange trades conducted by private companies, which regulators believe have been used to circumvent reporting requirements. Binance paid a $2.25 million penalty for compliance failures, and the Enforcement Directorate is investigating four firms under the Foreign Exchange Management Act. New KYC norms include live selfie verification, geolocation tracking, and IP address capture for all crypto transactions. The measures target the anonymity feature that has made crypto a favoured vehicle for money laundering and illicit finance.

## Impact on Industry

Technology companies operating in India must now navigate a compliance landscape spanning data protection, content regulation, and financial oversight simultaneously. The fast-tracked DPDP timeline demands significant investment in data governance infrastructure. The IT Rules amendments require platforms to police content shared by individual users — an operational challenge of immense scale in a country with over 900 million internet users. Crypto businesses face some of the tightest KYC norms globally. Legal experts note that the three regulatory streams create overlapping obligations: a data breach could simultaneously trigger DPDP penalties, IT Rules non-compliance, and FIU reporting requirements. India is moving decisively toward a tightly governed digital ecosystem — and the cost of compliance is rapidly becoming a barrier to entry for smaller players.
