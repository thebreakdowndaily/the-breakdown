## Cold Open

India is tightening its digital regulatory framework on three fronts simultaneously in what analysts describe as the most aggressive push for technology governance since the introduction of the Information Technology Act. The Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology is fast-tracking compliance with the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, overhauling the IT Rules to expand the Digital Media Ethics Code, and the Financial Intelligence Unit is cracking down on cryptocurrency over-the-counter trades with enhanced KYC requirements. The convergence of these three regulatory streams signals a fundamental shift from the hands-off approach that characterised India's early internet policy to a regime of mandated compliance and active enforcement.

## DPDP Act Rules

MeitY is pushing Big Tech to comply with the DPDP Act on an accelerated timeline, potentially shortening implementation from 18 months to just 12 months. The Data Protection Board, tasked with adjudicating violations, is being constituted. Several key provisions remain pending: cross-border transfer restrictions, Significant Data Fiduciary designations, and data localisation requirements are yet to be notified. The government expects the full DPDP framework to be operational by May 2027. Industry bodies have requested phased implementation.

## IT Rules Amendments

The draft Second Amendment to the Information Technology Rules, 2021, represents the most significant expansion of the Digital Media Ethics Code since its inception. For the first time, the code will apply to non-publisher users who regularly share news on social media â€” the so-called "shadow news" ecosystem comprising WhatsApp groups, YouTube channels, and individual social media accounts that curate and distribute news content. The amendment makes government advisories legally binding, meaning platforms lose their safe harbour protection under Section 79 of the IT Act if they fail to comply. Reduced takedown timelines have been proposed to accelerate the removal of content deemed unlawful. Civil society organisations and free speech advocates have raised serious concerns, arguing that the amendments could lead to over-censorship and chill legitimate political speech.

## Crypto Regulation

The FIU has escalated its enforcement actions in the cryptocurrency space, requesting over-the-counter transaction records above $10,000 from three major exchanges. The focus is on off-exchange trades conducted by private companies, which regulators believe have been used to circumvent reporting requirements. Binance, the world's largest crypto exchange, paid a $2.25 million penalty for compliance failures. The Enforcement Directorate is investigating four firms for alleged violations of the Foreign Exchange Management Act. New KYC norms have been made mandatory, including live selfie verification, geolocation tracking, and IP address capture for all crypto transactions. The measures are designed to address the anonymity that has made crypto a favoured vehicle for money laundering and illicit finance.

## Impact on Industry

Technology companies operating in India must now navigate a complex compliance landscape spanning data protection, content regulation, and financial oversight. The fast-tracked DPDP timeline demands significant investment in data governance infrastructure. The IT Rules amendments require platforms to police content shared by individual users â€” an operational challenge of immense scale. Crypto businesses face some of the tightest KYC norms globally. Legal experts note that a single platform could simultaneously be a data fiduciary under DPDP, an intermediary under IT Rules, and a reporting entity under FIU guidelines. The regulatory offensive signals that India is moving decisively towards a tightly governed digital ecosystem.
