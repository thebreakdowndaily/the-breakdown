# Anthropic Mythos AI: The Model That Could Hack Anything

## Cold Open

In April 2026, Anthropic released an AI model so capable at cybersecurity that the company refused to let the public use it. Mythos Preview could find vulnerabilities in decades-old code in minutes, exploit them autonomously, and generate working attacks faster than most human hackers. On June 9, Anthropic released a safe version called Fable 5 to the general public, while simultaneously launching Mythos 5 to a restricted set of partners through Project Glasswing. Three days later, on June 12, the company suspended access to both models, raising urgent questions about the safe deployment of frontier artificial intelligence.

## The Model

Mythos represents a new capability tier above the Opus class in Anthropic's Claude product line. Mythos Preview was launched on April 7, 2026 through Project Glasswing, a restricted-access partnership involving 12 technology companies and the United States government. The model was initially available only to approved security researchers and government agencies. On June 9, Anthropic released two new variants simultaneously: Fable 5, a safety-restricted version available to the general public, and Mythos 5, an upgraded model for Glasswing partners. On the same day, the company confidentially filed for an initial public offering, signalling its commercial ambitions even as it grappled with the safety implications of its most powerful creation.

## Technical Capabilities

The capabilities demonstrated by Mythos are unprecedented in the field of artificial intelligence. The model found more than 10,000 high-severity vulnerabilities in critical software systems. It produced its first Firefox exploit within one hour of analysing a patch that had been released just 18 days earlier. For Windows kernel vulnerabilities, Mythos generated its first proof-of-concept in 31 minutes and produced 18 out of 21 working exploits in under six hours. Among its most remarkable achievements was the discovery of a software bug that had lain dormant for 27 years. Beyond cybersecurity, the model demonstrated novel biology hypotheses that were preferred 80 per cent of the time over those generated by Opus-class models, and conducted autonomous multi-species single-cell research over the course of a week.

## Safety Concerns

The United Kingdom's Artificial Intelligence Safety Institute confirmed that Mythos Preview can autonomously attack vulnerable networks, validating Anthropic's decision to restrict access. The company built safety classifiers into Fable 5 that block queries related to cyberattacks, biological weapons, and chemical weapons. Despite these safeguards, Anthropic suspended access to both Mythos 5 and Fable 5 on June 12 â€” just three days after launch â€” citing the need for additional safety testing. On June 22, Fable 5 was pulled from standard subscription plans and moved to a usage-credit model, further restricting public access. The pattern of release and retreat highlights the tension between Anthropic's commercial ambitions and its safety obligations.

## Industry Response

The technology industry has reacted with a mixture of excitement and alarm. Cybersecurity firms have hailed Mythos as a potential breakthrough for vulnerability research while expressing concern about its potential for misuse in the wrong hands. Competing AI laboratories are closely watching Anthropic's approach to responsible deployment. The suspension of both models has intensified the debate about whether the AI industry is prepared to manage the risks of increasingly capable systems. Anthropic's dual-model strategy â€” a restricted high-capability version for trusted partners and a safety-filtered version for the public â€” is widely regarded as a reasonable approach, but the rapid suspension demonstrates how fragile safe AI deployment remains in practice.

## Regulatory Implications

Mythos has become a case study for regulators worldwide who are grappling with the implications of frontier AI. The UK AISI's evaluation of Mythos Preview, published on April 13, was one of the first independent assessments of a model's ability to cause harm at scale. The European Union's AI Act, still in its implementation phase, may require reevaluation in light of Mythos's demonstrated capabilities. In the United States, the existing executive order on AI safety did not anticipate a model capable of autonomously discovering zero-day vulnerabilities at this speed and scale. Anthropic's suspension of access suggests that existing regulatory frameworks are inadequate and that companies may need to self-regulate in the absence of clear government guidance. Mythos is the first AI model whose creators judged it too capable to release without a safety bypass switch, and the questions it raises will define AI governance for years to come.
