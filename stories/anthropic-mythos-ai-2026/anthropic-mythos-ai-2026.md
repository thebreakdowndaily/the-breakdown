## Cold Open

Mythos Preview produced its first Firefox exploit in one hour. It generated 18 out of 21 working Windows kernel exploits in under six hours. It unearthed a software vulnerability that had been embedded in a critical system for 27 years. On April 7, 2026, Anthropic released this model exclusively through Project Glasswing, a restricted-access partnership with 12 companies and the US government. On June 9, it launched two variants simultaneously — Fable 5 for the general public and Mythos 5 for Glasswing partners — and confidentially filed for an IPO that same day. By June 12, it had suspended access to both. The model is the most capable cybersecurity AI ever built. It is also the first whose creators decided it could not be trusted in anyone's hands.

## The Model

Mythos sits a full capability tier above Opus, the previous ceiling of Anthropic's Claude product line. Project Glasswing was established as a controlled test environment: approved security researchers and government agencies could probe Mythos Preview's abilities while attempting to contain its outputs. The dual-model launch on June 9 was intended as a template for responsible deployment — a filtered version for the public and an unrestricted version for verified partners. The IPO filing on the same day revealed the commercial stakes. Anthropic is pursuing a valuation north of $80 billion, and Mythos is the product that justifies the number. Three days later, the template collapsed.

## Technical Capabilities

The UK's Artificial Intelligence Safety Institute confirmed in an April 13 evaluation that Mythos Preview can autonomously attack vulnerable networks. The model's track record backs the finding: more than 10,000 high-severity vulnerabilities identified across critical software systems; a Firefox exploit generated within 60 minutes of analysing a patch released 18 days earlier; 18 of 21 Windows kernel exploits produced in under six hours, with the first proof-of-concept arriving in 31 minutes. Beyond cybersecurity, the model generated novel biology hypotheses preferred 80 per cent of the time over Opus-class outputs and conducted autonomous multi-species single-cell research over a week. These capabilities extend well beyond the cybersecurity domain for which Mythos was primarily designed.

## Safety Concerns

Anthropic built safety classifiers into Fable 5 that block queries related to cyberattacks, biological weapons, and chemical weapons. On June 12, the company deemed these insufficient and suspended access to both models. On June 22, Fable 5 was removed from standard subscription plans and shifted to a usage-credit model, further narrowing public availability. The pattern — release, restrict, retreat — tracks an organisation struggling to reconcile commercial pressure with safety obligations. Anthropic's dual-model strategy was widely viewed as a reasonable compromise. Its rapid suspension demonstrates that no current framework offers a reliable path for deploying AI this capable at scale.

## Industry Response

Cybersecurity firms have hailed Mythos as a potential breakthrough for vulnerability research while privately expressing concern about its applicability in offensive contexts. Competing AI laboratories are watching closely: if Anthropic cannot manage Mythos's deployment, no company with a less rigorous safety culture can claim to manage comparable systems. The episode has intensified the debate about whether the AI industry is prepared to govern frontier models without regulatory compulsion. Anthropic's approach — restricted high-capability for trusted partners, safety-filtered for the public — is regarded as the current state of the art in responsible deployment. The speed of its failure suggests the state of the art is not yet adequate.

## Regulatory Implications

Mythos has become a reference case for regulators worldwide. The UK AISI's April 13 evaluation was among the first independent assessments of a model's capacity for autonomous harm. The European Union's AI Act, still in implementation, may require revision to account for models that can discover zero-day vulnerabilities faster than human researchers. The US executive order on AI safety did not anticipate self-censorship by the model's creator as a primary safeguard. Anthropic's suspension of access implies that existing regulatory frameworks lack the specificity to govern frontier AI, and that companies must self-regulate in the absence of guidance. Mythos is the first AI whose builders concluded it was too capable to release without a safety kill-switch. The questions it raises will define AI governance for the next decade.
