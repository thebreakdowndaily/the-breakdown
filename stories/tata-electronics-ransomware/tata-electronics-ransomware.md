# 630GB of Secrets: How a Ransomware Gang Stole Apple's iPhone Specs and Tesla's Trade Secrets Through an Indian Factory

**THE BREAKDOWN — Technology / Cybersecurity / Supply Chain**

---

## Cold Open

On June 22, 2026, Tata Electronics — a critical supplier to both Apple and Tesla — confirmed it had been hit by a devastating ransomware attack. The group behind it, World Leaks, claims to have stolen 204,300 files totalling 630 gigabytes of data. Among the alleged haul are Apple's proprietary iPhone circuit board inspection standards, Tesla's engineering drawings marked "TRADE SECRET" for the Model 3 revamp, employee passport scans, internal emails, and SAP records. The files have been on the dark web since at least June 10. Tata confirmed the breach twelve days later. Apple is investigating. Tesla has said nothing. India's ambition to become an electronics manufacturing superpower just hit its most serious credibility test yet.

## The Attack

World Leaks emerged in January 2025 as a rebrand of Hunters International, which inherited tactics from the Hive ransomware operation dismantled by the FBI in 2023. Unlike traditional ransomware groups that encrypt files, World Leaks operates an Extortion-as-a-Service platform. Affiliates steal data using custom tools and publish it on a dark web leak site unless the victim pays. No encryption required — just theft and the threat of exposure. The group's known initial access vector is compromised VPN credentials lacking multi-factor authentication, the kind of vulnerability that proliferates in companies that prioritise production over security. World Leaks has claimed 125 victims globally, with the United States and India as primary targets. Previous victims include Dell Technologies (1.3 terabytes stolen in 2025) and Nike.

## Impact on iPhone Production

Tata Electronics is Apple's primary iPhone assembler in India, responsible for roughly one-third of the country's iPhone output. The company entered iPhone manufacturing in 2023 by acquiring Wistron's India operations, and expanded by taking a 60 per cent stake in Pegatron India in 2024. Its Hosur campus — the site referenced in 33 of the leaked file folders — is the nerve centre of India's iPhone production. The breach puts Apple's manufacturing diversification strategy under scrutiny. Apple has invested heavily in reducing dependence on China, and Tata is its biggest bet. Every supply chain security failure strengthens the case for remaining dependent on China's more mature manufacturing ecosystem. Apple's cybersecurity team is investigating, though the company has not confirmed whether its data was compromised.

## Ransomware Landscape

Indian manufacturing firms spend less than 2 per cent of revenue on IT security, compared with 5 to 7 per cent at comparable American and European firms. Many rely on basic antivirus tools rather than endpoint detection systems. Multi-factor authentication on VPN access is inconsistently deployed. World Leaks' playbook is simple: find a supplier with valuable intellectual property but weak authentication, exfiltrate data using custom tools that mimic legitimate traffic, and publish the haul unless paid. The Tata breach fits this pattern precisely. Cybersecurity experts have warned about this supply chain attack model for years. The target is rarely the Fortune 500 company itself; it is the vendor three steps down the chain. When a ransomware gang breaks into Tata, they steal the secrets of every company that trusts Tata with its intellectual property.

## Response and Recovery

Tata has confirmed the incident but has not disclosed when it occurred, how attackers gained access, or whether customers were affected. There is a critical gap between detection — "a few weeks ago," per Tata — and public disclosure on June 22, during which the data was already on the dark web. India's Computer Emergency Response Team has not responded to media inquiries. The company's credibility as a trusted Apple and Tesla supplier is damaged. For the 75,000-plus Tata employees whose passport data and personal information are now in criminal hands, the risk of identity theft is real. This breach is a stress test of India's credibility as a manufacturing hub and a warning about the fragility of supply chain trust. The 630 gigabytes on the dark web may or may not contain authentic secrets, but the damage to trust — between Tata and its clients, between India and global manufacturers — is already done.
