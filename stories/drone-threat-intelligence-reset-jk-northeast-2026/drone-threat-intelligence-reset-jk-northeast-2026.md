## Cold Open

India is undertaking the most significant intelligence restructuring in over a decade, merging intelligence operations for Jammu and Kashmir and the Northeast into a unified framework for the first time. The catalyst is the drone: the Ministry of Home Affairs documents two to three drone sightings and at least one confirmed incursion every day along India's borders. In May 2025, Pakistan launched 300 to 400 drones targeting approximately 36 locations across J&K during Operation Sindoor. The reset marks an official recognition that traditional intelligence-gathering methods — built around human sources and physical infiltration routes — cannot counter threats that increasingly arrive from the air.

## The Drone Threat

The drone challenge has evolved along two fronts. On the western border, Lashkar-e-Taiba has turned to UAVs to smuggle arms across the LoC after enhanced fencing sealed traditional infiltration routes. The NIA's chargesheet in the Pahalgam terror attack flagged cross-border drone dropping as a critical emerging threat. In the Northeast, Myanmar-based insurgent groups equipped with European drone technology are supplying UAVs to ULFA, NSCN, and Kuki-Zo aligned organisations. The common thread: commercially available quadcopters are becoming cheaper, harder to detect, and capable of swarming tactics that overwhelm point-defence systems.

## The Intelligence Reset

The new framework represents a paradigm shift in intelligence architecture. The unified approach for J&K and the Northeast — the first time both theatres have been brought under a single command — aims to facilitate seamless information sharing between central agencies and state police forces. The reset blends technological surveillance with revived human intelligence networks, recognising that drones alone cannot substitute for ground-level human sources. Advanced counter-drone systems are being deployed at scale: SAKSHAM, Akashteer, the D4 System, KAVACH laser systems, SkyStriker kamikaze drones, directed-energy weapons, and a range of jammers and spoofers. High-altitude long-endurance drones are providing persistent surveillance along the LoC and the Myanmar border.

## Lessons from the Anti-Maoist Campaign

The intelligence reset draws on India's successful campaign against Left Wing Extremism, which demonstrated that strong human intelligence combined with community trust-building and technological integration can dismantle insurgent networks. Security forces built a dense network of village-level informants, leveraged surrendered cadres for actionable intelligence, and deployed aerial surveillance in dense forest terrain. The same principles are now being adapted: rebuilding the HUMINT network that atrophied between 2022 and 2024, establishing community liaison mechanisms in border villages, and integrating drone detection data with human intelligence to create a comprehensive threat picture.

## Challenges Ahead

Rebuilding human intelligence networks takes years and requires trust eroded by operational neglect. The unified framework must navigate two different operational environments — J&K's high-intensity Pakistan-focused theatre versus the Northeast's multiple insurgent groups operating across porous Myanmar borders. Coordination between central agencies and state police forces, historically a weak link, will be critical. The success of the reset depends on sustained political commitment, adequate budgetary allocation, and the willingness of security agencies to embrace a genuinely integrated approach — none of which can be guaranteed in a single financial cycle.
