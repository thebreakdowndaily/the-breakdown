## Cold Open

Security agencies are undertaking a comprehensive restructuring of intelligence operations in Jammu and Kashmir and the Northeastern states, driven by the growing use of drones by terrorist groups for surveillance, weapons smuggling, and reconnaissance. The overhaul, described by officials as the most significant intelligence reset in over a decade, marks a recognition that traditional intelligence-gathering methods are no longer sufficient to counter threats that increasingly arrive from the air. For the first time, a unified intelligence framework is being developed for both regions, blending advanced technological surveillance with a concerted revival of human intelligence networks.

## The Drone Threat

The Ministry of Home Affairs has documented two to three drone sightings and at least one confirmed incursion every day along India's borders over the past five years. Lashkar-e-Taiba has been using drones to smuggle arms across the Line of Control after traditional infiltration routes were sealed by enhanced fencing. In May 2025, during Operation Sindoor, Pakistan launched 300 to 400 drones targeting approximately 36 locations across J&K. In the Northeast, Myanmar-based insurgent groups, equipped with European drone technology, are supplying UAVs to ULFA, NSCN, and Kuki-Zo aligned organisations. The NIA's chargesheet in the Pahalgam terror attack flagged cross-border drone dropping as a critical emerging threat, alongside a documented decline in human intelligence between 2022 and 2024.

## The Intelligence Reset

The new framework represents a paradigm shift in how intelligence is gathered, analysed, and acted upon. The reset prioritises blending technological surveillance with revived human intelligence networks, recognising that drones alone cannot substitute for ground-level human sources. Advanced counter-drone systems are being deployed at scale, including SAKSHAM, Akashteer, the D4 System, KAVACH laser systems, SkyStriker kamikaze drones, directed-energy weapons, and a range of jammers and spoofers. High-altitude long-endurance drones are providing persistent surveillance along the LoC and the Myanmar border, feeding real-time intelligence to ground commanders.

## Lessons from the Anti-Maoist Campaign

The intelligence reset draws heavily on the successful model employed against Left Wing Extremism. The anti-Maoist campaign demonstrated that a combination of strong human intelligence, community trust-building, and technological integration can effectively dismantle insurgent networks. Security forces established a dense network of village-level informants, leveraged surrendered cadres for actionable intelligence, and deployed aerial surveillance to track Maoist movement in dense forest terrain. The same principles are now being adapted for the drone threat: rebuilding the HUMINT network that atrophied between 2022 and 2024, establishing community liaison mechanisms in border villages, and integrating drone detection data with human intelligence to create a comprehensive threat picture.

## Challenges Ahead

Rebuilding human intelligence networks takes years and requires trust eroded by operational neglect. Commercially available quadcopters are becoming cheaper and harder to detect, while terrorist groups experiment with swarming tactics that overwhelm point-defence systems. The unified framework must navigate differing operational environments: J&K's high-intensity theatre versus the Northeast's multiple insurgent groups operating across porous borders. Coordination between central agencies and state police forces, historically a weak link, will be critical. The success of the intelligence reset will ultimately depend on sustained political commitment, adequate budgetary allocation, and the willingness of security agencies to embrace a genuinely integrated approach.
