India's Intelligence Reset: Countering the Drone Threat in J&K and the Northeast

A single drone drop in Baramulla that evaded all detection systems triggered the deepest restructuring of India's counter-terror intelligence apparatus since the 2008 Mumbai attacks — unifying J&K and the Northeast under a coordinated framework for the first time.

In early 2024, a quadcopter crossed the Line of Control under cover of darkness, navigated through multi-tiered security cordons, and dropped a payload of 20 pistols, Rs 15 lakh in cash, and Chinese grenades in the Gogal Dara forests of Baramulla. The drop was not detected until after the terror cell recovered the weapons — weapons later used in the Pahalgam attack. The NIA documented the operation in its chargesheet. That single drone drop triggered a fundamental reassessment of India's entire intelligence architecture.

By June 2026, that reassessment produced a concrete outcome: a comprehensive restructuring of intelligence operations across J&K and the Northeast, designed to close the gaps that allowed drone-facilitated terrorism to flourish. The Intelligence Bureau, coordinating with state police and central forces, is rolling out a unified intelligence framework for both regions — the first time such an integrated approach has been attempted.

The Intelligence Reset

The new intelligence architecture is the deepest restructuring of India's counter-terror apparatus since the 2008 Mumbai attacks — an acknowledgment that the old playbook, built around sealing borders and intercepting human infiltrators, has been rendered obsolete by drone technology.

The reset is built on three interdependent pillars, each addressing a critical failure identified in post-incident reviews and the NIA's own chargesheet analysis:

🔗
Unified Structure

Single coordination framework for J&K and Northeast enabling real-time intelligence sharing, joint threat assessments, and coordinated operational responses across both regions for the first time.

📡
Tech Surveillance

Augmented SAKSHAM, Akashteer, D4 systems with additional sensors, foliage-penetrating radar, and satellite-based tracking to create a continuous drone-space monitoring network.

👥
HUMINT Revival

Rebuilding human intelligence networks that were allowed to atrophy between 2022-2024. The NIA chargesheet flagged a "critical drop" as a primary reason the Pahalgam cell operated undetected.

Pillar 1: Unified Structure. Instead of separate intelligence setups for J&K and the Northeast — each with its own hierarchies, priorities, and protocols — a single coordination framework enables real-time information sharing, joint threat assessments, and coordinated operational responses. This addresses a long-standing gap: terror groups in one region often link to networks in the other, but intelligence apparatuses rarely connected the dots. The LeT network behind the Baramulla drone drop had cross-linkages with operatives in Bangladesh and Myanmar identified only after the fact.

Pillar 2: Technological Surveillance Upgrade. India already deploys SAKSHAM (Situational Awareness for Kinetic Soft and Hard Kill Assets Management), the Akashteer counter-drone air-defence system, and the D4 (Drone Detect, Deter and Destroy) System. These are being augmented with foliage-penetrating radar capable of detecting small UAVs through dense tree cover and satellite-based tracking to create what the Observer Research Foundation calls a "drone-space panopticon."

Pillar 3: Human Intelligence Revival. This is the most critical — and most difficult — component. The NIA's Pahalgam chargesheet flagged a "critical drop in human intelligence gathering between 2022 and 2024" as a primary reason the terror cell operated undetected, noting that sources were abandoned, creating a "deficiency of stable communication infrastructure." Rebuilding means re-establishing trust with local populations, recruiting new sources, and creating secure channels that protect both the sources and the intelligence they provide. Intelligence officials acknowledge this will take years, not months.

The Drone Threat: By the Numbers

Over the past five years, security agencies have recorded an average of 2-3 drone sightings and 1 confirmed incursion daily along India's borders — roughly 1,000-1,500 drone events per year, overwhelming existing detection infrastructure.

The escalation trajectory is stark. In 2020, sightings were sporadic — limited to reconnaissance near border outposts. By 2022, the frequency tripled, with payload drops reported across the International Border and LoC. By 2024, drones had become the primary delivery mechanism for weapons and narcotics. Operation Sindoor in May 2025 marked a step-change: 300-400 drones in a coordinated swarm targeting 36 locations from Kashmir to Sir Creek, demonstrating the threat had evolved from smuggling nuisance to strategic military challenge.

How Terror Groups Use Drones

The threat is multifaceted and evolving rapidly. Intelligence assessments identify four primary use cases, each demanding a different countermeasure:

1. Weapons Smuggling. Lashkar-e-Taiba has systematically shifted to drones after traditional infiltration routes — the "launch pads" across the LoC — came under tighter surveillance. The NIA's Pahalgam chargesheet documented a drone drop in Baramulla that evaded all detection systems, delivering 20 pistols, Chinese grenades, and Rs 15 lakh in cash. LeT sources commercial quadcopters from Chinese manufacturers, retrofits them with enhanced payload capacity, and deploys them from launch sites within 5-10 km of the border. Each drone costs $2,000-$5,000 but delivers weapons worth 10-20 times that on the Kashmir black market.

2. Reconnaissance and Surveillance. Drones equipped with high-resolution cameras and, in some cases, thermal imaging sensors are being used to monitor security force deployments, patrol patterns, checkpoints, and bunker positions. This intelligence feeds directly into ambush planning, target selection, and route mapping for infiltration teams. In the Northeast, reconnaissance drones have been used to map Assam Rifles and army border outposts near the Manipur-Myanmar border, identifying vulnerabilities in perimeter security. The Intelligence Bureau has documented at least 12 instances since 2023 where drone surveillance preceded militant attacks.

3. Direct Attacks and Swarm Warfare. Operation Sindoor (May 2025) demonstrated the terrifying potential of drone swarm attacks when Pakistan launched 300-400 drones targeting approximately 36 locations from Kashmir to Sir Creek. The attack employed a mix of reconnaissance UAVs, explosive-laden FPV drones, and loitering munitions. While most were neutralised by Indian counter-drone systems, the sheer scale and coordination represented a paradigm shift in cross-border warfare. Defence analysts noted that the swarm overwhelmed point-defence systems through sheer numbers — a tactic that will only become more sophisticated as autonomous coordination technology improves.

4. Narcotics Trafficking. Former J&K DGP Kuldeep Khoda: "Through drones, [Pakistan] managed to avoid the human chain used earlier for sending weapons and the hawala network for funding terrorism. Now, drones are dropping weapons for use by terrorists and narcotics to fund terrorism through their sale proceeds." Heroin processed in Afghanistan is routed through Pakistan, loaded onto drones, and dropped at pre-arranged locations across the border. The Narcotics Control Bureau estimates the sale proceeds at Rs 200-300 crore annually — directly funding terror operations.

The Northeast Dimension

The drone threat in India's Northeast is different in character but equally concerning — and in some respects more complex. The region faces drones flowing in from Myanmar and the vulnerability of critical infrastructure spread across difficult terrain.

The Myanmar Factor. The civil war in Myanmar has flooded the region with drone technology. Ethnic armed groups have invested heavily in commercially available quadcopters modified for reconnaissance and explosive delivery, leveraging European hardware and Western technical expertise. Intelligence sources warn this technology is "rapidly flowing back to banned Indian insurgent organisations — ULFA, NSCN, and Kuki Zo-aligned groups," enabling precision strikes in terrain that has traditionally favoured guerrilla fighters.

Strategic Assets at Risk. Intelligence assessments identify Assam Rifles and army border outposts near the 1,643-km India-Myanmar border as the most vulnerable — many are isolated and lack drone detection coverage. IAF bases at Chabua and Tezpur, ONGC oil fields including the Numaligarh refinery, and hydroelectric projects such as the 2,000 MW Subansiri Lower Dam are all considered high-value, hard-to-defend targets. The 2025-2026 period has seen a marked increase in rocket and drone testing by militant groups, suggesting preparation for a more sophisticated phase of operations.

The NIA's International Operation. In a landmark case, the NIA arrested seven foreign nationals — one US citizen and six Ukrainians — involved in illegal importation of European-made drones from Polish and Czech manufacturers, routed through Southeast Asian transshipment points to insurgent groups in Myanmar and Mizoram. The case is being studied as a template for how drone technology flows from global markets to local battlefields.

Lessons from the Anti-Maoist Campaign

The new intelligence framework draws explicitly from India's successful counter-insurgency campaign against Maoist (Naxalite) groups in the so-called "red corridor" stretching across central and eastern India. That campaign, which has reduced Maoist influence from a peak of 96 districts to fewer than 30 today, was built on a formula that intelligence officials believe can be adapted for the drone era.

An Intelligence Bureau official explained the formula: "The human intelligence was very strong, and the locals were completely on board. Further, technical intelligence remained solid. This ensured that real-time inputs gathered on the ground could be swiftly analysed, verified, and disseminated to security forces."

Three lessons are being applied:

First, community engagement as intelligence infrastructure. In the Maoist campaign, security forces invested heavily in winning local trust — protecting villages from reprisals, keeping sources safe. The same approach is planned for J&K and the Northeast, where alienation has historically made human intelligence difficult. As one official put it: "Development is also very crucial to ensure that the locals have nothing to complain about and they remain happy."

Second, integration of human and technical intelligence. The Maoist campaign succeeded because ground-level HUMINT was corroborated by phone intercepts, drone reconnaissance, and signals intelligence. The new framework creates joint analysis centres where human-source reports and technical data are correlated in real time — directly addressing the Pahalgam failure, where technical systems operated without ground-truth confirmation.

Third, sustained operational focus. The Maoist campaign took nearly two decades. Intelligence officials caution that the drone threat cannot be neutralised quickly — HUMINT rebuilding alone will take 3-5 years, bureaucratic silos must be broken, and the technological arms race with drone-equipped groups will continue indefinitely.

Counter-Drone Arsenal: Systems Matrix

The most significant recent addition is SAKSHAM, developed by DRDO with Bharat Electronics Limited. SAKSHAM integrates radar detection, EO/IR tracking, RF-based soft-kill, and directed-energy hard-kill into a single command-and-control framework, progressively deployed along the International Border and LoC. Complementing it is Akashteer, a mobile platform for protecting high-value assets and convoys, combining radar and RF sensors with soft-kill and hard-kill options. The D4 System fills the gap between fixed and mobile defence — a transportable solution that can be rapidly deployed based on intelligence inputs.

On the kinetic side, India has inducted SkyStriker kamikaze drones and HAROP loitering munitions. The indigenously developed Nagastra-1 — man-portable, 60-minute endurance, 15 km range — offers a lower-cost alternative. The KAVACH laser system along the International Border provides early-warning and tracking for low-altitude threats, integrated with jammers. HALE drones provide persistent surveillance from above, monitoring launch sites deep inside Pakistani territory.

What Comes Next

The intelligence reset is not a quick fix. Rebuilding HUMINT networks that atrophied between 2022 and 2024 will take years. The unified framework must overcome bureaucratic silos — state police, central forces, military intelligence, and the IB each have their own cultures and turf. And the technological arms race will continue: as counter-drone systems improve, attackers will adapt with smaller, faster, more autonomous platforms operating in swarms designed to overwhelm point defences.

Officials describe the coming period as "critical." The IB has warned of a possible increase in militant activity as Pakistan and anti-India elements in the Northeast intensify efforts to exploit the transition period — accelerating drone operations before the new framework becomes fully operational.

The 2026-27 Union Budget allocated Rs 1,200 crore specifically for counter-drone systems and intelligence infrastructure across J&K and the Northeast — a 40% increase over the previous year, with additional funding through MHA's modernisation budget and DRDO's technology development fund. Intelligence officials privately acknowledge the five-year requirement is closer to Rs 10,000-12,000 crore, covering sensors, system integration, training, and HUMINT rebuilding.

Key Challenges Ahead

  • › HUMINT timeline: Rebuilding source networks from near-zero will take 3-5 years minimum — there are no shortcuts in human intelligence
  • › Bureaucratic integration: IB, state police, military intelligence, and central forces must share information in real time — a cultural shift as much as a technical one
  • › Technology adaptation: Terror groups will counter improved defences with smaller drones, lower flight profiles, autonomous navigation, and swarm tactics
  • › Budget sustainability: Rs 1,200 crore allocated for FY26-27 against an estimated Rs 10,000-12,000 crore five-year requirement
  • › Myanmar pipeline: The porous India-Myanmar border continues to serve as a conduit for European drone technology reaching Northeast insurgent groups

Final Analysis

India's intelligence reset represents a belated but necessary recognition that the old playbook no longer works. The combination of drone technology, declining HUMINT, and evolving terror tactics has created a security gap only a fundamental restructuring can close. The anti-Maoist campaign — strong HUMINT, community trust, technological integration — provides a template. But that campaign took two decades. In an era where drone technology evolves in months, not years, India does not have the luxury of time. The success of this reset depends not on the sophistication of its counter-drone radars or directed-energy weapons, but on whether it can rebuild the human networks that are, ultimately, the only thing that can tell security forces what a drone is carrying before it drops its payload.
