Super El Niño 2026 — Potentially Historic Event Could Trigger Global Food Crisis

The Breakdown · June 25, 2026 · 10 min read

On June 11, the US National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration officially declared that El Niño conditions have developed in the tropical Pacific. The announcement was not a surprise — forecast models had signalled warming since early February. What made it urgent was the scale of what is coming next. Forecasters predict a 63% chance that by winter, sea surface temperatures in the eastern tropical Pacific will exceed 2.0°C above average — the threshold for a "very strong" or "Super El Niño." If the forecasts hold, this will rank among the largest El Niño events since records began in 1950, potentially surpassing the 1997-98 and 2015-16 events that caused tens of billions in damage and altered global weather patterns for years.

The event is unfolding against a backdrop that magnifies every risk. Global temperatures are already at record levels — the last 11 years have been the warmest on record. Food systems are under strain from the US-Iran conflict, which has disrupted energy markets, fertiliser trade, and shipping through the Strait of Hormuz. And the UN's World Meteorological Organization warns that this El Niño, layered on top of human-caused climate change, could produce extreme weather on a scale the modern world has not experienced.

What the Data Shows

The ENSO (El Niño-Southern Oscillation) cycle has swung decisively into its warm phase. After a period of below-average sea surface temperatures from August 2025 through February 2026, warm anomalies emerged in the far eastern equatorial Pacific in early February. By mid-April, above-average temperatures had expanded across the central to eastern Pacific. The latest weekly Niño-3.4 index — the primary metric used to measure El Niño strength — hit +0.7°C in the week ending June 17. The easternmost Niño-1+2 region reached +2.1°C.

But the most striking signal is subsurface. The International Research Institute for Climate and Society reports that ocean heat content anomalies in the upper 300 metres between the Date Line and 80°W are now nearly double what they were during the same period in mid-June 2023, when the last El Niño was developing. This vast reservoir of warm water — stretching across thousands of kilometres — is the fuel that will drive atmospheric disruption in the months ahead.

The UK Met Office, which formally declared El Niño on June 11, noted that "some forecasts suggest much higher values that would be of record strength." The North American Multi-Model Ensemble, which aggregates the world's leading climate models, projects El Niño to intensify through the Northern Hemisphere winter of 2026-27, with strong agreement across all 24 participating models.

The 2026 El Niño is already at +1.7°C and forecast to exceed the very strong threshold. Some models project anomalies above +3.0°C, which would eclipse all events since 1950.

The Global Impact Map

El Niño does not strike evenly. It reshapes atmospheric circulation across the planet, shifting the Pacific jet stream, altering monsoon patterns, and re-routing tropical rainfall. The consequences fall into four broad categories — and the stronger the event, the more severe each becomes.

☀️

Extreme Heat

Unusually warm weather will spread across the tropics and subtropics from September, peaking Dec-Feb. Global temperatures in 2027 are almost certain to break all records.

💧

Devastating Drought

Large regions of Australia, SE Asia, Southern Africa, Central America, the Sahel, and the Indian subcontinent face severe rainfall deficits and crop failure.

⛈️

Catastrophic Flooding

East Africa, parts of Central and East Asia, northern South America, and the southern United States will see excess rainfall, landslides, and flash flooding.

🌰

Food System Collapse

Rice, wheat, maize, palm oil, sugarcane, coffee, and cocoa face 10-50% price shocks. Rice alone could surge 50-100% — triggering export bans from India and Thailand.

The $342 Billion Question: Food Prices

The most tangible impact for most people will be at the supermarket. Risilience, a climate risk modelling firm that works with Tesco, Nestlé, and Inditex, estimates that the global value of lost agricultural production from a Super El Niño could reach $342.2 billion across 11 major commodities. Their modelling shows price shocks of 10% to 50% across core commodities, with highly exposed crops like rice, palm oil, sugarcane, and coffee surging 50% to 100% — or more.

The mechanism is straightforward. Four crops — wheat, rice, maize, and soybeans — account for over 60% of global calorie consumption. Unlike regional weather events that affect one commodity at a time, El Niño triggers "teleconnections" that cause simultaneous crop failures across different continents. Because demand for staple foods is inelastic — people must eat regardless of cost — even small supply deficits produce disproportionately large price surges.

The European Commission's Joint Research Centre warns that durum wheat prices are predicted to rise sharply as the event intensifies. Maize prices may also increase. The JRC report flags "plausible scenarios" where El Niño-driven climate shocks trigger cascading humanitarian crises in countries already grappling with conflict, displacement, and food insecurity.

THE RICE EXPORT BAN RISK

If El Niño triggers major shortfalls in rice production, India, Vietnam, and Thailand could enact export bans to feed their own populations — removing millions of tonnes from the global market. India imposed a similar ban in 2023 that sent rice prices to 15-year highs. A coordinated ban across all three countries would represent the largest supply disruption in the history of the global rice trade.

Compounding Crises: The Iran War Factor

The 2026 El Niño arrives at a moment of extraordinary fragility in global food systems. The US-Iran conflict and the closure of the Strait of Hormuz have disrupted two critical agricultural inputs: fuel and fertiliser. Natural gas is the primary feedstock for nitrogen fertiliser production, and its price has tripled since the conflict began. Shipping costs have surged as vessels re-route around the Cape of Good Hope. Farmers in import-dependent regions — particularly sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia — are already reducing fertiliser application, which will reduce yields even before El Niño's weather effects materialise.

"If fuel and fertiliser prices remain high, farmers may plant less, apply less fertiliser, or struggle to move food where it's needed," the World Resources Institute notes. "A potentially historic El Niño would layer drought, heat, or flooding risks onto an already fragile system, increasing the likelihood that high costs turn into real food shortages."

The WRI also warns that El Niño's effects could persist beyond the event itself: reduced fertiliser use, poor harvests, livestock losses, higher debt, and depleted household savings all affect the next planting season. The impacts could stretch into 2028.

Humanitarian Toll: Who Will Be Hit Hardest

The JRC, using its INFORM Warning tool — which combines hazard forecasts with data on conflict, food insecurity, displacement, and economic conditions — has identified the countries at highest risk. Many are in Central Africa: Sudan, Somalia, South Sudan, and Chad, along with countries already facing active conflict, mass displacement, and severe food insecurity. These nations hold the highest warning levels globally. El Niño will not create these crises from scratch — it will make them significantly worse.

This week, UN food agencies launched a $202 million appeal to shield 8.8 million people from El Niño via "anticipatory action" — early warning systems, cash transfers, and prepositioned supplies. It is the largest such pre-emptive appeal in the UN's history, a reflection of both the expected severity of the event and the depleted state of humanitarian funding as wealthy countries cut foreign aid budgets.

Historical Context: What Past Super El Niños Cost

The two most comparable events — 1997-98 and 2015-16 — offer a grim baseline. The 1997-98 El Niño, the strongest on record until 2015-16, caused an estimated $5.7 trillion in global economic losses, according to a 2023 study in Science. It triggered droughts in Indonesia and the Amazon, floods in East Africa and California, and killed an estimated 23,000 people worldwide through extreme weather events, disease outbreaks, and malnutrition. The 1982-83 event cost the global economy an estimated $4.1 trillion and caused 2,000 deaths from floods alone in South America.

The 2015-16 El Niño was comparable in strength but occurred in a different climate state. Global temperatures in 2015 were already 1.0°C above pre-industrial levels. In 2026, they are approaching 1.5°C. The baseline has shifted. "It would not take a very strong El Niño to see records broken this year," says Tom Di Liberto, a climate scientist at NOAA.

Dr Andrew Coburn of Risilience puts it more starkly: "In today's climate reality, a single large-scale climate event can trigger correlated failures across different continents and commodities, creating systemic risks that traditional supply chain diversification can no longer mitigate."

The Bottom Line

THE VERDICT

The 2026 El Niño is shaping up to be one of the most consequential climate events of the 21st century. Not because the ocean warming itself is unprecedented — though it may be — but because it arrives in a world already stretched to breaking point. Record global temperatures. A war disrupting energy and fertiliser markets. Food systems with zero margin for error. Humanitarian funding at historic lows. And 500 million smallholder farmers — the people who feed the developing world — facing a one-two punch of drought and debt. The forecasts are clear: this El Niño will strengthen through the winter, and its effects will persist through 2027 and beyond. The question is not whether there will be a crisis. It is how bad, and where, and who the world allows to go hungry.
