France: The Epicentre

France has borne the brunt of the heatwave. The national thermal indicator — an average of daytime and nighttime temperatures across 30 meteorological stations — hit 29.8°C on June 23, then 30°C on June 24. Both days broke the previous record. In Pissos, a town in the Nouvelle-Aquitaine region southwest of Bordeaux, the mercury hit 44.3°C on June 22. Paris reached 40.3°C on June 24 — only the fourth time in 150 years the capital has crossed 40°C.

The infrastructure buckled. On the night of June 23, a heat-related transformer explosion in the Finistère department of Brittany knocked out power to 68,000 households. Up to 106,000 customers lost power at the peak. The French power grid, which relies heavily on nuclear reactors that require river water for cooling, faced dual pressure: reduced capacity from warm rivers and surging demand for fans and air conditioning.

The human toll emerged in the water. French Prime Minister Sébastien Lecornu confirmed that 40 people — most of them young — drowned between June 18 and June 24 while swimming in rivers, lakes, and coastal waters to escape the heat. Two children, aged two and four, died in a hot car in southern France. Three elderly people died from heat-related causes near Bordeaux. The Louvre and the Eiffel Tower closed early. Six thousand schools were either shut or operating on adapted schedules.

United Kingdom: 100°F Looms

The UK recorded its hottest June day ever on June 24, with 36.1°C at Gosport, Hampshire, breaking the 1976 record of 35.6°C. The Met Office issued a "red extreme heat warning" for central and southern England and Wales — only the second such warning in British history, after July 2022 when the UK exceeded 40°C for the first time. Forecasters warned that Thursday could bring 39°C, which would be the second-hottest day ever recorded in the UK.

More than 1,000 schools closed. Train operators including the Gatwick Express urged passengers not to travel. London hit 35.1°C, and humidity levels in the capital were comparable to Houston and New Orleans. The UK Health Security Agency extended the red alert through Friday. Nighttime temperatures stayed above 20°C in many areas — providing no relief — with Brize Norton in Oxfordshire recording a minimum of 22.3°C.

The heatwave coincided with London Climate Action Week, drawing 75,000 delegates including UN Secretary-General António Guterres and dozens of heads of state — a collision of climate diplomacy with climate reality that organisers called "profoundly symbolic."

Spain, Italy, and Beyond

Spain recorded its highest average daily temperatures for June since records began in 1950 — 28.08°C on Monday and 28.17°C on Tuesday. The southern parts of the country exceeded 45°C. Two elderly people died of heatstroke, one in a nursing home near Bilbao and another in Almería. Italy declared red heat alerts for 16 cities, including Milan and Rome, as temperatures exceeded 40°C.

Germany reported drownings including a 26-year-old man in the Danube near Regensburg. The Netherlands issued a code orange warning, with forecasts of 39°C by Friday. Belgium and Luxembourg also escalated their heat warnings. The heat dome is forecast to expand eastward, with Poland, Croatia, and Hungary expected to face record temperatures from Friday through the weekend. Poland's weather service warned that temperatures could break the 1921 record of 40.2°C.

The Climate Context

This is the second major heatwave to hit Europe in two months. It comes after the World Health Organization's Europe office reported this month that more than 200,000 people across Europe died from heat-related causes between 2022 and 2026 — and that most of those deaths were preventable.

The 2003 heatwave killed an estimated 15,000 people in France alone, most of them elderly in apartments and retirement homes without air conditioning. Météo-France's comparison of the current event to 2003 is a measure of its severity. But the structural vulnerability has changed: more homes have air conditioning, early warning systems are better, and red alerts trigger faster public health responses. What has not changed is the underlying trend. Analysis by Climameter found that human-caused climate change made the current heatwave up to 4°C hotter, with Paris about 2.4°C warmer, Milan 3.8°C warmer, and Zaragoza 4°C warmer than they would have been without climate change.

Climate change amplified the heatwave by up to 4°C in parts of Europe, turning a severe weather event into a record-shattering one

The Infrastructure Gap

The heatwave exposed how poorly European infrastructure — designed for a climate that no longer exists — handles extreme heat. Rail networks in the UK and France imposed speed restrictions because tracks buckle above certain temperatures. Power grids in France, already strained by maintenance issues at nuclear plants, lost transformers to heat-related failures. Schools built with large south-facing windows and no air conditioning became unsafe for children. In Paris, parents taped survival blankets to classroom windows and pooled money for shade sails.

The European Trade Union Confederation warned that employers were refusing to implement heat safety measures, calling heat-related workplace deaths "avoidable." The contrast between public awareness — London Climate Action Week drawing 75,000 delegates — and public preparedness was impossible to ignore.

The Bottom Line

THE VERDICT

Western Europe's June 2026 heatwave is not an anomaly. It is the second major heat event in two months on a continent that the WHO says has already lost 200,000 people to heat-related causes in four years. France broke its national temperature record twice in two days. The UK broke its June record with temperatures that would have been unthinkable a decade ago. Spain recorded its hottest June days since 1950. A transformer exploded in Brittany, schools closed by the thousand, and 40 people drowned because the only escape from the heat was water — and water kills people who cannot swim. Climate analysis attributes up to 4°C of this heat to human activity. The infrastructure gap, the death toll, and the timing — coinciding with the world's largest climate week — make the point more vividly than any UN report: Europe is the fastest-warming continent, and it is not ready.
