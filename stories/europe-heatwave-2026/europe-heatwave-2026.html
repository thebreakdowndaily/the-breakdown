A heatwave that would have been “virtually impossible” 50 years ago killed 1,300 Europeans in ten days. The continent’s infrastructure — railways, power plants, highways, homes — was designed for a climate that no longer exists.

From June 18 to June 30, a stationary heat dome parked over Europe, breaking all-time national temperature records in seven countries. At its peak on June 28, 191 million people experienced temperatures of 35°C or higher. France, Germany, the Czech Republic, Poland, Hungary, Slovakia, and Denmark all recorded the hottest temperatures in their national histories. Belgium’s electricity market briefly traded at €1 per kilowatt-hour. French nuclear reactors shut down because the rivers used for cooling were too warm. German highways buckled. British railway tracks warped. And across the continent, the elderly died in homes not designed for heat.

The June 2026 heatwave was not an anomaly. It was the latest data point in a trend that has made Europe the fastest-warming continent on Earth — heating at twice the global average rate.

2.1 The Temperature Records

Seven countries broke all-time national temperature records during the June 18–30 heatwave. At least five more broke June monthly records. The geographic scope was extraordinary: records fell from the Atlantic coast of France to the plains of Hungary.

Poland’s record was particularly striking. The previous national record of 40.2°C had stood since 1921 — a 105-year-old mark that fell by 0.3°C. Denmark’s all-time record of 36.4°C from 1975 was broken by 0.6°C.

2.2 The Death Toll

Official Statement WHO: 1,300+ excess deaths since June 21

Source: WHO Director-General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus | June 28 | Confidence: 90

The World Health Organization reported that more than 1,300 excess deaths had been recorded across Europe since June 21. For context, Europe recorded an estimated 60,000 heat-related deaths in the summer of 2022 and 47,000 in 2023.

Official Statement France: ~1,000 excess deaths; 1,400+/day at peak

Source: Public Health France | June 28–29 | Confidence: 85

France accounted for the majority of recorded deaths. Public Health France reported that mortality reached 1,200 per day on June 24 and exceeded 1,400 per day on June 25–26. Approximately 85% of heat-related deaths in France were among people aged 65 and older.

Official Statement France: 74 drowning deaths since June 18

Source: French authorities via Euronews | June 29 | Confidence: 75

6. What's Next

Best Case (25%)

Temperatures ease in early July. Countries accelerate heat health action plans. EC mandates heat-proof infrastructure standards. The event becomes a catalyst for systemic adaptation.

Most Likely (55%)

Worst Case (20%)

Omega block persists through July. Mortality exceeds 60,000 in a single month. Grid failures cascade. Water shortages develop. Political will for climate action fractures.
