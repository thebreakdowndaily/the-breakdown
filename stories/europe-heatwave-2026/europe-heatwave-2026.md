## Cold Open

On June 24, 2026, France recorded its hottest day since modern records began in 1947, with the national thermal indicator reaching 30°C — shattering the record of 29.8°C set just the previous day. The peak temperature anywhere in France was 44.3°C in Pissos, Landes. Paris recorded 40.3°C — only the fourth time in 150 years the capital has crossed that threshold.

The United Kingdom recorded 36.1°C in Gosport, the highest June temperature ever measured in the country, breaking the previous record of 35.6°C set in 1976. Spain recorded daily average temperatures not seen since 1950. In Brittany, a transformer explosion in Finistère left 68,000 homes without power in the middle of the heatwave. And across western Europe, at least 40 people drowned since June 18 while attempting to cool off in rivers, lakes, and coastal waters.

## Record Temperatures

The heatwave began on June 17 and intensified rapidly over a week. France's national thermal indicator hit 29.8°C on June 23 — already a record — before climbing to 30°C on June 24. The UK's June record triggered only the second-ever red "risk to life" weather warning in British history. Forecasts suggest the UK could reach 39°C on June 25, which would be the second-hottest day ever recorded in the country, behind only the 40.3°C registered in July 2022. The heatwave is expected to extend into eastern Europe between June 25 and 27, affecting Poland, Croatia, and Hungary.

## The Science Behind It

The extreme temperatures result from a heat dome — a persistent area of high pressure that traps hot air over a region and prevents cooler maritime air from moving in. Climameter analysis indicates that climate change made this heatwave up to 4°C hotter than it would have been in a world without anthropogenic global warming.

The World Health Organization's European office has warned that Europe recorded over 200,000 heat-related deaths across the continent in the past four years alone. Europe is the fastest-warming continent on the planet, with temperatures rising at roughly twice the global average rate. This heatwave represents the second major extreme heat event to affect the region in just two months. The combination of a naturally occurring heat dome and the underlying warming trend creates a pattern in which records are not merely broken but shattered by significant margins.

## Human and Economic Toll

Nearly 6,000 schools in France were closed or forced to adapt. In the UK, over 1,000 schools closed and train operators urged passengers against all but essential travel as rails buckled and overhead wires sagged. The 40 drowning deaths since June 18 — concentrated in France but reported across multiple countries — represent a pattern: when extreme heat makes indoor spaces unbearable, people seek relief in water, but many, particularly the elderly and non-swimmers, lack the skills or physical capacity to stay safe.

The 68,000-home power outage in Finistère exposed the vulnerability of energy infrastructure designed for a climate that no longer exists. London Climate Action Week, with 75,000 delegates, began on June 24 — climate discussions unfolding in the midst of a climate-driven emergency.

## Adaptation and Response

The simultaneous failure of schools, railways, and power grids across western Europe has exposed a fundamental truth: European infrastructure was not built for the climate that is now arriving. France's nuclear fleet, dependent on river water for cooling, faces increasing stress during summer heatwaves. The UK's rail network, much of it laid in the Victorian era, was not designed to operate at 40°C.

The 40 drownings are a symptom of a deeper problem — when the only escape from heat is water, and water kills people who cannot swim, a society is not adapted to its climate. Governments across Europe are now confronting the need for cooling centres, early warning systems, redesigned building codes, and public education campaigns. The question is no longer whether Europe will face more heatwaves of this intensity, but how many lives and how much economic damage will be incurred before adaptation catches up with the warming.
