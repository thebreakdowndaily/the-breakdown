## Cold Open

In the largest coordinated action of its kind, the Central Bureau of Investigation raided over 80 locations across 16 states on June 25, 2026, deploying 60 special teams to dismantle a sprawling digital arrest scam network linked to more than 200 cases. Operation Chakra-VI â€” the sixth and most expansive phase of the CBI's cybercrime crackdown â€” resulted in the arrest of two key operatives and exposed a sophisticated modus operandi that included a fake website deceptively mimicking the Supreme Court of India's official domain.

## The Raid

The operation spanned states from Tamil Nadu to West Bengal, with simultaneous searches conducted in urban centres and smaller towns. The CBI arrested B. Naresh from Chennai and Sanjib Saha from Kolkata, both accused of operating shell companies and maintaining a network of mule bank accounts used to launder approximately Rs 2 crore in scam proceeds. Investigators seized digital devices, incriminating documents, and evidence linking the accused to a larger syndicate that may have defrauded victims across multiple jurisdictions. The scale of the operation â€” 80-plus locations, 60 teams, 16 states â€” reflects the depth and geographical spread of the scam network, which the CBI believes has been active for several years.

## The Fake Supreme Court Website

One of the most striking elements of the case is the scammers' use of a domain deceptively similar to the Supreme Court's official website. The fraudsters registered a lookalike URL and populated it with forged court orders, fabricated arrest warrants, and fake case details designed to convince victims that they were facing genuine legal proceedings. The CBI's investigation was triggered by a formal complaint from the Supreme Court Registry, which detected the fraudulent domain and alerted law enforcement. The fake site was meticulously designed: it replicated the Supreme Court's visual identity, included fabricated cause lists, and even featured counterfeit judicial officer profiles to lend credibility to the deception.

## Modus Operandi

The scam followed a chillingly effective script. Victims would receive calls or video calls from individuals impersonating police officers, CBI officials, or judicial officers. The caller would allege involvement in a serious crime â€” money laundering, drug trafficking, or terrorism â€” and inform the victim that a warrant had been issued for their arrest. To lend credibility, the fraudsters directed victims to the fake Supreme Court website, where the fabricated charges and forged warrants could be viewed. The victim was then placed under "digital arrest" â€” confined to their home and required to remain on a video call for hours or days â€” while being coerced into transferring money to designated accounts to secure bail or avoid prosecution. The psychological manipulation was extreme: victims were isolated, threatened, and prevented from contacting family members or legal counsel.

## International Dimension

Evidence gathered during the raids suggests that the scam network may have targeted foreign nationals as well, expanding its victim pool beyond India. The CBI has alerted overseas law enforcement agencies through Interpol channels, sharing intelligence on the modus operandi, the domain registration patterns, and the money trail. The international dimension adds significant complexity to the investigation, requiring coordination with multiple foreign jurisdictions. Operation Chakra-VI builds on previous phases: Chakra I-III targeted fraudulent call centres, while Chakra IV-V focused on illegal payment gateways and mule accounts. The CBI has indicated that further arrests are expected as forensic analysis of seized devices continues, and that Operation Chakra-VII is already in the planning stages.
