CBI Operation Chakra-VI

The Central Bureau of Investigation raided 80 locations across 16 states, deployed 60 special teams, and arrested two financial facilitators in the sixth iteration of its cybercrime crackdown. At the centre of the investigation: a website that impersonated the Supreme Court of India.

The Bust: 80 Locations, 16 States, 60 Teams

On June 25, 2026, the Central Bureau of Investigation executed one of the largest coordinated cybercrime operations in Indian history. Under the codename Operation Chakra-VI, the agency mobilised 60 specialised teams to search more than 80 locations across 16 states and union territories, targeting a network allegedly linked to over 200 cases of digital arrest fraud nationwide.

The searches spanned the country's geography: from Punjab and Gujarat in the west to Assam and Manipur in the northeast, and from Delhi and Haryana in the north to Tamil Nadu and Karnataka in the south. The full list of states covered included Punjab, Gujarat, Delhi, Maharashtra, Haryana, Tamil Nadu, Telangana, Andhra Pradesh, Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, Assam, West Bengal, Manipur, Karnataka, and Odisha.

According to a CBI spokesperson, the operation was aimed at "dismantling a targeted operational network involved in over 200 cases of digital arrest scams" — a crime that has emerged as one of India's fastest-growing forms of cyber-enabled fraud.

What makes this operation different from previous cybercrime crackdowns is not just its scale, but the audacity of the scam it targeted: a network that registered a fraudulent domain name designed to impersonate the Supreme Court of India, uploaded forged court orders, and convinced victims they were facing legitimate legal action.

The Fake Supreme Court Website

The investigation was triggered by a complaint from the Registry of the Supreme Court of India itself. The CBI discovered a fraudulently registered domain bearing a URL that the agency described as "deceptively similar" to the official website of the Supreme Court. The domain was allegedly used to create a convincing replica of the court's portal, complete with fabricated case listings, digital warrants, and enforcement notices.

"Investigation has revealed that the perpetrators uploaded forged and fabricated documents, including fake orders resembling those issued by courts and various law enforcement agencies, to lend credibility to their fraudulent activities," the CBI said in a statement.

This marks an escalation in the sophistication of digital arrest scams in India. While previous iterations involved fake police uniforms and video call backdrops modelled on police stations, the creation of a functional fake Supreme Court website represents a new level of criminal infrastructure. The site was not merely a static page — it was a working platform where victims could be instructed to "verify" their case status, view fabricated warrants, and receive payment instructions that appeared to come from the highest court of the land.

How the Fake Website Worked

Victims were directed to a URL that closely resembled the official supremecourtofindia[.]nic[.]in domain. The fraudulent site displayed forged case details, fake arrest warrants bearing the Supreme Court's seals, and fabricated notices from law enforcement agencies. Victims who visited the site were already primed by phone calls impersonating police or CBI officials, making the digital evidence appear legitimate.

The Two Arrests: Shell Companies and Mule Accounts

Two individuals were arrested during the operation: B Naresh, taken into custody from Chennai, Tamil Nadu, and Sanjib Saha, arrested in Kolkata, West Bengal. According to the CBI, both were allegedly involved in "the incorporation of shell companies and the opening and operation of mule bank accounts" that formed the financial backbone of the scam network.

Mule accounts — bank accounts opened by individuals who then transfer access to criminals — are a critical component of cyber fraud infrastructure. They allow scammers to receive and launder money while remaining anonymous. The arrest of the financial facilitators represents a significant blow to the network's ability to move money, though the CBI acknowledged that the investigation is ongoing and further arrests are expected.

"These accounts were reportedly used for laundering approximately Rs 2 crore of suspected proceeds of crime."

How Digital Arrest Scams Work

Digital arrest scams have become one of India's most pernicious forms of cyber fraud, targeting victims across income levels and age groups. The modus operandi follows a consistent pattern:

  1. Initial Contact: Victims receive a phone call or video call from individuals posing as police officers, CBI investigators, or judges. The calls often display official-looking caller ID spoofing.
  2. Accusation: The victim is told they have been implicated in a serious crime — money laundering, drug trafficking, or involvement with terrorist financing — and that a warrant has been issued for their arrest.
  3. Digital Arrest: The victim is placed under "digital arrest" — a concept with no legal basis but terrifying implications. They are told they are under continuous surveillance and must not leave their home or contact anyone.
  4. Verification: To prove the legitimacy of the threat, victims are directed to the fake Supreme Court website where they can "verify" the case against them by entering a case number. The fabricated case details and warrants displayed on the site appear official.
  5. Payment: Victims are instructed to transfer money to designated accounts for "verification" or "security deposits," with promises of refund once their innocence is established. The money is immediately siphoned through mule accounts.

The scam exploits a fundamental asymmetry: the fraudsters have prepared an elaborate technological infrastructure, while victims are caught off guard, frightened, and rushed into making decisions under extreme psychological pressure.

Bottom Line

Digital arrest scams represent a uniquely 21st-century form of crime: they combine sophisticated social engineering, technical infrastructure (fake government websites, caller ID spoofing, mule account networks), and psychological manipulation (fear of arrest, urgency, isolation). The CBI's Operation Chakra-VI targeted not just individual scammers but the entire infrastructure chain — from the domain registrants to the financial facilitators who launder the proceeds.

The Money Trail: Rs 2 Crore Through Shell Companies

The CBI's investigation traced approximately Rs 2 crore in suspected proceeds of crime flowing through a network of shell companies and mule accounts operated by the arrested accused. Shell companies — registered entities with no genuine business operations — were used to create a veneer of legitimacy for financial transactions, while mule accounts provided multiple layers of separation between the scam operators and the money.

During the searches across 80 locations, the CBI seized "incriminating documents, digital devices, mobile phones, and records relating to bank transactions," all of which are now being subjected to detailed forensic examination. The agency deployed "advanced forensic tools and technical expertise to identify critical elements of the criminal infrastructure operating both within India and abroad."

The forensic analysis is expected to reveal additional links in the financial chain, potentially leading to more arrests and the identification of the masterminds behind the network. The CBI's approach — targeting the financial facilitators rather than just the call centre operators — reflects a strategic shift toward dismantling the economic infrastructure that enables cyber fraud at scale.

The International Dimension

Perhaps the most concerning revelation from the CBI's investigation is that the network may have targeted victims beyond India's borders. The agency stated that preliminary findings suggest "nationals of several other countries may have been defrauded by the same network."

"Concerned law enforcement agencies in those jurisdictions are being alerted through appropriate channels," the CBI said, signalling that the investigation has an international component that could trigger cross-border cooperation and parallel prosecutions.

This aligns with a broader global trend: digital arrest scams that originated in India have been increasingly targeting victims in the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, Australia, and Southeast Asia. The fake Supreme Court website — a domain that would appear credible to anyone familiar with Indian institutions — could have been used to defraud both Indian citizens and foreign nationals of Indian origin.

The international dimension also complicates the investigation. Tracing money flows across borders, coordinating with foreign law enforcement agencies, and identifying victims in multiple jurisdictions requires resources and diplomatic cooperation that extend well beyond a domestic police operation.

The Operation Chakra Series

Operation Chakra-VI is the latest iteration of an ongoing CBI campaign against cybercrime infrastructure that began in 2023. Previous operations in the series have targeted different layers of the cyber fraud ecosystem:

  • Chakra-I: Initial crackdown targeting illegal call centres and phishing operations
  • Chakra-II: Focused on payment gateway aggregators and digital payment fraud
  • Chakra-III: Targeted SIM card fraud and telecom infrastructure abuse
  • Chakra-IV: Expanded to include social media account takeovers and investment scams
  • Chakra-V (July 2025): Raided 7 states, arrested 3 for operating mule bank accounts, focusing on the financial scaffolding of cybercrime
  • Chakra-VI (June 2026): The largest operation yet — 80 locations, 16 states, targeting the digital arrest ecosystem including the fake Supreme Court website

The evolution of the Operation Chakra series reveals a strategic progression. Earlier operations went after the visible tip of the iceberg — call centre operators and SIM card vendors. Later iterations have targeted the infrastructure deeper in the chain: payment gateways, mule accounts, shell companies, and now, the creators of fraudulent institutional websites.

Operation Chakra Series — Escalating Scope

Analysis: What This Means

Operation Chakra-VI reveals several important truths about the state of cybercrime in India and the institutional response to it.

First, the sophistication gap is narrowing. Scammers are no longer operating from makeshift call centres with basic phone banks. The creation of a functional fake Supreme Court website demonstrates significant technical capability — domain registration, web hosting, content management, and ongoing maintenance. This is organised crime with a technology budget.

Second, the financial infrastructure is the weak point. The CBI's focus on shell companies and mule account operators is strategically sound. While call centre operators are replaceable, the financial channels that move illicit money are harder to rebuild. By targeting the facilitators who open shell companies and operate mule accounts, the CBI is attacking a bottleneck in the scam supply chain.

Third, the scale of the problem exceeds any single operation. Two hundred linked cases across 16 states, with international victims, suggests that digital arrest scams have become industrialised. The CBI's 60 teams and 80 search locations, while impressive, represent a response to a problem that has already reached epidemic proportions.

Fourth, the Supreme Court angle is a watershed moment. The impersonation of India's highest judicial institution represents an escalation that carries symbolic weight. A complaint from the Supreme Court Registry signals that the judiciary itself recognises the threat, and the CBI's response suggests that this case will receive priority attention.

"Leveraging advanced forensic tools and technical expertise, the CBI identified critical elements of the criminal infrastructure operating both within India and abroad."

CBI statement on Operation Chakra-VI

The operation also raises questions about prevention. Digital arrest scams have been proliferating for years, with repeated warnings from government agencies. The Ministry of Home Affairs has issued public advisories about the "digital arrest" modus operandi, and platforms like Skype have been called upon to block accounts used for these scams. Yet the problem has continued to grow.

Regulatory gaps remain: domain registration in India allows for anonymous registration, making it difficult to trace the individuals behind fraudulent websites. Mule accounts proliferate because banks face limited incentives to actively monitor account activity for fraud indicators. And the cross-border nature of the crime means that even when Indian authorities act, perpetrators operating from outside the country remain difficult to reach.

Final Analysis

Operation Chakra-VI is the CBI's most ambitious cybercrime operation to date — 80 locations, 16 states, 60 teams, targeting a network of 200+ digital arrest cases. The discovery of a fake Supreme Court website reveals that Indian cyber fraud has entered a new phase of institutional impersonation. The arrests of B Naresh and Sanjib Saha strike at the financial infrastructure of the scam, but the investigation's international dimension suggests that this network extends far beyond India's borders. The operation demonstrates that Indian law enforcement is capable of large-scale coordinated action against cybercrime — but the scale of the problem demands systemic reforms in domain registration, banking oversight, and international police cooperation that no single operation can deliver.
