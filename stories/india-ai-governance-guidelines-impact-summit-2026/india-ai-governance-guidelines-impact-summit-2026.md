# India's AI Governance Guidelines: Seven Sutras, Three Institutions, and a Deliberate Third Way

## Cold Open

On February 16, 2026, Prime Minister Narendra Modi inaugurated the AI Impact Summit 2026 at Bharat Mandapam in New Delhi and unveiled the India AI Governance Guidelines — a comprehensive framework built around seven guiding principles called sutras and three new institutional bodies. The guidelines deliberately reject both the EU's binding, risk-based AI Act and the US's largely hands-off approach, positioning India as a distinct voice for the Global South. The framework relies on existing legal instruments — the IT Act, DPDP Act, and criminal statutes — for enforcement, with targeted amendments proposed only where necessary. It is an attempt to regulate AI without stifling it, and to do so on Indian terms.

## The Guidelines

India's approach is calibrated to avoid two extremes. The EU's AI Act categorises applications by threat level and imposes binding obligations; the US has no federal AI law, relying on sectoral regulation and voluntary commitments. India's principle-based, voluntary framework is a deliberate third way that prioritises inclusion and development over containment. The guidelines were developed through a consultative process involving working groups and a governance drafting committee that worked through 2024 and 2025. The key design choice: minimise regulatory disruption while providing a clear framework for responsible AI innovation, using existing legal infrastructure rather than creating an entirely new regulatory apparatus.

## Seven Sutras Framework

The seven sutras form the ethical and operational foundation: Trust is the Foundation; People First; Innovation Over Restraint; Fairness and Equity; Accountability; Understandable by Design; and Safety, Resilience and Sustainability. No single sutra takes precedence — they are designed to operate as an integrated whole. The "Innovation Over Restraint" sutra is the most significant distinguishing feature from the EU approach: it commits the government to fostering rather than stifling technological progress. "Understandable by Design" demands transparency and explainability in AI systems, directly targeting the black-box problem that has bedevilled regulators globally.

## Implementation

Three new institutions will operationalise the guidelines. The AI Governance Group (AIGG) handles strategic coordination across ministries and sectors. The Technology and Policy Expert Committee (TPEC) provides expert advisory on the intersection of technical development and regulatory policy. The AI Safety Institute (AISI) focuses on testing standards, benchmarking, and safety research — analogous to AI safety bodies in the UK and US. These institutions will be supported by the IndiaAI Mission's seven deployment pillars: compute capacity, high-quality datasets, innovation centres, application development, future skills training, startup financing, and safe and trusted AI deployment. PM Modi also outlined the M.A.N.A.V. vision — Moral and Ethical Systems, Accountable Governance, National Sovereignty, Accessible and Inclusive, Valid and Legitimate — as the philosophical compass.

## Global Context

India's guidelines enter a crowded and fragmented regulatory landscape. The EU has a binding AI Act; China has a state-centric approach with licensing for algorithms and generative AI; the US has no federal law. India's principle-based, voluntary fourth path seeks to balance innovation with accountability while asserting digital sovereignty. The approach positions India as a potential model for other developing nations navigating AI regulation — offering a framework that is rigorous enough to build trust but flexible enough to adapt to rapid technological change. The question is whether voluntary compliance will be sufficient, or whether the absence of binding enforcement will eventually force India toward harder regulation as AI deployment scales.
