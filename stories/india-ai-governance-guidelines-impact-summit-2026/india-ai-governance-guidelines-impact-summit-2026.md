# India's AI Governance Guidelines: Seven Sutras and a Vision for Safe AI

## Cold Open

On February 16, 2026, Prime Minister Narendra Modi inaugurated the AI Impact Summit 2026 at Bharat Mandapam in New Delhi, a five-day event that brought together policymakers, technologists, and researchers from across the globe. On the first day of the summit, the government unveiled the India AI Governance Guidelines â€” a comprehensive framework that adopts a principle-based, techno-legal approach to artificial intelligence regulation. Rather than imposing rigid, prescriptive regulation, the guidelines rely on voluntary compliance anchored by seven guiding principles called sutras, alongside the creation of three new institutional bodies to oversee the safe and trusted development of AI in India.

## The Guidelines

The guidelines represent a deliberate Indian approach to AI governance that rejects both the heavy-handed regulation of the European Union's AI Act and the largely hands-off free-market approach of the United States. Instead, India has positioned itself as a voice for the Global South, arguing that AI should be harnessed as a tool for inclusion and development rather than feared as a threat to be contained. The framework relies on existing legal instruments â€” the Information Technology Act, the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, and relevant criminal statutes â€” for enforcement, with targeted amendments proposed only where necessary. This approach minimises regulatory disruption while providing a clear framework for responsible AI innovation. The guidelines were developed through a consultative process involving working groups and a governance drafting committee that worked through 2024 and 2025.

## Seven Sutras Framework

At the heart of the guidelines are seven sutras that collectively form the ethical and operational foundation for AI governance in India. The sutras are: Trust is the Foundation, which emphasises that AI systems must earn and maintain public confidence; People First, which prioritises human welfare and rights in AI deployment; Innovation Over Restraint, which commits to fostering rather than stifling technological progress; Fairness and Equity, which addresses algorithmic bias and inclusive access; Accountability, which establishes clear responsibility for AI outcomes; Understandable by Design, which demands transparency and explainability in AI systems; and Safety, Resilience and Sustainability, which covers technical robustness and environmental impact. These seven principles are designed to work as an integrated whole, with no single sutra taking precedence over the others.

## Implementation

To operationalise these guidelines, the government has established three new institutions. The AI Governance Group (AIGG) will handle strategic coordination across ministries and sectors. The Technology and Policy Expert Committee (TPEC) will provide expert advisory on the intersection of technical development and regulatory policy. The AI Safety Institute (AISI) will focus on testing standards, benchmarking, and safety research â€” a function analogous to AI safety bodies in the United Kingdom and the United States. These institutions will be supported by the IndiaAI Mission's seven deployment pillars: compute capacity, high-quality datasets, innovation centres, application development, future skills training, startup financing, and the deployment of safe and trusted AI. ## Global Context

India's AI Governance Guidelines enter a crowded and fragmented global regulatory landscape. The European Union has adopted the risk-based, binding AI Act that categorises applications by threat level. China has pursued a state-centric approach with licensing requirements for algorithms and generative AI services. The United States has no federal AI law, relying instead on sectoral regulation and voluntary commitments from technology companies. India's principle-based, voluntary approach represents a distinct fourth path â€” one that seeks to balance innovation with accountability while asserting digital sovereignty. The guidelines position India as a potential model for other developing nations navigating the complex terrain of AI regulation, offering a framework that is rigorous enough to build trust but flexible enough to adapt to rapid technological change.
