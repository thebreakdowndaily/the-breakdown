Introduction: A Third Way for AI Governance

In February 2026, as world leaders, technologists, and researchers gathered at Bharat Mandapam in New Delhi for the five-day AI Impact Summit 2026, India did something no other major power had done: it unveiled a comprehensive AI governance framework without passing a single new law. The India AI Governance Guidelines, released ahead of the summit, adopt a principle-based, techno-legal approach anchored in seven guiding sutras and propose three new national institutions to oversee the AI ecosystem.

The timing is deliberate. India chairs the Global Partnership on AI (GPAI) in 2026 and is positioning itself as the voice of the Global South in the global AI debate. Rather than copying the European Union's risk-tiered regulation or China's state-centric licensing model, India's framework offers what officials describe as a "third way": a flexible, principle-based architecture that relies on existing laws for enforcement while creating new institutions for coordination, standard-setting, and safety research.

The guidelines are the product of a MeitY drafting committee formed in July 2025, which spent six months reviewing India's existing legal framework, studying global regulatory approaches, and consulting stakeholders across industry, academia, and civil society. The committee's conclusion: India's existing statutes — the IT Act, the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, criminal laws, and sectoral regulations — already cover many AI-related risks. What was missing was a coordinating framework and a forward-looking institutional architecture.

The result is a document that is ambitious in scope but pragmatic in execution. It does not ban, license, or classify AI systems by risk tier. Instead, it sets out expectations: transparency reports, disclosure of AI-generated content, grievance redressal mechanisms, and stricter safeguards for high-risk applications affecting safety, fundamental rights, or livelihoods.

Bottom Line

India's AI Governance Guidelines reject the rigid regulatory approach of the EU's AI Act in favour of a principle-based framework built on seven sutras. The guidelines establish three new institutions — AIGG, TPEC, and AISI — while relying on existing laws for enforcement. PM Modi's M.A.N.A.V. vision provides the philosophical foundation, positioning India as a Global South leader in AI governance.

The Seven Sutras of India's AI Governance Framework

The seven sutras form the philosophical backbone of India's AI governance approach — each designed to guide policymakers, developers, and deployers without prescribing specific technical solutions

The Seven Sutras

The guidelines are built on seven principles, or "sutras" — a deliberate Sanskrit term that evokes the concise, aphoristic style of ancient Indian philosophical texts. Each sutra is designed to guide policymakers, developers, and deployers without prescribing specific technical solutions. Together, they form the philosophical backbone of India's approach.

Trust is the Foundation
Sutra 1
AI systems must be reliable, secure, and predictable. Trust is not optional — it is the precondition for adoption.
People First
Sutra 2
AI must augment human capabilities, not replace human judgment. Human oversight is non-negotiable in high-stakes decisions.
Innovation Over Restraint
Sutra 3
India chooses to enable innovation rather than impose prohibitions. Governance should not stifle the AI ecosystem.
Fairness & Equity
Sutra 4
AI systems must not discriminate. Datasets, algorithms, and outcomes must be tested for bias across caste, gender, region, and language.
Accountability
Sutra 5
Developers and deployers are responsible for their AI systems. Grievance redressal must be accessible and effective.
Understandable by Design
Sutra 6
AI should be transparent and explainable. Users have a right to know when they are interacting with AI.
Safety, Resilience & Sustainability
Sutra 7
AI systems must be safe, resilient to adversarial attacks, and environmentally sustainable throughout their lifecycle.

The sutras are deliberately broad — they are designed to be enduring principles that outlast any specific technology or application. As one MeitY official put it: "We are not building a law for today's AI. We are building a compass for AI we haven't imagined yet."

Three New Institutions

The guidelines propose a new institutional architecture for AI governance in India, with the Ministry of Electronics & Information Technology as the nodal ministry. This is where the framework moves from philosophy to machinery.

1. AI Governance Group (AIGG)

A high-level inter-ministerial body responsible for strategic coordination across government, industry, and academia. The AIGG will set national AI priorities, review emerging risks, and recommend policy responses. It is designed to be agile — meeting quarterly and reporting directly to the Cabinet.

2. Technology & Policy Expert Committee (TPEC)

An expert advisory body bringing together technologists, legal scholars, ethicists, and industry representatives. TPEC will provide specialised advice on technical standards, risk classification, and sector-specific guidelines. Unlike the AIGG, which is bureaucratic, TPEC is designed to be nimble and draw on external expertise from academia and the private sector.

3. AI Safety Institute (AISI)

A dedicated research and testing body focused on AI safety. AISI will develop testing standards, conduct safety research, assess risks, and operate an incident reporting mechanism. This is the closest India comes to a technical regulator — but its role is advisory and standard-setting, not enforcement. Think of it as India's answer to the UK AI Safety Institute, but with a developing-world perspective.

The three-institution structure creates a clear division of labour: AIGG sets strategy, TPEC provides expertise, and AISI handles technical safety. The effectiveness of the entire framework depends on whether these institutions are adequately resourced. The Budget 2026-27 has allocated significant funds for AI compute infrastructure and skilling, but the operational budgets for these new bodies remain unspecified.

The M.A.N.A.V. Vision

"AI must not reduce human beings to mere data points or raw material. Therefore, AI must be democratised. It must become a medium of inclusion and empowerment, especially across the Global South."

PM Narendra Modi, AI Impact Summit 2026 inaugural address

At the summit, PM Modi presented India's vision for AI through the acronym M.A.N.A.V. — the Sanskrit word for "human." It is a deliberate framing: India's approach to AI is fundamentally anthropocentric, placing human dignity and agency at the centre of technological development.

  • M — Moral & Ethical Systems: AI must be built on ethical guidelines that respect human rights and cultural contexts. India rejects the notion that ethics is a Western construct.
  • A — Accountable Governance: Transparent rules, robust oversight mechanisms, and clear lines of responsibility for AI outcomes.
  • N — National Sovereignty: Data belongs to those who generate it. India asserts the right of nations to regulate AI in accordance with their sovereign priorities.
  • A — Accessible & Inclusive: AI must not be a monopoly of a few powerful corporations or nations. It must be a multiplier for development, especially in the Global South.
  • V — Valid & Legitimate: AI systems must be lawful, verifiable, and subject to independent audit. Legitimacy comes from compliance with legal frameworks and societal values.

The M.A.N.A.V. vision is significant not just for its content but for its framing. Modi positioned India as a unique voice in the global AI debate — neither the heavy regulation of the EU nor the free-market approach of the US. Instead, India offers a "Global South" perspective: AI as a tool for inclusion, sovereignty, and development, not just efficiency and profit.

This philosophical stance has practical implications. India is pushing for AI models trained on Indian languages, datasets that reflect Indian social realities, and governance frameworks that work for countries with limited regulatory capacity. The message to other developing nations: you don't need to copy Brussels or Beijing.

Global AI Governance Approaches Compared

Global Comparison

India's guidelines enter a crowded global landscape of AI governance initiatives, but occupy a distinct position. The European Union's AI Act (effective August 2024) classifies AI systems by risk tier and imposes binding obligations with fines up to 7% of global revenue. It is the gold standard of hard regulation — comprehensive, enforceable, and prescriptive. But it is also rigid, expensive to comply with, and has been criticised for potentially stifling Europe's already-small AI sector.

China has taken a state-centric approach, requiring licenses for generative AI services, mandating content filtering, and asserting state control over algorithms. China's model prioritises social stability and party control over innovation or individual rights. It is effective at what it sets out to do, but it is not a model most democracies can or would want to replicate.

The United States has no comprehensive federal AI law. Instead, it relies on a patchwork of sectoral regulations (healthcare, finance, civil rights) plus voluntary commitments from leading AI companies, a presidential executive order, and state-level initiatives. The US approach maximises flexibility but creates regulatory uncertainty and enforcement gaps.

India's principle-based, voluntary framework sits deliberately between these poles. It is more structured than the US approach but less coercive than the EU's. It is more democratic than China's but retains the flexibility to adapt as technology evolves. The bet is that India's scale — 1.4 billion people, a vast tech talent pool, a thriving startup ecosystem — will make it the world's most important laboratory for AI governance.

The IndiaAI Mission: Seven Pillars of Deployment

The governance guidelines are part of a larger ecosystem: the IndiaAI Mission, structured around seven deployment pillars. The governance framework is Pillar 7 — "Safe and Trusted AI" — but it cannot be understood in isolation from the other six:

  1. Compute Capacity: Building GPU infrastructure through revised procurement norms. The government has reduced the minimum turnover requirement from Rs 100 Cr to Rs 50 Cr to widen participation, with Budget 2026-27 allocating significant funds for AI compute.
  2. Datasets: Creating India-specific datasets for training models on Indian languages, dialects, and contexts. This is critical for making AI work for India's linguistically diverse population.
  3. Innovation Centres: Establishing AI research and application development hubs across the country, with a focus on agriculture, healthcare, and education.
  4. Application Development: Funding AI solutions for public services — from crop yield prediction to diagnostic tools in primary health centres.
  5. Future Skills: Expanding AI education through the FutureSkills initiative and partnerships with higher education institutions. The goal is to train 500,000 AI professionals by 2028.
  6. Startup Financing: Supporting India's AI startup ecosystem through dedicated funds and incentives. India already has the third-largest AI startup ecosystem globally.
  7. Safe and Trusted AI: The governance framework itself — the guidelines, institutions, and sutras that ensure AI development remains aligned with Indian values and priorities.

The integration of governance with deployment is perhaps the most innovative aspect of India's approach. Rather than treating regulation as a separate exercise that constrains innovation, the IndiaAI Mission treats governance as one pillar among seven — a necessary enabler of deployment, not a brake on it.

What This Means for India's AI Future

India's AI Governance Guidelines represent a deliberate strategic choice: to lead through adoption and innovation rather than regulation. By positioning itself as the first Global South country to host a government-led global AI summit and release a comprehensive governance framework, India is staking a claim to leadership in the next phase of AI development.

The bet is that India's scale will make it the natural laboratory for AI deployment. The governance framework is designed to facilitate that deployment while building guardrails that evolve with the technology. Whether that bet pays off depends on execution: on whether the AISI develops credible safety standards, whether the AIGG coordinates effectively across ministries, and whether voluntary compliance produces actual accountability.

The next 12 months will be critical. The three institutions — AIGG, TPEC, and AISI — must move from paper to practice. They need budgets, staffing, and political backing. The first compliance reviews will test whether voluntary guidelines carry any real weight. And the global reaction — from investors, tech companies, and other governments — will shape whether India's "third way" becomes a model for the Global South or a footnote.

"The architecture is sound. But institutions are only as good as the people who staff them and the budgets that sustain them."

Senior MeitY official involved in drafting the guidelines

Civil society organisations have raised legitimate concerns about the lack of binding enforcement mechanisms, particularly for high-risk AI applications affecting safety, fundamental rights, and livelihoods. The reliance on voluntary compliance places significant trust in the same companies whose products have already demonstrated bias, misinformation, and safety failures globally. India's approach will need to demonstrate that principle-based governance can produce tangible accountability — not just aspirational statements.

Final Analysis

Final Analysis

India's AI Governance Guidelines are a characteristically Indian solution: ambitious in scope, pragmatic in execution, and designed to maximise flexibility. They reject the binary of "regulate heavily" vs "do nothing" in favour of a third path — institutional architecture plus voluntary standards plus existing legal enforcement. The approach has the virtue of agility: it can adapt as AI evolves without requiring new legislation each time. But agility can also mean weakness if the institutions lack teeth. The next 12 months — as AIGG, TPEC, and AISI move from paper to practice — will determine whether India's "third way" becomes a model for the Global South or a cautionary tale.
