## Cold Open

At 3:07 PM on June 22, 2026, Sukhmani Singh called his father from the second floor of a coaching centre in Lucknow: "Papa, there's a fire, please save me." Thick smoke from an AC duct fire had filled every corridor. There was one staircase, no emergency exit, and an automatic gate that jammed as panicked students rushed toward it. By the time 19 fire tenders brought the blaze under control three hours later, 15 people — most between 16 and 25 — were dead. Nine more were injured, two with spinal fractures from jumping off upper floors. The building had been approved for residential use. It operated commercially for 12 years.

## A Building With a History

The building at MS/102/D, Sector D, Aliganj was approved on August 20, 2014 under the self-certification building plan scheme — for residential use only. It was never used as a residence. By 2014, it housed an animation coaching centre, a pet shop, a veterinary clinic, and a gaming zone. In 2016, the Lucknow Development Authority detected unauthorised construction and registered Case No. 08/2016. On May 10, 2016, the competent authority issued a demolition order. On July 5, 2016 — less than two months later — the demolition order was revoked. No official record explains why. The building continued to operate.

## The 15-Metre Loophole

The building stood roughly three storeys — below the 15-metre height threshold that triggers mandatory fire NOC requirements under most state fire services acts. A senior fire department official confirmed: "Since this building was below 15 metres, it did not come under the mandatory fire clearance regime." This is a national problem. In Delhi, an estimated 95 per cent of residential buildings fall outside fire safety scrutiny because of this height-based exemption. The National Building Code of 2016 classifies buildings below 15 metres as "low-rise" with minimal requirements: hose reels, manual call points, fire extinguishers, one enclosed staircase. At 15.1 metres, requirements jump to pressurised staircases, automatic detection systems, sprinklers, and fire officer approval. The Lucknow building never crossed that threshold. It operated for over a decade without a fire NOC, emergency exits, smoke detectors, or sprinklers.

## What Went Wrong

Five systemic failures converged. The demolition order gap: LDA identified unauthorised construction in 2016, issued a demolition order, and revoked it two months later without explanation. The height exemption: the 15-metre threshold exempted the building from pre-construction fire scrutiny. Single staircase: one staircase served as both entry and exit, with an automatic gate that jammed. Mixed-use violations: a building approved for residential use operated as a coaching centre, gaming zone, pet shop, and veterinary clinic. AC duct as fire vector: preliminary findings indicate the fire started in the air conditioning duct and spread smoke rapidly — the same mechanism that killed 14 at Kamala Mills, Mumbai in 2017.

India recorded 5,888 fire deaths in 2024 — 16 per day on average — down from 17,700 in 2015, though the decline may reflect under-reporting. Sixty per cent of fire deaths occur in residential buildings. But the deadliest fires — in coaching centres, gaming zones, hotels — are concentrated in structures below fire safety scrutiny thresholds. ## What Happens Next

The structural issues will outlast the investigation. The 15-metre loophole remains. The pattern of issuing and revoking demolition orders remains. The single-staircase death trap remains. Delhi is reviewing its fire safety exemption framework after the Malviya Nagar fire killed 21 in May 2026. Telangana proposed amendments in January 2026. The question is whether these reviews will produce enforcement — or merely more reports that gather dust. India does not lack fire safety laws. The National Building Code of 2016 is comprehensive. The problem is implementation — the gap between what the law requires and what happens in the buildings where young people study, work, and live. The SIT will submit its report in seven days. The facts are already known. The question is whether anything will change.
