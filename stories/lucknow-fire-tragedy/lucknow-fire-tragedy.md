The 23-year-old was on the second floor of a three-storey commercial building on Usha Mehta Marg in Lucknow's Aliganj area, where he was attending classes at an animation coaching centre. ## A Building With a History

The building at MS/102/D, Sector D, Aliganj Scheme, had a paper trail that tells the story of regulatory failure in miniature.

Originally allotted to Vijay Kumar through a lottery on July 11, 1980, under a hire-purchase scheme, the property passed through multiple hands. In 2005, it was sold through a registered sale deed. In January 2013, Virendra Pratap Shukla and Surendra Pratap Shukla purchased it. The Lucknow Development Authority completed mutation in their favour on August 7, 2014.

The building's map was approved on August 20, 2014, under the self-certification building plan scheme â€” for residential use only. The property measured approximately 1,992 square feet, well under the thresholds that trigger mandatory fire safety scrutiny.

But the building was never used as a residence. By 2014, it was operating commercially: an animation coaching centre on the upper floors, a pet shop and veterinary clinic on the ground floor, a gaming zone on the third floor. The Rameshwaram Group ran the operation.

In 2016, the Lucknow Development Authority detected unauthorised construction and registered Case No. 08/2016 against Virendra Pratap Shukla. On May 10, 2016, the competent authority issued a demolition order against the unauthorised portions.

Then, on July 5, 2016 â€” less than two months later â€” the demolition order was revoked. The circumstances remain unexplained. No official record clarifies why the order was reversed. The building continued to operate. The coaching centre continued to pack dozens of young students into its upper floors every afternoon.

## The 15-Metre Loophole

The building stood roughly three storeys â€” well below the 15-metre height threshold that triggers mandatory fire NOC requirements under most state fire services acts. A senior fire department official confirmed to Hindustan Times: "Since this building was below 15 metres, it did not come under the mandatory fire clearance regime."

This is not a Lucknow problem. It is a national one. Under current fire safety laws across most Indian states, buildings below 15 metres â€” roughly G+4 floors â€” are exempt from mandatory fire safety certification. In Delhi, an estimated 95% of residential buildings fall outside fire safety scrutiny because of this height-based exemption. Telangana's fire services department proposed amending its Fire Services Act in January 2026 to cover buildings below 15 metres, acknowledging that "a vast number of buildings below 15 metres, where fire incidents are more frequent, remained outside our purview."

The National Building Code of 2016 classifies buildings below 15 metres as "low-rise" with minimal fire safety requirements: hose reels, manual call points, fire extinguishers, and one enclosed staircase per wing. At 14.9 metres, a building faces moderate requirements. At 15.1 metres, it crosses into a regime with substantial cost and layout implications â€” pressurised staircases, automatic detection systems, sprinklers, and fire officer approval.

The Lucknow building never crossed that threshold. And so it operated for over a decade without a fire NOC, without emergency exits, without smoke detectors, without sprinklers, and with a single staircase that served as both entry and exit.

## The Pattern

The Lucknow fire is not an isolated tragedy. It is the latest in a sequence of deadly fires in India that share the same DNA: mixed-use buildings operating without proper clearances, inadequate escape routes, regulatory systems that detect violations but fail to act on them, and young people â€” students, workers â€” who pay the price.

**Delhi, June 2023:** A fire at a coaching institute in Mukherjee Nagar injured 61 students. Two were critically injured. The common thread: buildings that violate their original approvals, regulatory bodies that issue warnings but fail to enforce them, and a culture of impunity that allows demolition orders to be revoked and commercial operations to continue unchecked.

## What Went Wrong: A Systems Failure

The Lucknow fire was not caused by a single failure. It was the product of a system that detects violations but lacks the will or mechanism to act on them.

**The demolition order gap:** The LDA identified unauthorised construction in 2016 and issued a demolition order. Two months later, it was revoked. No public explanation was provided. This pattern â€” detection, action, reversal â€” is consistent across Indian fire tragedies. In Rajkot, the gaming zone had been flagged for operating without clearances. In Delhi, coaching centres were surveyed and found non-compliant, but the surveys were periodically nullified and restarted.

**The height exemption:** The 15-metre threshold for mandatory fire safety clearance means that the vast majority of India's urban buildings â€” including those housing coaching centres, small hotels, restaurants, and mixed-use commercial operations â€” fall outside the fire department's pre-construction scrutiny. This is not a loophole that was discovered after the fact; it is a known gap that has been flagged by fire services departments, researchers, and even the National Human Rights Commission.

**Single staircase, no emergency exit:** The building had one staircase serving as both entry and exit. An automatic gate system further complicated escape when panic-stricken students rushed toward it. There was no fire escape, no alternative route, no refuge area.

**Mixed-use violations:** A building approved for residential use was operating as a commercial establishment with a coaching centre, gaming zone, pet shop, and veterinary clinic. The ground-floor pet clinic housed animals that also perished in the fire.

**AC duct as fire vector:** Preliminary findings suggest the fire started in the building's air conditioning duct. Thick smoke spread rapidly through the duct system â€” the same mechanism that killed 14 people at a restaurant in Kamala Mills, Mumbai in 2017.
