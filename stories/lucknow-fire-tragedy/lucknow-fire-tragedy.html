Lucknow Fire Tragedy: 15 Dead in Aliganj Coaching Centre

A building approved for residential use operated commercially for 12 years. A demolition order was issued—then revoked in two months. On June 22, 2026, 15 students paid the price for a system that detects violations but does not act on them.

TB The Breakdown Editorial Team June 25, 2026 10 min read Fact-checked

What happened

A fire at a coaching centre in Lucknow’s Aliganj area on June 22, 2026 killed 15 students and injured 9. The building, approved for residential use, had been operating commercially for 12 years.

Why it matters

The tragedy exposes systemic failures: revoked demolition orders, a 15-metre height loophole that exempts buildings from fire safety checks, and a regulatory culture that identifies violations but fails to enforce compliance.

Key takeaway

India does not lack fire safety laws. The problem is implementation—the gap between what the law requires and what actually happens in the buildings where young people study, work, and live.

Reading time: 10 min

At 3:07 PM on Monday, June 22, 2026, Sukhmani Singh called his father. “Papa, there’s a fire, please save me.” The 23-year-old was on the second floor of a three-storey commercial building in Lucknow’s Aliganj area, attending classes at an animation coaching centre. Within minutes, thick smoke from what investigators believe was an AC duct fire had filled every corridor. There was one staircase. No emergency exit. No alternative route. An automatic gate system at the entrance jammed as panicked students rushed toward it.

By the time 19 fire tenders, the NDRF, and SDRF brought the blaze under control after three hours, 15 people were dead. Nine more were injured, two with spinal fractures from jumping off upper floors. Most victims were between 16 and 25 years old.

Bottom Line

A building approved for residential use operated commercially for 12 years. A demolition order was issued—then revoked in two months. A 15-metre height loophole left it exempt from fire safety checks. On June 22, 2026, 15 students paid the price for a system that detects violations but does not act on them.

The 15-Metre Loophole

The building stood roughly three storeys—well below the 15-metre height threshold that triggers mandatory fire safety clearance across most Indian states. A senior fire department official confirmed: “Since this building was below 15 metres, it did not come under the mandatory fire clearance regime.”

This is not a Lucknow problem. It is a national one.

State / City Current Threshold Impact Status

The National Building Code of 2016 classifies buildings below 15 metres as “low-rise” with minimal fire safety requirements: hose reels, manual call points, fire extinguishers, and one enclosed staircase per wing. At 14.9 metres, a building faces moderate requirements. At 15.1 metres, it crosses into a regime with substantial cost and layout implications—pressurised staircases, automatic detection, sprinklers, and fire officer approval.

“Due to this existing provision, a vast number of buildings below 15 metres, where fire incidents are more frequent, remained outside our purview.”

— Telangana Fire Services Department, proposing amendment to Fire Services Act, January 2026

The Pattern: India’s Deadly Fire Cycle

The Lucknow fire is the latest in a sequence of deadly incidents that share the same DNA: buildings operating without proper clearances, inadequate escape routes, regulatory systems that detect violations but fail to act, and young people who pay the price.

The common thread: buildings that violate their original approvals, regulatory bodies that issue warnings but fail to enforce them, and a culture of impunity that allows demolition orders to be revoked and commercial operations to continue unchecked.

The Response

Within hours, the political machinery activated:

  • CM Yogi Adityanath cut short his Aligarh visit, visited the site, reviewed operations from the roof of an adjacent building, and visited injured students at KGMU. Announced Rs 5 lakh ex gratia for each deceased victim’s family.
  • PM Narendra Modi announced Rs 2 lakh from PMNRF for each deceased person’s next of kin and Rs 50,000 for each injured person.
  • Four officials suspended: Gaurav Kumar (Executive Engineer, LDA), Kamlendra Kumar Singh (Food Safety Officer), Anil Kumar (Assistant Engineer), Pramod Pandey (Junior Engineer).
  • Four building owners arrested: Ram Krishna Upadhyay (43), Virendra Prasad Shukla (62), Tushar Krishna Jaiswal (31), Suresh Kumar Sahu.
  • FIR registered under BNS Sections 110, 105, 125 and UP Fire and Emergency Services Act.
  • Two-member SIT constituted: Amit Abhijat (ACS, Tourism) and Praveen Kumar (ADGP, LKO Zone)—7-day deadline.

Cooking gas cylinder/stove bursts account for 23.6% of fire deaths. Electrical short circuits account for 17.2%. Maharashtra leads with 639 fire deaths in 2024, followed by Gujarat, Delhi, and Madhya Pradesh.

The Fifteenth Finance Commission recommended Rs 5,000 crore for strengthening fire services at the state level. But implementation has been uneven, and the structural problem—buildings that violate their approvals operating in regulatory grey zones—remains unsolved.

What Happens Next

The SIT has seven days to report. The four arrested owners face charges under BNS Sections 105 and 110—culpable homicide and attempt to commit culpable homicide, which carry sentences of up to life imprisonment. The four suspended officials face departmental proceedings.

But the structural issues will outlast the investigation. The 15-metre height loophole remains. The pattern of issuing and revoking demolition orders remains. The mixed-use building problem—residential approvals masking commercial operations—remains. The single-staircase death trap remains.

Delhi is already reviewing its fire safety exemption framework after the Malviya Nagar fire killed 21 in May 2026. Telangana proposed amendments in January 2026. The question is whether these reviews will produce enforcement—or merely more reports that gather dust.

Final Analysis

The Lucknow fire was not a natural disaster. It was a man-made catastrophe enabled by a regulatory system that knows how to identify risks but does not act on them until people die. A building with a revoked demolition order. A coaching centre in a residential building. A single staircase. An AC duct that became a chimney. A height exemption that left 95% of buildings outside fire safety scrutiny.

India does not lack fire safety laws. The National Building Code of 2016 is comprehensive. The problem is implementation—the gap between what the law requires and what actually happens in the buildings where young people study, work, and live.

Fifteen students called their parents on Monday afternoon. Some said “save me.” Others said nothing at all. The system that should have protected them failed at every level—from the LDA official who revoked a demolition order in 2016, to the fire department that could not inspect a building below 15 metres, to the building owners who packed dozens of students into a structure with no emergency exit.

The SIT will submit its report in seven days. The question is not what it will find—the facts are already known. The question is whether anything will change.
