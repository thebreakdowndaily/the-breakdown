# India-US After G7: What Actually Changed

## Cold Open

Prime Minister Narendra Modi and President Donald Trump met on June 16 in Evian-les-Bains for their first face-to-face in 16 months. The handshake was warm, the conversation short, and the embrace that defined their earlier relationship was conspicuously absent. Behind the optics, five substantive issues remain unresolved — and the real deadline has shifted to July 24, when the universal 10 per cent tariff on Indian goods expires. On June 22, USTR Jamieson Greer lands in Delhi for final negotiations.

## Five Issues on the Table

**The Trade Deal.** US Ambassador Sergio Gor says only one per cent of the interim agreement remains unresolved — but that one per cent covers market access for American dairy, medical devices, and agricultural products, India's most protected sectors.

**The Dead Sailors.** On June 10, US forces struck three tankers in the Gulf of Oman. Three Indian sailors died. India summoned the US charge d'affaires — a rare and serious diplomatic démarche that has injected a human dimension into trade negotiations.

**Tariffs.** The US has already imposed a 25 per cent tariff on Indian goods plus a penalty linked to India's Russian oil purchases. A new Section 301 investigation could add another 12.5 per cent, targeting India's digital services and pharmaceutical sectors.

**H-1B Visas.** Indian tech workers face tighter restrictions — no specifics announced, but the signal aligns with Trump's broader immigration posture.

**The US-Iran Deal.** The Strait of Hormuz reopening and falling oil prices weaken India's argument for buying Russian crude and give Trump additional leverage in trade talks.

## What Actually Changed

The relationship has shifted from personal chemistry to hard bargaining — which may be more honest, if less comfortable. India is building strategic options: Modi signed a deal with the EU, deepened ties with France, and thawed relations with China. The US-Iran deal recalibrated energy math: lower costs benefit India, but Trump gains negotiating leverage. The real work happens in Delhi at the Greer-Goyal meeting on June 22-24, not in the G7 photo op. July 24 is the hard deadline — when the universal 10 per cent tariff expires and USTR Greer must have terms to present.

## What Both Sides Are Missing

India's narrative: "America is being unfair." America's narrative: "India is dragging its feet." Both are correct and incomplete. India is the world's fastest-growing major economy and has real leverage through market size and strategic autonomy. The US has real leverage through tariff power, technology access, and financial system dominance. The question is whether both sides can use their leverage without fracturing a relationship that has taken two decades to build.

## What to Watch

- June 22-24: USTR Greer in Delhi for final negotiations
- July 7: Section 301 hearings on forced labour tariffs
- July 24: Universal 10 per cent tariff expires — the hard deadline
- Mid-July: Target for signing the interim framework
