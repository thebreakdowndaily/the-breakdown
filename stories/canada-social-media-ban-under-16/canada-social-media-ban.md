## Cold Open

Canada has introduced Bill C-34, a landmark piece of legislation that bans social media for children under 16 and imposes new regulatory requirements on AI chatbot operators. Tabled in Parliament on June 10, 2026, the bill positions Canada alongside Australia, Greece, and a growing coalition of nations seeking to restrict minors' access to digital platforms. The legislation marks one of the most comprehensive attempts by a Western democracy to address mounting concerns over the impact of social media and artificial intelligence on child safety and mental health.

## The Bill's Key Provisions

At the heart of Bill C-34 is a complete prohibition on social media access for children under 16, requiring platforms to implement age verification mechanisms. The bill creates a new Digital Safety Commission with significant enforcement powers, including the authority to levy fines, issue compliance orders, and compel platforms to modify their operations. It mandates that platforms remove harmful content within 24 hours of being notified, substantially tightening existing moderation timelines. On the AI front, the legislation imposes a Duty to Act Responsibly on chatbot operators, requiring them to ensure their systems do not expose minors to harmful, manipulative, or age-inappropriate interactions. This includes obligations around transparency, safety-by-design, and regular auditing of AI systems.

## Global Context

Canada's move is part of a broader international shift. Australia passed similar legislation in late 2024 banning social media for children under 16, while Greece introduced comparable restrictions earlier this year. The European Union's Digital Services Act already requires platforms to assess and mitigate risks to minors, and the United Kingdom's Online Safety Act imposes a duty of care on platforms towards children. In India, the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology has signalled that it is examining similar regulatory frameworks. What distinguishes Canada's approach is the explicit inclusion of AI chatbots within the regulatory scope, reflecting growing global anxiety about generative AI's impact on younger users.

## Enforcement and Oversight

The Digital Safety Commission will operate as an independent regulatory body with the power to investigate complaints, conduct audits, and impose administrative monetary penalties for non-compliance. Platforms found in breach of the 24-hour takedown requirement could face fines of up to C$10 million or 3 per cent of global revenue, whichever is higher. The Commission is also empowered to require platforms to publish transparency reports detailing their content moderation practices and the effectiveness of their age verification systems. Critics, however, have raised concerns about the potential for over-blocking of legitimate content and the privacy implications of mandatory age verification.

## What's Next

Bill C-34 now moves to committee stage, where expert witnesses and stakeholder groups will provide testimony. Technology companies are expected to lobby vigorously against provisions they consider overly burdensome, particularly around AI chatbot regulation and the accelerated takedown timelines. Civil liberties groups have also expressed reservations about age verification mechanisms, arguing they could create new vectors for surveillance and data collection. If passed, the legislation would come into force in phases over 18 months, giving platforms time to build compliance infrastructure. The bill's progress will be closely watched by other nations considering similar measures, as Canada's approach could serve as a template for comprehensive digital child safety regulation.
