## Cold Open

Canada tabled Bill C-34 on June 10, 2026, making it the second Western democracy after Australia to ban social media for children under 16 — but the legislation goes further than Canberra's by explicitly regulating AI chatbots. The bill creates a Digital Safety Commission with the power to fine non-compliant platforms up to C$10 million or 3 per cent of global revenue, imposes a 24-hour takedown mandate for harmful content, and requires age verification across all social platforms. The legislation positions Canada as a test case for comprehensive digital child safety regulation in the Anglosphere.

## The Bill's Key Provisions

The prohibition on under-16 access is categorical: platforms must implement age-verification mechanisms or face penalties. The 24-hour harmful-content takedown requirement tightens existing moderation timelines substantially — the EU's Digital Services Act allows for "prompt" action without a fixed clock. The AI provisions impose a "Duty to Act Responsibly" on chatbot operators, requiring safety-by-design, transparency, and regular auditing to prevent exposure of minors to manipulative or age-inappropriate interactions. This marks one of the first legislative attempts to regulate generative AI's interaction with children.

## Enforcement and Oversight

The Digital Safety Commission will operate as an independent regulator with powers to investigate, audit, and levy administrative penalties. Critics from two directions have emerged: technology companies argue the compliance burden — especially age-verification infrastructure — is disproportionate, while civil liberties groups warn mandatory age verification creates new vectors for surveillance and data collection. The privacy implications are non-trivial: to prove a user is under 16, platforms must collect age-identifying data, creating a honeypot that regulators in other jurisdictions have struggled to secure.

## Global Context

Canada joins a rapidly hardening international landscape. Australia passed an under-16 ban in late 2024. Greece introduced similar restrictions in early 2026. The UK's Online Safety Act imposes a duty of care toward children, and the EU's DSA requires risk assessments for minors. India's MeitY has signalled it is examining comparable frameworks. Canada's distinction is the inclusion of AI chatbots within the regulatory perimeter — a reflection of growing anxiety about generative AI's capacity to build emotional dependence in younger users. The convergence of these national efforts suggests a global norm is crystallising faster than many platforms anticipated.

## What's Next

Bill C-34 now enters committee stage, where technology companies are expected to lobby aggressively against the chatbot provisions and the accelerated takedown timeline. The bill would come into force in phases over 18 months if passed, giving platforms time to build compliance infrastructure. The legislation's trajectory will be closely watched by India, Brazil, and other large markets developing their own digital child safety frameworks. Whether Canada's template proves workable or overreaching will shape regulatory debates far beyond Ottawa.
