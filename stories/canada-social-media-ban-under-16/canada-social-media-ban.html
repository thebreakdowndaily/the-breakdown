The Lede

On June 10, 2026, the Canadian government introduced Bill C-34, the Safe Social Media Act — the most comprehensive online safety legislation in the country's history. The bill would ban social media accounts for children under 16, create a new federal regulator with enforcement powers, and impose legally binding safety duties on AI chatbot operators.

The legislation arrives six months after Australia became the world's first country to enact a social media ban for under-16s, which resulted in nearly 5 million teen accounts being deactivated within a month. It also follows weeks after families affected by Canada's worst mass shooting sued OpenAI, alleging the company knew the alleged killer was planning the attack on ChatGPT but did not warn police.

For over 20 years, the Canadian Centre for Child Protection has documented a steep and accelerating rise in online harms against children. This Bill is a finite and critical period — one that demands protection, not exploitation.

Canadian Heritage statement on Bill C-34

What the Bill Does

1. Age Restriction: Social media platforms must prevent children under 16 from having accounts. Platforms can seek exemption if they demonstrate sufficient child safety safeguards. Adult content platforms cannot obtain exemption.

2. AI Chatbot Regulation: For the first time in Canada, AI chatbot operators face a statutory Duty to Act Responsibly — requiring them to mitigate harmful content, implement crisis intervention protocols (for self-harm, suicide, or violence), and prevent harmful chatbot behaviours.

3. New Regulator: The Digital Safety Commission of Canada will be an independent body enforcing compliance, auditing platform safety measures, and supporting victims of online harms.

4. Content Takedown: Platforms must remove content that sexually victimises a child or non-consensual intimate images within 24 hours. The bill covers seven categories of harmful content including content inciting violence, fomenting hatred, and inducing self-harm.

5. Transparency: Platforms must submit publicly disclosed digital safety plans, label AI-generated content, and provide blocking/flagging tools.

Global Context: The Tipping Point

Canada joins a rapidly accelerating global movement. Australia led in December 2025. Greece announced an under-15 ban from January 2027. France, Denmark, and Poland are actively considering similar legislation. The UK's Online Safety Act already mandates age verification, with full implementation expected by 2027.

The momentum reflects a fundamental shift in how governments approach child online safety — moving from voluntary industry self-regulation toward legally enforced age restrictions with independent oversight. Australia's experience is instructive: within a month of its law taking effect, platforms collectively deactivated nearly 5 million teen accounts, demonstrating that enforcement at scale is technically feasible when regulation demands it.

What Both Sides Are Missing

The Case for Regulation

Proponents argue the status quo is unsustainable. The Canadian Centre for Child Protection documented a steep and accelerating rise in online child sexual abuse and exploitation over 20 years. AI chatbots present new risks: they can interact persistently with children, reinforce harmful behaviours, and respond unsafely in crisis situations involving self-harm or violence. The bill's supporters point to Australia's successful deactivation of 5 million teen accounts as proof that age restrictions can work at scale.

The Case Against

Critics raise three core objections. Effectiveness: Age verification technologies remain imperfect — VPNs, shared devices, and parental workarounds can bypass restrictions. Free expression: Advocacy groups warn that blanket age bans could limit access to educational content, LGBTQ+ support networks, and mental health resources. Implementation timeline: The bill could take 18 months to set up the regulator, meaning the age ban might take effect before any exemption mechanism exists for responsible platforms.

What the Data Shows

The global shift is measurable. Australia: 5 million teen accounts deactivated in 30 days. Greece: under-15 ban from 2027. Canada: under-16 with possible exemptions. France, Denmark, Poland: actively debating. The question is no longer whether to regulate — it's how to regulate without creating unintended harms worse than the ones being addressed.

Key Insight

The core tension is unresolved: how do you protect children from online harms without creating a surveillance infrastructure, restricting legitimate access, or giving platforms a roadmap to circumvent the rules?

What to Watch Next

1. Summer recess: Parliament is due to break soon. Carney's slim majority means the bill's path to passage depends on cross-party support. Government officials estimate a year for the bill to pass.

2. Platform responses: Meta, Google, TikTok, and Snap will lobby for exemptions. The exemption mechanism — what constitutes "sufficient safeguards" — will be the central battleground.

3. AI chatbot scope: The bill exempts chatbots with "functional" purposes but covers those that "mimic human-like relationships." How the Digital Safety Commission draws this line will shape Canada's AI industry.

4. Global domino effect: If Canada passes, pressure will mount on the US (which has no federal age ban) and on EU member states still debating implementation of the Digital Services Act.

The Bottom Line

Canada's Bill C-34 represents the most ambitious attempt yet to regulate both social media and AI chatbots under a single statutory framework. Its success or failure will be watched closely by every other democracy considering similar moves. The core tension is unresolved: how do you protect children from online harms without creating a surveillance infrastructure, restricting legitimate access, or giving platforms a roadmap to circumvent the rules?

The bill's 18-month timeline to establish the regulator means the real test is still ahead. But the direction of travel is unmistakable: the era of self-regulation for social media and AI is ending.
