The US-Iran Peace Deal: Full 14-Point Agreement Explained

The Breakdown · June 18, 2026 · Updated June 25 · 12 min read

On June 17, President Donald Trump sat down at the Palace of Versailles with French President Emmanuel Macron and signed a two-page memorandum of understanding with Iran. The document ended the most expensive four months of American warfare since the Iraq invasion — and started a 60-day countdown to negotiate a permanent peace.

The deal does not end the war. It pauses it, buys time, and shifts the battlefield from the Strait of Hormuz to a negotiating table. Iran gets sanctions relief and a $300 billion reconstruction fund. The US gets a reopened waterway, a promise Iran will not seek nuclear weapons, and a 60-day window to prove diplomacy can succeed where bombing did not.

Here is exactly what is in the 14-point agreement — and what happens next.

The 14 Points, Decoded

Points 1–2: Ceasefire and Sovereignty

The agreement declares an "immediate and permanent termination of military operations on all fronts, including in Lebanon." Both sides pledge to respect each other's sovereignty and territorial integrity and refrain from interference in internal affairs. The Lebanon clause is significant: it rejects Israel's concerns about Hezbollah. Israel has signaled it does not feel bound by any Lebanon-related provisions.

Points 3–5: The 60-Day Clock and the Strait

The US and Iran commit to negotiating a final deal within 60 days, extendable by mutual consent. During this window, Iran will allow safe passage of commercial vessels through the Strait of Hormuz — free of charge. The US will dismantle its naval blockade of Iranian ports within 30 days, with vessel traffic proportional to Strait reopening progress.

Points 6–7: The $300 Billion Fund and Sanctions Relief

The US undertakes to "develop a definitive, mutually agreed plan with at least $300 billion for the reconstruction and economic development of Iran." The US is not required to contribute directly — the fund is expected to come from international partners and private capital. More consequentially, the US agrees to terminate all sanctions — including UN Security Council resolutions, IAEA Board resolutions, and all unilateral US sanctions — on an agreed schedule.

“The $300 billion figure is not aid. It is access. The question is whether private capital will flow into a country that has been a pariah for decades.”

Vali Nasr, Johns Hopkins SAIS

Points 8–9: The Nuclear Question

Iran reaffirms it shall not procure or develop nuclear weapons. The two sides agree to resolve the disposition of enriched uranium stockpiles through a "minimum methodology" of on-site down-blending under IAEA supervision. Pending a final deal, both sides maintain the status quo: Iran pauses its nuclear program, the US imposes no new sanctions and deploys no additional forces.

Points 10–14: Implementation Mechanics

The US Treasury immediately issues waivers for Iranian oil exports. The US makes available frozen Iranian funds. Both sides agree that the final deal will be endorsed by a binding UN Security Council resolution. The MOU takes effect immediately upon signing.

The Signing: Versailles, June 17

Trump signed the deal during a state dinner at the Palace of Versailles — the same site where the Treaty of Versailles ended World War I in 1918. He was flanked by French President Macron, who hosted the G7 summit. Iran's President Pezeshkian signed simultaneously in Tehran. Pakistan Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif, who mediated the talks, confirmed the agreement took "immediate effect." Vice President JD Vance was expected to attend a ceremonial signing in Switzerland but postponed it, announcing he would lead the US negotiating team instead.

What Was Won — and What Was Given Up

The deal is asymmetric by design. Iran gets its most important concessions upfront: oil sanctions waived immediately, the naval blockade lifted, a credible path to full sanctions termination, and $300 billion in reconstruction financing. The US, by contrast, gets promises that must be verified later: that Iran will not seek nuclear weapons, that the Strait will stay open, and that a final deal can be reached within 60 days.

Trump acknowledged this imbalance. "It does not achieve some of our initial war goals," he said at Versailles, specifically citing Iran's ballistic missile program. He warned he would "bomb the hell" out of Iran if no final deal emerged. The threat underscores the fragility of the agreement: it is a ceasefire backed by a bombing threat, not a peace built on trust.

Sanctions relief is phased — oil waivers are immediate, full termination depends on final deal negotiations

Khamenei's Endorsement — and the Red Lines

On June 18, Iranian Supreme Leader Ayatollah Mojtaba Khamenei endorsed direct negotiations with the US for the first time. State media read a statement signaling his approval — a critical political cover for President Pezeshkian, whose government had been negotiating under the threat of hardliner opposition.

But Iran's parliamentary speaker warned the country "will not return to pre-war conditions," suggesting Iran may charge tolls for the Strait of Hormuz after the 60-day free passage window expires. Before the war, there were no tolls. The MOU leaves this future arrangement open to negotiation with Oman and Gulf states.

The First Sticking Points

Within days of the signing, disagreements emerged. VP Vance claimed Iran had agreed to invite IAEA inspectors back. Iran's Foreign Ministry denied this — spokesman Baghaei said Tehran had "no clear schedule" for inspections. Iran also disputed that $12 billion in frozen assets had been released, though US officials suggested the figure was being negotiated.

Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi confirmed via X that sanctions on oil exports and petrochemical sales had been waived, the blockade lifted, and some frozen assets released. But the big questions — the enrichment schedule, the inspection regime, the sanctions termination timeline — remain unresolved.

"We have all the cards."

Vice President JD Vance, on US negotiating position

The EU Wildcard

European Union foreign policy chief Kaja Kallas announced the EU would leave its own sanctions on Iran in place for now. The 27-nation bloc imposed separate sanctions over Iran's nuclear program, human rights violations, and the Hormuz closure. "Once the conditions allow, member states will discuss whether the lifting of sanctions is in place, but we are not there yet," Kallas said. This means Iran faces a two-track sanctions regime: US relief progressing, EU relief stalled.

What Happens Next

The 60-day clock is running. Two working groups will negotiate: one on sanctions removal, one on nuclear issues. The big unknowns:

Enriched uranium. Iran's stockpile is believed to be buried under rubble from US strikes. The MOU calls for on-site down-blending under IAEA supervision — a compromise from the original US demand that the material be removed from the country entirely.

Sanctions schedule. The US has pledged to terminate all sanctions but left the timeline for a final deal. Oil waivers give Iran immediate relief; everything else is conditional.

Regional security. The Lebanon clause binds the US but not Israel. Israeli strikes on Hezbollah could unravel the ceasefire.

The $300 billion. The fund is not US-paid. It requires private capital and international partners to materialize. Whether investors trust a post-war Iran enough to commit billions is an open question.

The Bottom Line

THE VERDICT

The Versailles Accord is not a peace deal. It is an agreement to try to reach one. Trump gave Iran its most important concessions upfront — sanctions waivers, blockade lift, a $300B development fund — in exchange for a 60-day window to negotiate a permanent arrangement. The war paused, not ended. The Strait reopened, not guaranteed. The nuclear question deferred, not resolved. Whether this becomes Trump's Camp David or his Munich depends entirely on what happens in the next 60 days.
