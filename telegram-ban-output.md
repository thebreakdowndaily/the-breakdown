## A. NEWS SUMMARY

**Headline:** India Blocks Telegram for 354 Million Users Until June 22 — Pavel Durov Calls It "Punishing the Wrong People"

**100-word summary:** India's government ordered a nationwide block on Telegram until June 22 under Section 69A of the IT Act, acting on NTA recommendations ahead of the June 21 NEET-UG retest. The block targets cheating rackets using Telegram to sell fake exam papers and spread misinformation. A separate order forces Telegram to disable its message-editing feature in India until June 30 — the NTA alleges fraudsters used it to fabricate paper leak "evidence" by editing old messages post-exam. Telegram CEO Pavel Durov slammed the move, saying it punishes 150M+ ordinary Indians, not the actual leakers. Digital rights groups IFF and SFLC.in called it disproportionate, unconstitutional, and a band-aid solution that fails to address systemic exam security failures.

**Key insight:** The government is treating the symptom (Telegram as distribution channel) rather than the disease (insider leaks in the exam printing and logistics chain). The ban is politically necessary to show action, but legally and practically questionable.

**India banned Telegram for 354 million users. Here's what actually happened and why it matters.**

On Monday, the Indian government blocked Telegram nationwide.

The reason? Cheating networks using the platform to sell fake NEET exam papers ahead of the June 21 retest.

Here are the details most coverage is missing:

**1. The ban has two parts no one is talking about.**
Part A: Telegram is blocked entirely until June 22.
Part B: Telegram must DISABLE message editing in India until June 30.

Part B is actually the more interesting story. The NTA says fraudsters used message editing to fabricate paper leak "evidence" — editing old posts AFTER exams to make it look like papers were leaked before.

**2. The legal basis is Section 69A of the IT Act.**
Designed to block specific "information" (terrorism content, hate speech). Not designed to block entire platforms. The Internet Freedom Foundation argues this is a legal stretch. The Supreme Court's Shreya Singhal judgment (2015) upheld 69A only because it was "narrowly tailored."

**3. Telegram CEO Pavel Durov fired back.**
"This punishes 150M+ ordinary Telegram users in India — not the insiders who leaked the exam materials. And the ban hasn't stopped anything. The leaks just moved to other apps."

He's not wrong.

**4. The real problem isn't Telegram.**
NEET's original May 3 exam was cancelled because of insider leaks — from the printing and logistics chain. Telegram was used to DISTRIBUTE what was already leaked. Blocking Telegram doesn't secure the supply chain. It just blocks the distribution channel.

**5. Students are caught in the middle.**
Millions of NEET aspirants depend on Telegram for study groups, doubt-clearing, and shared resources. The ban comes in their FINAL WEEK of preparation.

**The uncomfortable question:**
If the government can block an entire platform used by 354 million people for exam fraud, what precedent does that set?

Cheating is wrong. Exam fraud destroys trust. But collective punishment of 354 million users isn't the answer — it's an admission that the system can't secure itself.
