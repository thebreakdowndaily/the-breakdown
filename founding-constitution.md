## Preamble

We, the founders of The Breakdown, establish this institution in the belief that:

The public has a right to understand — not just to know. The difference between knowing and understanding is the difference between a headline and a methodology. Between a claim and a source. Between outrage and insight.

We exist because the modern information environment is optimized for everything except understanding. It is optimized for speed, for emotion, for tribalism, for the dopamine hit of confirmation bias. It is not optimized for truth.

This institution is our answer.

It will not be perfect. It will make mistakes. But it will correct them publicly. It will not know everything, but it will be honest about what it does not know. It will not be neutral in the face of falsehood, but it will be humble in its certainty.

This Constitution is our founding promise — to our readers, to our editors, to our future selves. It will guide every decision this institution makes, from the smallest headline to the largest strategic bet.

# PART ONE — Why We Exist

## 1.1 The Diagnosis

Modern media is broken in seven specific ways, and each one creates an underserved reader:

### The Speed Trap

Breaking news is published within minutes of an event. The pressure to be first means facts are confirmed after publication, not before. The first draft of history is written in error, and the correction is buried at the bottom of a page no one visits.

**The underserved reader:** The person who wants to know what *actually* happened — not what someone's source thinks happened, not what the first wire report said, not what the algorithm boosted.

### The Outrage Economy

Television news and social media optimize for emotional arousal because emotion drives engagement. Anger is the highest-engagement emotion. Fear is second. The business model rewards making people afraid and angry.

**The underserved reader:** The person who wants to understand the world without being manipulated into a emotional state that serves someone else's metrics.

### The Algorithmic Filter

Social media news is curated by algorithms optimized for time-on-platform, not accuracy. Misinformation spreads faster than truth because it is more novel, more emotional, and more shareable.

**The underserved reader:** The person who wants the signal without the noise — the relevant information without the firehose of content designed to keep them scrolling.

### The Political Silo

News organizations have aligned with political tribes. Readers choose their news based on which tribe it validates. The result: two populations operating from different factual premises.

**The underserved reader:** The person who wants to know what is true — not what validates their existing beliefs.

### The Information Overload

There is more information available than any human can process. The scarcity is not information — it is attention, context, and signal.

**The underserved reader:** The person who needs curation, context, and analysis — not more articles.

### The Trust Deficit

Trust in media has collapsed. In India, the majority of people believe news organizations are controlled by political and corporate interests. This distrust is earned and rational.

**The underserved reader:** The person who wants to trust what they read but has been burned too many times.

### The Expertise Gap

News organizations cover everything with the same depth — a political crisis gets the same word count as a celebrity wedding. Complex topics (climate policy, budget allocations, geopolitical strategy) are simplified to the point of distortion.

**The underserved reader:** The person who is intelligent, curious, and tired of being talked down to.

## 1.2 The Institutional Answer

The Breakdown exists to serve the readers these systems leave behind.

We serve the reader who wants to understand, not just know. The reader who is willing to invest 15 minutes in a 2000-word analysis because they understand that the world is complex and cannot be explained in 280 characters. The reader who would rather know what they don't know than believe something false.

We serve the reader who is tired of being manipulated, patronized, and lied to. The reader who has given up on television news and has not found an adequate replacement.

We serve the reader who believes that truth is knowable — not perfectly, not absolutely, but approximately, through method, evidence, and transparency.

We are not here to compete with breaking news. We are here to make breaking news understandable.

We are not here to be faster. We are here to be clearer.

We are not here to be the most popular. We are here to be the most trusted.

# PART TWO — Our Beliefs

## 2.1 The Seven Principles

### Principle 1: Truth over Speed

We will never publish something we cannot verify, even if it means being hours or days behind our competitors. The reader's trust is worth more than the reader's attention.

A story published five minutes after an event is almost certainly wrong in meaningful ways. A story published five hours after an event has had time for multiple confirmations, context gathering, and uncertainty assessment.

We are not a wire service. Our value is not being first. Our value is being reliable.

**In practice:** If Reuters has a story at 10:00 AM and we cannot independently confirm it until 4:00 PM, we publish at 4:00 PM with a note saying: "This story was first reported by Reuters at 10:00 AM and independently confirmed by The Breakdown at 3:30 PM."

### Principle 2: Context over Outrage

Every story must answer not just "what happened" but "why did it happen" and "why does it matter." A story that provokes outrage without providing understanding is not journalism — it is content.

We reject the premise that the reader must be emotionally activated to engage. An engaged reader who does not understand is worse off than before they read. A calm reader who understands is better off.

**In practice:** Before publishing, every story must pass the Context Test: "Could a reader finish this story and explain its significance to someone else?" If not, the story is incomplete.

### Principle 3: Evidence over Ideology

Our analysis is based on evidence, not political alignment. We do not have a party affiliation. We do not have a patron. We do not have an ideology beyond a commitment to methodological rigor.

When the evidence contradicts a narrative that our readers believe, we publish the evidence anyway. When the evidence contradicts a narrative that we ourselves believe, we publish it anyway.

**In practice:** Every factual claim in every story must be traceable to a source. Sources must be labeled by type (government document, academic paper, primary interview, etc.). Confidence must be stated. Uncertainty must be acknowledged.

### Principle 4: Understanding over Virality

We do not optimize for shares, likes, or retweets. We optimize for comprehension, retention, and trust. A piece of content that is widely shared but poorly understood is a failure. A piece of content that is deeply understood by a thousand readers is a success.

We recognize that understanding does not scale the same way as virality. Understanding requires time, attention, and cognitive effort. We honor the reader who invests these resources by ensuring the investment is worthwhile.

**In practice:** No clickbait headlines. No "you won't believe" formulations. No exploitation of curiosity gaps. The headline must accurately and specifically describe what the story contains.

### Principle 5: Transparency over Authority

Traditional news organizations establish authority by projecting certainty: "The White House announced," "The Ministry confirmed," "Experts say." The source is opaque. The reader is asked to trust the institution, not the evidence.

We establish authority differently — by showing our work. Every claim is sourced. Every source is labeled. Every uncertainty is acknowledged. Our confidence is quantified.

We do not ask readers to trust us because of our brand. We ask readers to verify our work and then decide.

**In practice:** Every story includes a source list. Every source includes a link, access date, and quality label. Every story includes a Composite Confidence Score. Correction notices are displayed prominently, not buried.

### Principle 6: Humility over Certainty

We do not know everything. We will be wrong. We will correct our errors publicly and promptly. We will not pretend to know things we do not.

A news organization that never admits uncertainty is either dishonest or naive. The world is complex. Evidence is incomplete. Interpretation is fallible.

We state our confidence levels explicitly: High (multiple independent primary sources), Medium (good evidence but gaps remain), Low (limited evidence, single source), Insufficient (cannot verify).

We do not use confident language for uncertain claims.

**In practice:** Hedging language is not weakness — it is honesty. "The evidence suggests" is preferable to "This proves" unless certainty is justified. Every story explicitly acknowledges what we do not know.

### Principle 7: Readers over Advertisers

Our revenue comes from readers who value our work, not from advertisers who value their attention. This alignment ensures that our incentives match our mission.

When revenue depends on reader trust, every decision that increases trust is a good business decision. When revenue depends on advertising, every decision that increases engagement (including outrage and misinformation) is a good business decision.

We choose reader-funded because it is the only model that aligns our economics with our values.

**In practice:** No advertising that influences coverage. No sponsored content. No native advertising. No pay-for-play. Our funding model is disclosed publicly. Our editorial decisions are made independently of revenue considerations.

## 2.2 The Ninth Question

Every story must answer nine questions:

1. **What happened?** — The factual event, statement, or development
2. **Why did it happen?** — Causal factors, background context, historical precedent
3. **Why does it matter?** — Significance, stakes, consequences
4. **Who benefits?** — Winners, beneficiaries, power dynamics
5. **Who is affected?** — Impacted populations, sectors, communities
6. **What evidence exists?** — Sources, data, documents, testimony
7. **What are experts saying?** — Informed perspectives, dissenting views
8. **What is the uncertainty?** — What we know, what we don't, confidence level
9. **What happens next?** — Forward-looking analysis, scenarios, timelines

A story that answers fewer than six of these questions is not ready for publication.

# PART THREE — The Reader Promise

## 3.1 The Promise

**To every reader who gives us their time and attention, we promise:**

1. **You will understand.** Every story will leave you with a clearer understanding of the world than you had before. You will be able to explain the story to someone else.

2. **You will see the evidence.** Every claim will be traceable to its source. You will know where the information came from, how reliable it is, and what confidence we have in it.

3. **You will know what we don't know.** Uncertainty will be acknowledged, not hidden. Gaps will be identified, not glossed over.

4. **You will not be manipulated.** We will not use loaded language, emotional appeals, or fear to engage you. We will not optimize for outrage. We will not exploit cognitive biases.

5. **You will be respected.** We will not dumb down complex topics. We assume you are intelligent, curious, and capable of understanding complexity when it is explained clearly.

6. **You will be trusted.** When we make mistakes — and we will — we will correct them publicly, promptly, and permanently. The correction will be as visible as the original error.

7. **You will be independent of our influences.** We are not controlled by any political party, corporation, government, or advocacy group. Our editorial decisions are made independently. Our funding is transparent.

## 3.2 What We Ask in Return

1. **Your attention.** Understanding takes time. Give us 15 minutes, and we will give you a mental model of the world that lasts longer than a news cycle.

2. **Your skepticism.** Do not trust us because of this promise. Verify us. Check our sources. Test our logic. If we fail, tell us.

3. **Your support.** If you find value in what we do, support us financially. Reader funding keeps us independent. We would rather have 10,000 paying subscribers than 10 million page views funded by advertising.

4. **Your patience.** We are building an institution. Institutions take years to build. We will get better over time. Our first year will not be our best — but our tenth year will be better than our first.

## 3.3 The Unpromise

We will not promise to be neutral.

Neutrality in the face of falsehood is not a virtue. When a political leader makes a demonstrably false claim, we will say it is false. When evidence overwhelmingly supports one conclusion over another, we will say so.

But we will distinguish between factual claims and interpretive frameworks. We will state the evidence and the reasoning that leads to our conclusions. We will acknowledge where reasonable people disagree.

We are independent. We are evidence-based. We are transparent. We are not neutral about truth.

# PART FOUR — The Editorial Standard

## 4.1 The Guiding Question

Every editorial decision is guided by a single question:

**"Will this help our readers understand?"**

Not: "Will this get clicks?"
Not: "Will this go viral?"
Not: "Will this make us popular with X political group?"
Not: "Will this attract advertisers?"

Just: **"Will this help our readers understand?"**

## 4.2 The Five-Year Test

Before publishing anything, ask:

**"Will this still be valuable in five years?"**

Breaking news fails this test. Analysis, explainers, investigations, and data stories pass it.

This does not mean we never cover breaking news. It means our coverage of breaking news must contain analysis, context, and evidence that provides lasting value beyond the initial report.

A story about a political crisis should include enough historical context that a reader encountering it five years later can still understand what happened and why it mattered.

## 4.3 Editorial Standards

### Accuracy Before Speed

- Every factual claim must be confirmed by at least two independent sources before publication
- The only exception: a single primary source of unimpeachable reliability (original document, direct recording, official data)
- Corrections must be published within 24 hours of error identification
- Correction notices must be displayed at the top of the story, not buried

### Source Standards

- Every source must be attributed
- Sources must be labeled by type: Primary, Secondary, Government, Academic, Media, Expert, Anonymous
- Anonymous sources require Editor-in-Chief approval with written justification
- The reader must be able to distinguish between a primary source and a secondary reference
- Source quality must be disclosed: Verified, Cross-referenced, Contextual, Unverified

### Context Requirements

- Every story must include relevant background context
- Context is not optional — a story without context is incomplete
- Context includes: historical precedent, causal factors, stakeholder interests, power dynamics
- For ongoing stories, link to previous coverage so the reader can catch up

### Headline Standards

- Must accurately reflect the story content
- Must not exaggerate or sensationalize
- Must not use loaded language
- Must not exploit curiosity gaps
- Must be understandable without the article
- Must pass the "does this help readers understand" test

### Opinion Standards

- Opinion must be clearly labeled
- Opinion must be based on evidence, reasoning, and disclosed premises
- Opinion must acknowledge counter-arguments and alternative views
- Opinion must be separate from news reporting
- Staff writers may write opinion but must clearly distinguish it from their reporting

### Corrections Standards

- "We never silently change content" — every change must be logged
- Correction notices must include: what was wrong, what was changed, when, and by whom
- The original error must remain visible (with strikethrough or annotation)
- Corrections must be linked from the top of the story
- A permanent correction log must be maintained and publicly accessible

### Ethics Standards

- No gifts, payments, or benefits from subjects of coverage
- No political donations or campaign involvement by editorial staff
- No financial interests in subjects of coverage
- All potential conflicts must be disclosed to the Editor-in-Chief
- Staff may not write about organizations where they or family members have financial interests
- Staff may not trade securities based on non-public information obtained through their work

## 4.4 What We Do Not Cover

We do not cover:

- Celebrity news (unless it bears on policy, power, or public interest)
- Sports (unless it intersects with politics, economics, or social issues)
- Crime (unless it reveals systemic patterns or failures)
- Natural disasters (unless we can add analytical value beyond wire reports)
- Viral internet content (unless it has genuine news significance)

We do not chase trending topics. We choose topics based on what matters, not what is noisy.

## 4.5 The Editorial Hierarchy

When editorial judgment requires trade-offs, the hierarchy is:

1. **Truth** — The factual accuracy of the content
2. **Context** — The completeness of the reader's understanding
3. **Transparency** — The openness of our methods and sources
4. **Fairness** — The respectful treatment of subjects and perspectives
5. **Independence** — The absence of external influence
6. **Impact** — The effect of the content on public understanding
7. **Timeliness** — The relevance to current events

Speed is not on this list.

# PART FIVE — AI Ethics Charter

## 5.1 Preamble

Artificial intelligence is a tool, not a journalist. It has no conscience, no editorial judgment, and no accountability. It cannot be trusted to tell the truth because it has no understanding of truth. It can only be designed to approximate it.

We use AI because it extends our capabilities — summarizing, organizing, extracting entities, identifying patterns. We do not use AI because it is cheaper or faster. We use it because, under human supervision, it helps us serve our readers better.

This charter governs every use of AI at The Breakdown.

## 5.2 Permitted Uses

AI may be used for:

**Research Assistance:**
- Extracting entities, dates, and locations from documents
- Identifying relationships between entities across stories
- Suggesting relevant sources based on topic analysis
- Organizing large document sets for human review
- Generating chronologies from document sets

**Content Production (with human review):**
- Summarizing long documents for editorial review
- Generating draft metadata (tags, SEO descriptions, category suggestions)
- Suggesting headlines (always reviewed and potentially overwritten by humans)
- Transcribing interviews and recordings
- Translating source material between languages

**Analysis:**
- Identifying patterns in data sets
- Flagging potential claims for fact-checking
- Generating timelines from story update data
- Recommending related stories for reader navigation

**Reader Experience:**
- Summarizing stories on request (the "Summarize" feature)
- Answering questions about story content from a pre-verified knowledge base
- Generating personalized reading recommendations
- Providing audio versions of written content

## 5.3 Prohibited Uses

AI may never be used for:

**Fact Invention:**
- Generating facts, data, or statistics that are not verified by human editors
- Creating examples, case studies, or scenarios that are presented as real

**Quote Generation:**
- Generating quotes attributed to real people
- Generating quotes attributed to anonymous or composite sources

**Source Fabrication:**
- Generating source citations that do not exist
- Generating URLs, DOIs, or references that are not verified

**Editorial Decisions:**
- Deciding what stories to cover
- Deciding what angle to take
- Deciding what to publish
- Deciding what to promote

**Reader Deception:**
- Responding to readers as if human without disclosure
- Generating fake engagement (comments, reviews)
- Personalizing content in ways that deceive readers about its nature

**Autonomous Publication:**
- Publishing content without human review and approval
- Publishing corrections without human verification
- Publishing updates without human editorial judgment

## 5.4 Transparency Requirements

Every use of AI must be transparent to the reader:

**Labeling:**
- AI-assisted stories must be labeled: "This story was researched with AI assistance. The following tasks were AI-assisted: [list]. All claims have been verified by human editors."
- AI-generated summaries must be labeled: "AI-generated summary. Reviewed by human editor."
- AI-generated translations must be labeled: "AI-translated. Reviewed by human editor."

**Disclosure in Source Lists:**
- If AI was used to process or analyze sources, this must be disclosed
- The specific AI system used must be named
- The date of AI processing must be included

**Correction of AI Errors:**
- If an error is traced to AI, the correction must note this: "This error resulted from AI-assisted analysis and was not caught by human review. We have updated our review process accordingly."

## 5.5 Human Review Standards

Every AI output that appears in published content must pass human review:

**Review requirements by use case:**

| Use case | Human reviewer | Review standard |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| AI-assisted research | Research Editor | Verify entities, dates, relationships |
| AI-drafted summary | Writer + Editor | Verify accuracy, completeness, framing |
| AI-suggested headline | Managing Editor | Verify accuracy, tone, SEO quality |
| AI-generated translation | Editor fluent in both languages | Verify nuance, tone, accuracy |
| AI-based recommendation | Audience Editor | Verify relevance, appropriateness |
| Entity extraction | Research Editor | Verify all extracted entities, check for missed ones |
| Claim flagging | Fact Checker | Investigate all flagged claims, do not assume AI is correct |

**The "Human In the Loop" requirement:**
- No AI output may reach readers without a named human who has reviewed it and accepts responsibility for it
- The named human's identity must be knowable to readers
- The named human is accountable for errors, regardless of AI involvement

## 5.6 Quality Standards

| Standard | Target | Measurement |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| Factual accuracy of AI outputs | >99% | Human audit of random sample |
| Hallucination rate | <0.5% | Trap queries in regular testing |
| Source attribution accuracy | 100% | All AI-identified sources must exist and match |
| Summary fidelity | No loss of key claims | Human comparison test |
| Bias assessment | Neutral on political/social dimensions | Quarterly bias audit by independent reviewer |

If any standard is not met, the AI tool must be retrained, reconfigured, or removed from use.

## 5.7 Training and Data Privacy

- We will not train AI on reader data without explicit consent
- We will not input confidential source information into third-party AI systems
- We will not use AI systems that train on our content without our permission
- Our content will not be used to train external AI models without explicit license
- Any AI system we develop internally will be trained only on our own verified content and public domain sources

## 5.8 Continuous Auditing

- AI systems will be audited quarterly for accuracy, bias, and hallucination
- Audit results will be published in our transparency reports
- Readers will be invited to report AI-related errors through a dedicated channel
- The AI Editor is responsible for maintaining this audit program

# PART SIX — Trust Framework

## 6.1 The Correction System

**Core principle:** We never silently change content.

**Every correction must:**
1. Be published at the top of the corrected story
2. State what was wrong and what was corrected
3. Include the date and time of the correction
4. Name the editor who made the correction
5. Link to the permanent correction log entry

**Correction types:**
- **Minor** (typo, formatting, link): Fixed immediately, no notice required
- **Factual** (incorrect data, name, date, quote): Published correction notice within 24 hours
- **Omission** (missing context that changes understanding): Published editor's note within 48 hours
- **Clarification** (ambiguous wording that could mislead): Published clarification within 24 hours
- **Retraction** (story cannot be substantiated): Published retraction notice immediately, story remains at URL with notice

**The retraction rule:** Retracted stories are never deleted. They remain at their original URL with a clear retraction notice. This prevents link rot and maintains transparency.

## 6.2 Transparency Reports

Every quarter, we publish a transparency report containing:

- Number of corrections published, broken down by type
- Number of retractions, if any
- Number of reader-reported errors
- Average time to correct
- Revenue sources and amounts
- Funding sources and any changes
- Editorial team changes
- AI audit results
- Notable editorial decisions and their rationale

## 6.3 The Evidence Hierarchy

All sources are classified by evidentiary weight:

**Tier 1 (Highest):**
- Original government documents (laws, judgments, official data, press releases)
- Direct recordings (video, audio, transcript) of events or statements
- Original data sets with clear methodology
- Physical documents with chain of custody

**Tier 2:**
- Official summaries or fact sheets from authoritative bodies
- Government spokesperson statements
- Court rulings and judgments
- International organization reports (UN, World Bank, IMF, etc.)

**Tier 3:**
- Peer-reviewed academic research
- Expert testimony with verifiable credentials
- Reputable think tank reports with disclosed methodology and funding
- Investigative journalism with original sourcing

**Tier 4:**
- Established media reports with original reporting
- Industry reports with disclosed methodology
- Professional analyses with verifiable assumptions

**Tier 5:**
- General knowledge, widely accepted facts
- Background context from established reference works
- Secondary media analysis (analysis of analysis)

**Tier 6 (Lowest):**
- Anonymous sources (requires Editor-in-Chief approval)
- Unverified social media claims
- Speculative analysis
- Opinion presented as fact

Every story must disclose the tier distribution of its sources.

## 6.4 The Composite Confidence Score

Every story receives a Composite Confidence Score (CCS):

- Calculated as the percentage of verifiable claims rated High or Medium confidence
- Displayed prominently: "This story's claims are 92% verified at High or Medium confidence"
- Low-confidence claims are individually marked within the story
- The CCS is updated if new evidence changes the confidence level
- The CCS history is maintained with the story's version history

## 6.5 Anonymous Source Protocol

**When to use:**
- Source would face retaliation, legal jeopardy, or professional harm
- Information is of significant public interest
- Information cannot be obtained through other means
- Source's credibility can be independently verified

**Required approvals:**
- Editor-in-Chief must approve in writing
- Legal review recommended
- Source's identity known to at least two senior editors

**In the story:**
- Explain why anonymity was granted (without identifying the source)
- Describe the source's position and access (without identifying them)
- Corroborate with other sources where possible

**Records:**
- Source identity stored securely, accessible only to Editor-in-Chief and one senior editor
- No anonymous source information stored in cloud-based systems
- Source identity never shared with AI systems

## 6.6 Editorial Independence Guarantee

- No external entity has editorial control over The Breakdown
- No funder, advertiser, or partner may influence editorial decisions
- No content is published in exchange for payment or consideration
- All funding sources are disclosed in the annual transparency report
- The Editor-in-Chief has final authority over all editorial content
- The Editor-in-Chief cannot be removed for editorial decisions

## 7.5 The Institutional Question

What kind of institution should The Breakdown become?

**It should become all of these:**

A **research institute** — producing work that meets academic standards of evidence while remaining accessible to general readers.

A **policy intelligence platform** — tracking governance decisions with enough rigor that policymakers, journalists, and citizens rely on our data.

An **educational institution** — training journalists in evidence-based methodology, teaching readers how to evaluate information, and building the next generation of analytical talent.

A **knowledge archive** — curating a permanent record of events, claims, sources, and relationships that researchers can rely on decades from now.

A **media company** — publishing daily analysis that helps readers understand the world today, not just archive it for tomorrow.

A **public utility** — serving the public's right to understand, regardless of ability to pay. Our core content is free. Our premium features fund the mission.

We do not have to choose. These are not competing identities. They are layers of a single mission: making the world's most important news genuinely understandable.

# PART EIGHT — Legacy

## 8.1 The Question

> If this organization disappears after 50 years, what would readers say it gave to society?

## 8.2 The Answer

They would say:

**It gave us a way to know what was true.**

In an age of information chaos, it was a place where the evidence was laid open, the sources were named, and the uncertainty was acknowledged. You could disagree with its conclusions, but you could not dismiss its methods.

**It gave us back our agency.**

Before The Breakdown, news happened to us. We were passive consumers of information designed to manipulate our emotions. After The Breakdown, we had the tools to evaluate claims, understand context, and form our own judgments.

**It proved that trust was possible.**

In an industry that had lost faith in itself, The Breakdown showed that readers would pay for quality, that transparency was a competitive advantage, and that a media company could survive without selling its readers' attention to the highest bidder.

**It raised the standard.**

Other news organizations adopted its methods — source labels, confidence scores, correction transparency. It became the benchmark. "Breakdown-style" became a descriptor for evidence-based journalism.

**It trained a generation.**

Its alumni went on to build similar institutions in other countries, other languages, other formats. The methodology spread. The readers learned to demand more. The industry got better.

## 8.3 The Values That Should Survive

If The Breakdown itself disappears, these values should survive:

**The primacy of evidence.** All authority derives from the quality of evidence, not the prestige of the source.

**The obligation of transparency.** Anyone who asks the public to trust them must show their work.

**The humility of uncertainty.** Knowledge is always incomplete. Wisdom is knowing what you do not know.

**The courage of correction.** Institutions that cannot admit error cannot be trusted.

**The discipline of method.** Truth is not a product of intuition or authority. It is the outcome of consistent, rigorous method.

**The independence of judgment.** Truth is not determined by popularity, political alignment, or commercial interest.

## 8.4 The Final Instruction

To every editor, writer, and contributor who inherits this institution:

You are not here to serve us — the founders. You are not here to serve our reputation, our legacy, or our preferences.

You are here to serve the truth.
